Before we officially start exploring, let's cover some basic rules to make sure you understand everything that follows. Don't rush—sharpening the axe won't delay the woodcutting.

It can help you systematically understand how to use your phone, grasp the reasoning behind features, and—whether you're an Android fan or an Apple devotee—most of the thinking applies universally. It is not a manual for any specific phone model, nor is it tech news chasing the latest gadgets, and it's certainly not a guide to becoming addicted to your phone. Honestly, if you're expecting "how to enjoy scrolling videos more," you may be disappointed—we care far more about making your phone truly serve you, not the other way around.

2. Our "Secret Code" Guide

To be clearer, the book uses some small markers—these markers are like tacit codes between us:

  • [Button Name]: Refers to a specific button or App name on the screen, such as [Settings], [OK].
  • >: Indicates the order of operations. For example, "Tap [Settings] > [Sound]" means first tap Settings, then tap Sound.
  • Bold: Very important! It may be a key concept or a safety warning—please pay close attention.
  • Italics: Supplementary thoughts or analogies to help you understand better.

3. Android and iOS—We Cover Both

Guess what—some things have different names but do the same thing. For example: Application (Android) and App (iOS), Settings (called Settings on both), File Manager (Android) and Files (iOS). Knowing they're the same thing is enough; don't get tripped up by the terminology.

Additionally, within the Android camp there's Huawei's HarmonyOS, which has its own characteristics—we'll cover it specifically later. In short, no matter what phone you use, this book can help you.

4. Phone Storage: Why Did You Buy 128GB but Only Have Less Than 110GB Available?

Everyone knows the storage units: 1TB = 1024GB, 1GB = 1024MB, 1MB = 1024KB... But here's the catch: hard drive manufacturers usually calculate in base 1000, while the system calculates in base 1024, so the "128G" drive you bought will "shrink" in actual capacity.

But there are three main reasons your phone's storage shrinks:

  1. The system itself takes up space: The operating system is like a house's load-bearing walls—it must exist. Modern systems routinely occupy 10-20GB, which is completely normal.
  2. Manufacturer pre-installed apps: The apps that come with a new phone also take up space; some can be uninstalled, some cannot.
  3. Partitioning and formatting losses: For technical reasons—just like the lining of a backpack also takes up volume.

Tips for Buying a Phone

Nowadays 128GB phones are basically not enough; 256GB is a safer starting point. If you like taking photos or playing games, consider going straight to 512GB or larger. Remember—when it comes to storage, it's better to have surplus than to come up short.

Hands-on: Check Your Phone's "ID Card"

Open [Settings], find About Phone (or a similar option), and note down: phone model, system version, total storage, and available storage. This is the first step to understanding it. Also check battery health so you have a clear picture.

5. Your Phone Is a "Digital Asset Vault"

Before diving into specific knowledge, let's establish a core understanding: your phone has long been more than a communication tool—it is your most important digital asset vault. Contacts, chat history, family photos, work documents, banking apps, payment codes, health data, electronic IDs... Together, this data is often worth more than the phone itself.

Why Does This Matter?

If your phone is lost or broken, you lose not only a few thousand yuan of hardware, but more likely years of irreplaceable data—photos of your child growing up, voice messages from departed loved ones, important contracts that were never backed up. Scientific organization and protection habits can safeguard you across three dimensions:

  • Improve operating efficiency: Standardized file classification makes system lookups faster and keeps the phone from lagging over the long term.
  • Extend service life: Reasonable storage management reduces the read/write burden on the chip, making both battery and storage last longer.
  • Ensure data security: Layered management + regular backups + tightened permissions keep "data disasters" away from you.
Our Core Philosophy: "Prevention First, Combined with Treatment"

Just as you can't wait until you're sick to see a doctor for wellness, phone management must start with daily habits. What this book repeatedly emphasizes is not some advanced trick, but these simple principles:

  • Prevention first: Install apps only from official stores, strip metadata from photos before sharing, never reuse passwords—these "small actions" cost very little but block the vast majority of risks.
  • Regular check-ups: Organize the download folder once a month, audit permissions once a quarter, do a full backup every six months—a fixed rhythm is a hundred times more effective than ad-hoc firefighting.
  • Always have a fallback: Enable cloud sync, keep account passwords recorded, write a "digital estate checklist"—so that when accidents happen, you and your family can respond calmly.

Chapter 1: Hello, My Personal Computer

Your phone is actually the most personal supercomputer in your pocket. It is countless times more powerful than the computers that once sent humans to the Moon, yet it rests quietly in your palm. Today, let's get to know this "silent giant."

1. The "Innards" of Phone Hardware: A Deep Dive

Small as it is, the phone has all the vital organs. Let's get to know these precision components one by one, like in an anatomy class.

1. SoC (System on Chip): The Phone's "Brain + Heart"

SoC stands for System on Chip, which translates to "cramming the entire system onto a single chip." It's not just the CPU (Central Processing Unit)—it integrates the CPU, GPU (Graphics Processing Unit), NPU (Neural Processing Unit), ISP (Image Signal Processor), baseband (responsible for communication), and more all together.

It's like building a city's government building, power plant, communication center, and police station all in one tower—extremely efficient.

CPU: The Core of General-Purpose Computing

The CPU is the most central component of the SoC, responsible for handling the vast majority of computing tasks. Phone CPUs use the ARM architecture, different from the x86 architecture of computers. ARM features a Reduced Instruction Set (RISC), with lower power consumption, making it more suitable for mobile devices.

CPU Big-LITTLE Core Design: Modern phone CPUs generally adopt a "big.LITTLE" core architecture (ARM's big.LITTLE technology), dividing cores into different performance tiers:

  • Super core / Big core: Strongest performance, handles heavy-duty tasks. Such as game loading, app launching, complex computations. Highest frequency (3GHz+), but also highest power consumption.
  • Medium core: Balances performance and power, handles everyday medium-load tasks. Scrolling Weibo, watching videos, and multitasking mainly rely on it.
  • Small core / Efficiency core: Lowest power consumption, handles lightweight background tasks. Standby, listening to music, receiving messages—almost no power draw.

The three tiers of cores work together: the system scheduler automatically assigns tasks based on load—lightweight tasks go to small cores to save power, heavy work goes to big cores to crank through it, then switches back to small cores when done. This is why a phone can both run games smoothly and stay on standby all day.

Brand Architecture Characteristics:

  • Qualcomm Snapdragon 8 Elite: Uses the self-developed Oryon CPU architecture (derived from the Nuvia acquisition), 2 super cores (4.32GHz) + 6 performance cores (3.53GHz), all based on TSMC's second-generation 3nm process. Completely departs from ARM's public architecture, with leapfrog improvements in both single-core and multi-core performance. GPU is Adreno 830 with a slice architecture, graphics performance up 40%; Hexagon NPU has about 45 TOPS of computing power, AI inference performance up 45%. (Note: Samsung Galaxy custom version has higher frequencies, with super cores up to 4.47GHz.)
  • MediaTek Dimensity 9400: 1 super core (Cortex-X925, 3.62GHz) + 3 performance cores (Cortex-X4, 3.3GHz) + 4 efficiency cores (Cortex-A720, 2.4GHz), continuing the all-big-core architecture, TSMC's second-generation 3nm process. GPU is Immortalis-G925 (12-core), peak performance up 41%, ray tracing performance up 40%. AI processor is the 8th-generation NPU 890, supporting on-device LLM operation, on-device LoRA training, and on-device video generation.
  • Apple A18 Pro: 2 performance cores (~4.04GHz) + 4 efficiency cores (~2.4GHz), TSMC's second-generation 3nm process. 6-core GPU supports hardware ray tracing; 16-core NPU (~35 TOPS) provides powerful computing for on-device AI and Apple Intelligence. Apple's self-developed architecture still leads in single-core performance and energy efficiency, and the unified memory architecture provides high-bandwidth advantages for AI inference.
  • Huawei Kirin 9020: Equipped on the Mate 70 series, uses the self-developed Taishan CPU architecture, with continuously enhanced NPU and ISP capabilities, supports 5.5G and satellite communication, representing the latest breakthrough in domestic chips.
GPU: The Graphics Rendering Engine

The GPU (Graphics Processing Unit) is specifically responsible for graphics and parallel computing tasks. It has far more cores than the CPU (often hundreds or thousands), but each core's functionality is relatively simple, suitable for simultaneously processing large amounts of similar computations.

Graphics Rendering Pipeline:

  1. Vertex processing: Calculates the position coordinates of each vertex in a 3D model.
  2. Rasterization: Converts 3D graphics into pixels on a 2D screen.
  3. Pixel shading: Calculates color, texture, and lighting effects for each pixel.
  4. Output compositing: Composites all layers into the final image and sends it to the screen for display.

GPU Impact on Daily Use: Not just for games! Interface sliding, animation effects, video playback, photo filters, and AR apps all rely on the GPU. A phone with a weak GPU may drop frames when sliding the interface, and games will stutter directly.

GPUs by Brand: Qualcomm uses its self-developed Adreno GPU; MediaTek uses ARM Mali GPU; Apple uses its self-developed GPU, with performance and energy efficiency both being industry benchmarks.

NPU: The AI Acceleration Engine

The NPU (Neural Processing Unit) is a processor specifically designed for neural network operations. Compared to the CPU and GPU, the NPU is more efficient and consumes less power when handling AI tasks.

Neural Network Computing Methods: The core operations of an NPU are matrix multiplication and convolution—precisely the most fundamental and frequent computations in neural networks. Through hardware-level optimization, the NPU can process large amounts of matrix operations in parallel, dozens of times faster than a CPU, with much lower power consumption.

AI Processing Pipeline:

  1. Data input: Camera images, microphone audio, sensor data, etc.
  2. Preprocessing: Adjusts data format and size to fit neural network input requirements.
  3. Inference computation: The NPU loads the trained model, performs forward computation on the input data, and outputs the result.
  4. Post-processing: Converts AI output into usable results, such as face coordinates, text content, scene labels.

Practical Application Scenarios:

  • Computational Photography: Night scene synthesis, portrait bokeh, HDR processing, AI beauty.
  • Voice assistants: Speech recognition, natural language understanding, speech synthesis.
  • Real-time translation: AI subtitles, conversation translation.
  • Smart recognition: Text recognition (OCR), object recognition, scene recognition.
  • On-device LLMs: The latest flagship chips already support running small AI LLMs (7B parameter class) on the phone, enabling offline AI conversations, document summarization, smart writing, and more. The Snapdragon 8 Elite's Hexagon NPU has about 45 TOPS of computing power, the Dimensity 9400's NPU 890 also supports on-device LLMs (LLM inference speed up 80%), and the Apple A18 Pro's 16-core NPU provides about 35 TOPS of computing power.
  • AI image processing: AI erasure (one-tap removal of passersby from photos), AI image expansion (extending image boundaries), AI style transfer, text-to-image generation, and more.
ISP: Image Signal Processor

The ISP processes the raw data from the camera, turning light signals into beautiful photos.

How to judge performance? Look at the model! Apple A18 Pro, Qualcomm Snapdragon 8 Elite, MediaTek Dimensity 9400, and Huawei Kirin 9020 are all flagship-level. Mid-range options include Snapdragon 8s Gen3/7+ Gen3, Dimensity 8400. Don't just look at core count—architecture and process (nanometer number) are more important. The 3nm process is currently the most advanced, with energy efficiency far superior to 5nm and 4nm.

2. RAM: The Temporary Workbench

RAM is like your desk—the bigger it is, the more files you can spread out at once. 12GB is the current mainstream, 16GB suits heavy users, and 24GB+ is for gamers and geeks.

RAM type also matters: LPDDR5 is faster and more power-efficient than LPDDR4X. Although Apple's RAM isn't large, system optimization is good, so it appears "sufficient."

What happens when RAM is insufficient? Apps get killed by the system in the background, requiring reload when switching. This is why sometimes you switch to WeChat and come back to find your game is gone.

3. Storage (ROM/Flash): The Long-Term Warehouse

Storage is like your bookcase—the place that holds all your photos, videos, and apps. The mainstream now is UFS 3.1 or UFS 4.0, several times faster than the older eMMC.

Why does storage speed matter? It determines app opening speed, game loading speed, and photo saving speed. After long use, slow storage makes the phone increasingly laggy.

4. Screen: Your Eyes

The screen is the main window for interaction between you and your phone; its quality directly affects the experience.

  • Material: OLED has vivid colors, high contrast, and is more power-efficient (pixels don't emit light when displaying black), but may have flicker at low brightness that strains the eyes; LCD is more eye-friendly and lower cost, but contrast and color aren't as good as OLED.
  • Resolution: 1080P is mainstream, 2K is sharper but more power-hungry. Apple's "Retina display" concept means: at a certain distance, the human eye cannot distinguish individual pixels.
  • Refresh rate: 60Hz is standard, 90Hz/120Hz is smoother, silky-smooth when scrolling. High refresh rates consume more power, so many phones support adaptive refresh rate (LTPO), dynamically adjusting based on content.
  • Touch sampling rate: Affects touch response speed; gamers should pay attention—the higher, the more responsive.
  • Dimming method: DC dimming is more eye-friendly than PWM dimming, less likely to tire the eyes at low brightness. Many phones now support high-frequency PWM dimming, also for eye protection.
Screen HDR Technology

HDR (High Dynamic Range) lets the screen simultaneously display brighter highlights and darker shadows, greatly improving image layering.

  • Implementation principle: Ordinary screens have a limited brightness range (SDR), with insufficient gap between the brightest and darkest. HDR screens have higher peak brightness (usually above 1000 nits), greater contrast, and wider color gamut, able to display more detail.
  • Mainstream standards: HDR10 (most basic, free and open source), HDR10+ (promoted by Samsung, dynamic metadata), Dolby Vision (best image quality but requires licensing). Which standard a phone supports depends on screen hardware and system licensing.
  • Practical effect: When watching HDR video, sunlight is more glaring, shadows deeper, colors richer. The prerequisite is that the video itself is HDR-produced and the screen supports HDR display.
Photography HDR Technology

HDR in photography and screen HDR are two different things. Photography HDR uses algorithms to solve the problem of "overexposed highlights, dead-black shadows."

  • Algorithm principle: When you press the shutter, the phone actually takes multiple photos at different exposures (underexposed to preserve highlight detail, overexposed to preserve shadow detail, normal exposure to preserve midtones), then synthesizes them into a single high dynamic range photo.
  • Processing pipeline: Multi-frame capture → alignment compensation (anti-shake) → exposure fusion / tone mapping → color correction → output final photo.
  • Effect optimization: Modern phones can already achieve "real-time HDR"—what you see in the viewfinder is the HDR-processed result, WYSIWYG. Apple's Smart HDR and Android's auto HDR are both very mature.
5. Battery and Charging: The Source of Energy

The battery is the phone's most "short-lived" component, visibly aging after two or three years. Mainstream capacity now is 5500-7500mAh.

  • Charging power: The Android camp has already raced to 100W or even 200W, fully charging in a dozen minutes; Apple is still hovering at 20-30W. Does fast charging damage the battery? Using original accessories is generally fine—the phone has temperature protection.
  • Wireless charging: Convenient but inefficient and generates heat. Suitable for casual charging at the office, not suitable when you're rushing out.
  • Reverse charging: The phone becomes a power bank, charging earphones and watches—useful for emergencies.
6. Camera: The Magic of Light and Shadow

Modern phones often have three or four cameras, each with its own role:

  • Main camera: The most commonly used, with the largest sensor and best image quality.
  • Ultra-wide angle: For landscapes and architecture, with a wider field of view.
  • Telephoto: For distant subjects, with both optical zoom and digital zoom.
  • Macro: For shooting very small things at close range.

Pixel count isn't everything! Sensor size (bigger sensor beats smaller), aperture size (f/1.8 is larger than f/2.4), and stabilization (OIS optical stabilization is important) all affect image quality. Computational Photography (AI algorithms) is becoming increasingly important, able to combine multiple photos into one good photo.

Periscope Telephoto Lens

Traditional telephoto lenses require a long optical path, which would make the phone very thick. The periscope telephoto uses a 90-degree prism to refract light, allowing the optical path to unfold horizontally inside the phone, thereby achieving 5x or even 10x optical zoom while keeping the phone slim.

  • Structural principle: Light enters through the lens → prism refracts 90 degrees → passes through the lens group along the phone's width → reaches the sensor. It's like laying a vertical telescope on its side.
  • Optical characteristics: High optical zoom multiplier (5x-10x), image quality far exceeding digital zoom. But the sensor is usually smaller than the main camera, with average low-light performance.
  • Imaging advantages: Shooting the moon, distant buildings, and stage close-ups—beyond the reach of ordinary phones.
OIS Optical Stabilization vs EIS Electronic Stabilization

Stabilization is a key technology affecting photo and video quality; the two methods have completely different principles:

  • OIS (Optical Image Stabilization): Counteracts handshake by physically moving the lens or sensor. There's a micro-gyroscope inside the lens to detect the direction and magnitude of shake, then drives a motor to move the lens group in the opposite direction. The advantage is no image quality loss; the disadvantage is high cost and space usage.
  • EIS (Electronic Image Stabilization): Counteracts shake through software algorithms that crop and offset the image. The phone records a slightly larger frame, then crops a stable area based on gyroscope data. The advantage is no extra hardware needed; the disadvantage is that it crops the image (smaller field of view) and may blur in low light.
  • Practical choice: For photos, prioritize OIS; for video, combining both works best. Many flagship phones now support "Sensor-Shift" sensor-shift stabilization, which works better than traditional lens stabilization.
Optical Zoom vs Digital Zoom
  • Optical zoom: Changes focal length by physically moving the lens group, with no image quality loss. Optical zoom on phones usually has only fixed multiples (such as 2x, 3x, 5x, 10x), and cannot zoom continuously like a camera.
  • Digital zoom: Enlarges the center area of the image, essentially cropping + interpolation upscaling. The more you zoom, the worse the image quality. 100x digital zoom looks cool, but the actual image quality is already very blurry.
  • Hybrid zoom: Combines optical and digital, using multi-frame synthesis algorithms to make up for image quality loss between optical zoom multiples. Better than pure digital, but still not as good as optical.
Audio: Speakers and Display Chip

Single speaker vs Dual speakers:

  • Single speaker: Only one sound unit at the bottom, sound biased to one side, no sense of stereo. Common in mid-to-low-end phones.
  • Dual speakers (Stereo): One speaker each at the top and bottom, forming a stereo sound field. When watching videos or listening to music, left and right channels are distinct, with much stronger immersion. Using the earpiece as the top speaker is a common solution.
  • Sound quality difference: Dual speakers aren't just "louder"—the key is spatial sense and layering. Watching blockbusters with a single speaker always feels like something's missing.

Gaming phone dedicated display chip:

Some gaming phones and performance phones are equipped with an independent display processing chip, whose functions are:

  • Frame insertion: Interpolates 30fps game footage to 60fps or 120fps through algorithms, making the picture smoother. This process is done on the dedicated display chip, not occupying the SoC's GPU resources.
  • Super-resolution: Uses AI algorithms to upscale low-resolution footage to high resolution, making the picture clearer.
  • Power reduction: Because frame insertion and super-resolution are handled by the dedicated display chip, the SoC's load is reduced, lowering heat and power consumption, making games more stable.
GPS Positioning: Single-Frequency and Dual-Frequency

Phone positioning mainly relies on satellite navigation systems such as GPS.

  • Single-frequency GPS: Only receives L1 band signals, with positioning accuracy of about 3-5 meters. Sufficient in open areas, but in dense urban canyons with tall buildings, signal reflection causes significant positioning deviation.
  • Dual-frequency GPS: Simultaneously receives both L1 and L5 band signals. The L5 band has a longer wavelength and stronger anti-interference capability; dual-frequency joint computation can eliminate most ionospheric errors, achieving positioning accuracy of 1-3 meters. The advantage is obvious for city navigation and ride-hailing positioning.
  • Multi-constellation support: Modern phones support not only GPS but also BeiDou (China), GLONASS (Russia), Galileo (Europe), and other satellite systems. The more satellites connected simultaneously, the faster and more accurate the positioning.
7. Sensors: The Phone's Little Tentacles

There are over a dozen sensors hidden inside the phone, working silently:

  • Accelerometer + Gyroscope: Senses phone orientation, for game steering and auto-rotate.
  • Ambient light sensor: Automatically adjusts screen brightness.
  • Proximity sensor: Turns off the screen when the phone is close to your ear during a call.
  • Fingerprint sensor: Mainstream now is under-display optical or ultrasonic fingerprint.
  • Face recognition: Apple's Face ID uses 3D structured light, with high security; many Android phones use 2D photo recognition, with average security.
  • NFC: Near Field Communication, for transit cards, access cards, bank cards.
  • Barometer: Measures altitude, can even detect when you climb stairs.
  • Hall sensor: Detects the opening and closing of magnetic cases.

2. Operating Systems: Android vs iOS vs HarmonyOS

Hardware is the body; the operating system is the soul. The three major systems each have their own characteristics:

  • Android: Like an open-source community, phone manufacturers can deeply customize it, offering many choices. You can change themes, install third-party app stores, and tinker with various tricks. But because of its openness, fragmentation is severe, with significant experience differences between brands.
  • iOS: Like a carefully tended garden, Apple controls both hardware and software, with a unified and smooth experience. App quality is high, privacy protection is good, but freedom is low—many things must follow Apple's rules.
  • HarmonyOS: Huawei's new species, focusing on distributed capabilities, allowing different devices to collaborate seamlessly. We'll cover it specifically later.

All three have extremely rich ecosystems—just choose the lifestyle you prefer. There's no absolute good or bad, only what suits you.

3. Phone Ports Explained

Phone ports are bridges connecting to the outside world, and different ports vary greatly:

Micro USB
  • Physical characteristics: Flat trapezoidal connector, with distinct front and back (you can't insert it reversed). It has been largely phased out on phones, but some low-end devices and old equipment still use it.
  • Transmission limitations: Only supports USB 2.0 standard, with a maximum transfer rate of 480Mbps; charging power usually doesn't exceed 10W.
  • Disadvantages: Front and back are not interchangeable, short insertion lifespan, slow transfer speed, low charging power.
Lightning (Apple Lightning connector, discontinued)
  • Physical characteristics: Apple's self-developed 8-pin connector, reversible. Used on iPhone 14 and earlier models, now fully discontinued.
  • Transmission limitations: The Lightning connector physically only supports USB 2.0 standard, with a maximum transfer rate of 480Mbps. Even using a USB-C to Lightning cable, transfer speed is limited by the Lightning connector itself.
  • Reason for discontinuation: The EU passed regulations requiring all phones to use USB-C connectors starting in 2024; Apple switched to Type-C from the iPhone 15 series. The Lightning connector has officially exited the stage.
  • Note: Apple's MFi-certified cables contain a certification chip; non-certified cables may not charge or transfer data properly. If you're still using an older iPhone, it's recommended to keep Lightning cables on hand.
USB Type-C
  • Physical characteristics: 24-pin oval connector, reversible. It has become the standard connector for phones and most electronic devices. The EU has legislated that all phones, tablets, earphones, and other portable devices must use USB-C connectors starting in 2024, and other regions worldwide are following suit.
  • Transmission protocols: Type-C is just the connector form; actual transmission capability depends on the internal protocol. Supports USB 2.0 (480Mbps), USB 3.0/3.1/3.2 (5-20Gbps), and even USB4 (40Gbps).
  • Important distinction: Not all Type-C connectors support USB 3.0! Many phones use Type-C connectors but only connect USB 2.0 pins internally, with transfer speeds as slow as the old connector. iPhone 15/16 standard models only support USB 2.0 speeds; the Pro models support USB 3.0 (requires a USB-C to USB-C cable).
  • Charging capability: Supports USB PD and other fast charging protocols, up to 240W charging.
  • Video output: Supports DisplayPort Alt Mode, can output video to a monitor via an adapter cable.
3.5mm Audio Connector
  • Physical characteristics: Round 3-segment or 4-segment jack; 3-segment transmits left and right channel audio, 4-segment adds a microphone channel.
  • Characteristics: Analog signal transmission, plug-and-play, no drivers or conversion needed. Stable sound quality, zero latency.
  • Current status: Most flagship phones have removed the 3.5mm connector, requiring a Type-C adapter or Bluetooth earphones. Some mid-range phones and gaming phones still retain it.

5. Baseband and Antenna: The Unsung Heroes of Phone Communication

Your phone can make calls and access the internet thanks to the partnership of the baseband chip and antenna.

Baseband Chip:

The baseband is the processor in the phone responsible for wireless communication. It modulates digital signals into radio waves for transmission, and demodulates received radio waves back into digital signals. Baseband performance directly affects call quality, internet speed, and signal stability. Qualcomm is the largest mobile baseband supplier; MediaTek and Samsung also have self-developed basebands. Starting from iPhone 16e, Apple has equipped its self-developed C1 baseband chip, marking Apple's official entry into the self-developed baseband era; the C1 baseband uses a 4nm process with excellent power consumption. iPhone 16/16 Plus/16 Pro/16 Pro Max still use Qualcomm basebands (Snapdragon X75 5G), with subsequent models expected to gradually transition to self-developed basebands. Earlier iPhone XS/11 series using Intel basebands had poor signal performance, which was a pain point for many users.

Antenna Design:

Phone antennas are hidden inside the body, usually arranged around the frame. Antenna design directly affects signal reception performance:

  • MIMO technology: Multiple-input multiple-output, using multiple antennas to simultaneously send and receive data, improving speed and stability. 4×4 MIMO is faster than 2×2 MIMO.
  • Number of antennas: Flagship phones usually have a dozen antennas (cellular, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, NFC, GPS each need independent antennas).
  • Impact of metal body: Metal shields signals, so metal-body phones need antenna bands designed in (plastic strips on the frame). Glass and plastic bodies have less impact on signals.
Network Technology Evolution:
  • 4G LTE: Theoretical peak rate 1Gbps downlink / 500Mbps uplink (Cat.18), actual experience usually 50-150Mbps. Widest coverage, sufficient for use.
  • 5G NR: Theoretical peak rate 10Gbps+ downlink. Sub-6GHz band has better coverage; mmWave millimeter wave is extremely fast but has very limited coverage.
  • 5G-A (5G Advanced): Enhanced version of 5G, also called 5.5G. Downlink rate up to 10Gbps, uplink 1Gbps, with significantly improved connection density and positioning accuracy. Starting in 2024, China Mobile, China Telecom, and China Unicom have begun deploying 5G-A networks in key cities; flagship phones such as Huawei Mate 70 series and Xiaomi 15 series already support 5G-A. 5G-A also brings "integrated sensing and communication" capabilities, where base stations can simultaneously communicate and sense the environment, providing infrastructure for low-altitude economy (drone delivery) and vehicle-to-everything.
Network Band Classification:

The network bands supported by a phone determine which countries and carriers' networks it can be used on:

  • FDD-LTE Band (B series): Such as B1, B3, B5, etc., 4G bands. Different countries use different bands; China mainly uses B1/B3/B5/B8/B34/B38/B39/B40/B41.
  • TDD-LTE Band: Another 4G standard, heavily used in China with B38/B39/B40/B41.
  • 5G NR Band (N series): Such as n1, n3, n28, n41, n78, n79, etc. n78 (3.5GHz) is China's main 5G band; n41 (2.5GHz) is used more by China Mobile.
  • Purchase advice: If you need to use the phone abroad, confirm before purchase whether it supports the target country's bands. China mainland phones usually have the most complete support for Chinese bands, but may lack some overseas bands.

Appendix: Android Phone Memory Expansion Explained

When buying a phone, you often see promotions like "12GB+8GB RAM"—as if 8GB of RAM has appeared out of thin air. But can it really make the phone faster? Could it actually damage the phone? In this section, we'll thoroughly explain the technology of "memory expansion."

1. Essential Definition

Android phone "memory expansion" (also called memory extension, memory fusion, RAM Plus, etc.) does not actually physically add RAM chips; it's a system-level software technology: by dynamically allocating part of the phone's internal storage space (ROM/UFS flash) to simulate virtual RAM for use.

To use an analogy: physical RAM is the "high-speed workbench," while expanded memory is the "temporary storage room." When the workbench isn't enough, the system moves temporarily unused data to the storage room, and moves it back when needed. The storage room has large capacity, but back-and-forth moving is definitely slower than keeping it directly on the workbench.

2. Technical Principle

Mainstream manufacturers implement it in two main ways:

1. zRAM Compression

A compressed data space is partitioned within RAM, compressing inactive app code for storage, with a compression ratio of about 2:1. The data remains in RAM—fast and doesn't wear the flash—but requires CPU involvement for compression and decompression.

2. Swap Partition

Directly partitions a fixed space in UFS flash as virtual memory, writing background process data to storage. Depends on storage bandwidth; read/write speed is far lower than physical memory.

The "12GB+8GB RAM" promoted by manufacturers is usually a combination of 12GB physical RAM + 8GB flash virtual memory. It's worth noting that zRAM doesn't occupy phone storage by default; only when zRAM is completely filled with data does the system create swap files on storage, and this situation is extremely rare.

3. Speed Gap Is the Key Bottleneck

The fundamental limitation of this technology is the speed difference. Currently, mainstream UFS 4.0 flash has sequential read speeds of about 4200MB/s, only 1/10–1/15 of LPDDR5X physical memory. Therefore, during light-load zRAM compression, the speed is close to real RAM, but once entering high-load scenarios, the speed drops to a few dozenths.

4. Applicable Scenarios and Benefit Boundaries

Memory expansion is not a panacea; effects vary by model and usage scenario:

  • When it helps: Models with 6GB physical RAM + UFS 3.1 or above, with ≥30GB free space, can keep 3-4 more apps alive in the background after enabling 3GB expansion, with a 68% reduction in reload rate—suitable for heavy multitaskers like delivery riders and ride-hailing drivers.
  • Daily scenarios: Significant improvements for WeChat multi-instance, browser tab retention, instant messaging response, with noticeably shorter cold-start times for background apps.
  • Limited scenarios: Limited improvement for high-bandwidth scenarios like large game loading and 4K video editing.
  • When it's harmful: Models with 8GB RAM + UFS 2.2 and insufficient free space, where enabling it actually increases game frame rate fluctuations, with more battery drain when scrolling short videos and lag when switching.
  • When it's useless: Models with 16GB+ of large RAM see almost zero benefit—at most keeping one more app alive, but increasing CPU load by 12% and heat by 2-3°C. For models with more than 12GB RAM, it's recommended to simply turn it off to avoid meaningless compression computation.

5. Naming and Enabling Methods by Manufacturer

Different brands call it differently: Xiaomi calls it "Memory Extension," Huawei calls it "Memory Fusion," Samsung calls it "RAM Plus," vivo names it "Extended RAM," OnePlus calls it "Memory Extension." This feature is a system-native capability and is not directly tied to the underlying Android version.

The general enabling path is: Enter [Settings] → [More Settings] → find the [Memory] or [Performance] sub-item → enable "Memory Expansion" and select the capacity tier (usually 2GB–7GB), taking effect after restart. After enabling, the system will display a memory combination such as "8GB+4GB".

Notes

  • Memory expansion occupies part of the phone's storage space; please ensure free storage is no less than 20-30GB, otherwise it may affect system fluidity.
  • Using flash as virtual memory causes read/write wear; although modern UFS flash has a long lifespan, long-term high-frequency swap may still accelerate storage aging.
  • If the phone itself has small physical memory (such as 4GB or below), enabling memory expansion only provides temporary relief and cannot fundamentally solve lag issues.

Summary

Memory expansion is a mature system-level optimization technology that can improve multitasking capability and background retention for mid-to-low-end models without hardware changes, but it's limited by the huge speed gap between flash and physical memory, and is not a "miracle cure." Whether to enable it and how much to set should be judged based on your model's physical memory size, flash specification, free storage space, and usage habits.

0. Don't Get Lost Online—Telling Wi-Fi and Mobile Data Apart via "Roads" and "Cars"

Many people have both the Wi-Fi and signal icons lit up at the top of their phone screen, but are completely confused about the essential difference between them. Core Metaphor: Roads and Cars

Imagine the internet as a city. "Roads" are the channels between your phone and the internet, and "cars" are the data traveling on the roads. Wi-Fi and mobile data are two different roads, but the data (cars) itself is the same.

1. Essential Difference: Local Channel vs. Remote Highway

Wi-Fi is essentially a local area network technology—it's like the road wirelessly laid downstairs in your building, responsible for transmitting data from the router to your phone. But if your broadband bill is unpaid or the fiber cable is broken, the road is still there, but the "highway" beyond is cut off, so naturally you can't go anywhere. Mobile data, on the other hand, is transmitted through carrier 4G/5G base stations—equivalent to driving directly onto the carrier's high-speed dedicated line. As long as there's signal, you can connect directly to the internet.

2. Cost and Scenarios: Fixed Monthly Fee vs. Pay-per-Use

Wi-Fi is usually a one-time investment (monthly broadband fee)—as long as the power is on, the whole family can use it unlimited, making it ideal for high-traffic scenarios like downloading movies and gaming. Mobile data is billed by usage—exceeding your plan may result in throttling or extra charges, making it more suitable for lightweight tasks in mobile scenarios like subways and business trips.

Try It: Check Your Data Plan

Open your phone's [Settings] > [Mobile Network] > [Data Management] to see how much data you have left this month and which apps use the most. Knowing your numbers means you won't panic when you go out.

3. Security Considerations: Public Wi-Fi Is a Hidden "Phishing Ground"

In terms of security, mobile data is usually safer than public Wi-Fi. Mobile data is encrypted by the carrier, while public Wi-Fi can indeed be set up by hackers as "fake hotspots" to intercept unencrypted traffic. But let's be clear: in today's world of ubiquitous HTTPS, what attackers can intercept is which websites you visit (domains) and unencrypted HTTP content; while HTTPS-encrypted data like WeChat messages and banking app login passwords cannot be directly read—unless a more complex SSL stripping attack is performed and the user ignores certificate warnings. Even so, when making payments on the go, using mobile data directly is still the safest choice.

Security Reminder

In public places like airports, malls, and cafes, do not connect to free Wi-Fi with suspicious names (like "Free_WiFi_2024"). Even if you do connect, never log into banking apps or enter passwords on it.

4. Checking on Your Phone: Decoding the Status Bar's "Hidden Language"

How do you know what your phone is using to connect right now? Taking a Huawei phone as an example, when a two-way arrow icon appears next to the carrier signal or WLAN signal in the status bar, it means you're using that method to access the internet; if both light up at the same time, it means network acceleration is in effect and the phone is using both channels simultaneously.

iPhone users have it more intuitive: if the Wi-Fi icon is lit, you're on Wi-Fi; if not, look at the signal bars in the upper right corner to know you're on mobile data. Some Android phones also display "5G" or "4G" in the status bar, directly telling you the current mobile network type.

I. Mobile Networks (4G/5G) and Data

Mobile networks are the foundation of staying connected anywhere. From 2G to 5G, speeds have increased a thousandfold, but the principles are much the same.

Network standards:
  • 4G LTE: Currently the widest coverage, sufficient speed (theoretically up to 100Mbps+), relatively low power consumption.
  • 5G: Fast (theoretically up to 10Gbps), but coverage is still being rolled out, and power consumption is high. Ideal for downloading large files and watching HD video.
  • 5G SA vs NSA: SA is standalone networking; NSA relies on 4G base stations. SA offers a better experience, but requires both carrier and phone support.
Data management:
  • What data is: The amount of data generated when your phone goes online. Video and images use the most data; text and voice use relatively little.
  • Data-saving tips: Turn on "Data Saver" in settings, and update apps or watch HD video over Wi-Fi.
  • Signal bars ≠ speed: Strong signal only means a good connection—speed also depends on how busy the base station is and how far you are from it.

Important Warning: Turn off [Data Roaming] Before Going Abroad!

Using mobile data abroad can result in astronomical bills! Before going abroad, be sure to turn off data roaming in settings, or purchase a local data card/international roaming plan in advance.

II. Wi-Fi: Connection, Sharing, and Security

Wi-Fi is the main connection for homes and offices—fast and doesn't use mobile data. But Wi-Fi is constantly evolving, and the differences between standards are significant.

Connecting to Wi-Fi:

Go to [Settings] > [WLAN], select a network, and enter the password. Many phones now support QR code connection, which is even more convenient.

Sharing passwords:

Both Android and iOS let connected phones quickly share passwords with friends. Android generates a QR code; iOS can share automatically by bringing devices close together.

Wi-Fi standard evolution:

Wi-Fi standards are set by IEEE, and each generation brings significant improvements:

Standard Protocol Band Max Speed Features
Wi-Fi 4 802.11n 2.4/5GHz 600Mbps Legacy standard, decent coverage
Wi-Fi 5 802.11ac 5GHz 3.5Gbps Currently the most widespread, sufficient speed
Wi-Fi 6 802.11ax 2.4/5GHz 9.6Gbps Strong multi-device performance, low latency
Wi-Fi 6E 802.11ax 2.4/5/6GHz 9.6Gbps Adds 6GHz band, less congestion
Wi-Fi 7 802.11be 2.4/5/6GHz 46Gbps Ultra-fast transmission, multi-link aggregation
  • Wi-Fi 6 key technologies: OFDMA (Orthogonal Frequency-Division Multiple Access) allows multiple devices to transmit simultaneously; MU-MIMO (Multi-User Multiple Input Multiple Output) improves multi-device concurrency efficiency; TWT (Target Wake Time) makes devices more power-efficient.
  • Wi-Fi 6E: Adds the 6GHz band on top of Wi-Fi 6, providing more channels and reducing interference. Requires both phone and router support.
  • Wi-Fi 7: Introduces MLO (Multi-Link Operation), which can use multiple bands simultaneously for data transmission, drastically reducing latency. 320MHz ultra-wide channels enable a speed leap. Phones like Xiaomi 15, Huawei Mate 70, and Samsung S25 can use Wi-Fi 7 networks (requires a Wi-Fi 7 router).
  • 2.4GHz vs 5GHz: 2.4GHz has better wall penetration but slower speed and more interference; 5GHz is faster but weaker at penetrating walls. The 6GHz band of Wi-Fi 6E/7 is the fastest but weakest at wall penetration, suitable for use within the same room.
Public Wi-Fi risks:

Public Wi-Fi may have hackers eavesdropping on data or setting up phishing hotspots. Guidelines:

  • Avoid logging into bank accounts or entering passwords.
  • Best to use a VPN for encrypted connections.
  • Don't auto-connect to unfamiliar Wi-Fi.
  • Use HTTPS websites (look for the lock icon in the address bar).

III. Bluetooth: The All-Rounder of Short-Range Wireless Communication

Bluetooth is probably one of the most frequently used wireless technologies on your phone—wireless earbuds, smart watches, car systems, keyboards and mice all rely on it to connect.

Technical principles:

Bluetooth operates in the 2.4GHz ISM band, using Frequency-Hopping Spread Spectrum (FHSS) to spread data across 79 channels and rapidly switch between them, effectively avoiding interference. Bluetooth communication distances are divided into three classes: Class 1 about 100 meters, Class 2 about 10 meters (common in phones), and Class 3 about 1 meter.

Version evolution:
  • Bluetooth 4.0: Introduced Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE), allowing small-battery devices like wristbands and heart rate monitors to connect wirelessly, extending battery life from days to months.
  • Bluetooth 4.2: Improved transmission speed and privacy protection, supports IPv6 direct connection.
  • Bluetooth 5.0: Doubled transmission speed (2Mbps), 4x the range (theoretically 300 meters), 8x the broadcast capacity. Supports dual audio (connecting two pairs of earbuds simultaneously).
  • Bluetooth 5.1: Introduced direction finding (AoA/AoD), with centimeter-level positioning accuracy.
  • Bluetooth 5.2: New LE Audio standard, supports LC3 codec (better sound quality, lower power consumption), multi-stream audio, and broadcast audio.
  • Bluetooth 5.3/5.4: Further optimized power consumption and connection stability, enhanced periodic broadcasting.

LE Audio adoption: In 2024-2025, LE Audio has entered a rapid adoption phase. Mainstream brands' new true wireless earbuds (such as Huawei FreeBuds Pro 4, Xiaomi Buds 5 Pro, Samsung Galaxy Buds3, etc.) all support LE Audio and LC3 codec, providing lower latency and better sound quality. When buying new earbuds, prioritize those that support LE Audio.

Application scenarios:
  • Audio transmission: Wireless earbuds, car audio. Bluetooth 5.2's LE Audio brings a qualitative leap in both sound quality and latency.
  • Data transmission: Close-range file and photo transfer. While slower than Wi-Fi Direct, it's simpler.
  • Device pairing: Smart watches, wristbands, keyboards, mice, game controllers.
  • Location services: Bluetooth beacons for indoor navigation, item tracking (like Apple AirTag).
  • Smart home: Bluetooth Mesh networks to control light bulbs, door locks, etc.

IV. Infrared Remote Control: Phone Becomes a Universal Remote

Infrared remote control is one of the oldest wireless control technologies, but it's still practical on phones. Some Android phones (such as certain Xiaomi and Huawei models) have built-in infrared emitters that can remote-control TVs, air conditioners, set-top boxes, and other appliances.

Working principle:

Infrared remote control emits infrared light pulse signals at a specific frequency (usually 38kHz) through an infrared LED, and the appliance's infrared receiver decodes these pulses to execute corresponding operations. Different brands use different encoding protocols—common ones include NEC, RC5, RC6, SONY, etc. The infrared app built into the phone contains encoding libraries for various brands—just select the brand and model to match.

Limitations:
  • Directionality: Infrared travels in straight lines and must be aimed at the device—cannot pass through walls.
  • Short range: Effective distance is generally 5-8 meters.
  • No Apple support: iPhone has no infrared emitter and cannot use this feature.

V. NFC: The Magic of a Tap

NFC (Near Field Communication) is a very short-range (within 10 cm) high-frequency wireless communication technology, operating at 13.56MHz with a maximum transmission rate of 424Kbps.

Communication principles and modes:

NFC evolved from RFID technology and supports three working modes:

  • Read/Write mode: The phone reads information from NFC tags, such as triggering actions by reading NFC stickers.
  • Peer-to-Peer mode: Two NFC devices transmit data to each other, such as Android Beam (discontinued).
  • Card Emulation mode: The phone emulates a smart card—this is the most commonly used mode. Transit cards, access cards, and bank cards all use this mode.
Security mechanisms:
  • Ultra-short distance: The communication distance within 10 cm is itself a physical security guarantee—remote eavesdropping is virtually impossible.
  • Secure chip: The phone has a built-in independent Secure Element (SE) or uses HCE (Host Card Emulation). Payment keys are stored in the secure area and cannot be extracted even if the phone is rooted.
  • Fingerprint/Face verification: Payments require biometric confirmation—even if the phone is lost, it cannot be used for fraudulent transactions.
  • Tokenization: Apple Pay, Huawei Pay, etc., do not transmit real card numbers but use one-time tokens—even if intercepted, they cannot be reused.
Typical applications:
  • Transit cards/Access cards: Write physical card information into the phone—just tap your phone.
  • Mobile payments: Apple Pay, Huawei Pay, etc.—bind your bank card to your phone and pay with a tap.
  • Device pairing: Tap to quickly pair Bluetooth earbuds and speakers.
  • Identity verification: NFC ID card reading, electronic driver's license verification.

V. Personal Hotspot: Turn Your Phone into a Wi-Fi Router

When you have plenty of data, you can turn on the hotspot for tablets and computers.

  • How to enable: Settings > Personal Hotspot/Tethering, turn it on.
  • Things to note: Very power- and data-hungry—remember to set a complex password and limit the number of connected devices.
  • USB tethering: Connect to a computer with a cable—more stable and faster than wireless hotspot.

Hands-On: Set Up and Manage a Hotspot

  1. Open [Settings], find "Personal Hotspot" or "Tethering," turn it on, and set the name and password.
  2. Use another device (like a computer) to search for and connect to this Wi-Fi.
  3. On your phone, see if you can manage connected devices or kick off unrecognized ones.

Chapter 4: Installing and Managing Apps—Your Phone's "Skill Packs"

Apps make your phone capable of anything, but they need to be managed. Just like you'd assign a new employee a desk and permissions, installing apps requires a standardized process.

I. Official App Stores: The Safest "App Supermarket"

  • Android: Google Play (international) or brand-built stores (like Huawei AppGallery, Xiaomi App Store).
  • iOS: Only the App Store.
  • HarmonyOS: Huawei AppGallery.

Core security value: reviewed, guaranteed updates, payment security. Action guideline: prioritize downloading only from official stores!

II. Pure/Protection Mode: Turn On Your Phone's "Antivirus Wall"

Mainstream domestic brands (Huawei, Xiaomi, OPPO, vivo, Honor) all have a built-in Pure Mode or Protection Mode—a system-level security switch. Strongly recommended for all users to enable.

1. What exactly is it?

The core function of Pure Mode (also called "Protection Mode") is to block third-party app installations: when enabled, the system will only allow app installations from the official app store. Installation packages downloaded from browsers, QR codes, chat apps, and other channels will be directly intercepted, eliminating malware at the source.

2. Who it's for
  • Elderly users: Can't tell "official" from "fake" ads—Pure Mode is the most reliable safeguard.
  • Users insensitive to app permissions: Don't need to install niche apps, beta versions, or foreign-region games.
  • All users who want to improve device security: Even for power users, having it on is better than off—if you have a temporary need, just turn it off briefly.
3. How to enable (taking Huawei/Xiaomi as examples)
  • Huawei/HarmonyOS: [Settings] > [System & Updates] > [Pure Mode], select "Enable."
  • Xiaomi: [Settings] > [Privacy Protection] > [Protect Privacy], or [Settings] > [App Settings] > [App Installation], turn off "Install unknown apps."
  • OPPO/vivo: [Settings] > [Security] > [Install Apps from External Sources], turn off each authorization one by one.
4. App Store Security Detection Mechanisms

App stores aren't just for show—there's a complete security pipeline behind them:

  • Virus scanning: Each app is jointly scanned by multiple antivirus engines before listing, to identify known trojan and virus signatures.
  • Permission review: The store reviews whether the permissions an app requests are "reasonable"—a flashlight app asking for contacts? Directly rejected or delisted.
  • Developer certification: Listing requires enterprise qualification, personal ID, or credit card verification. If problems arise, someone can be held accountable—not a "black app."
  • Continuous monitoring: Listed apps are also continuously rescanned, and newly discovered malicious behavior results in forced delisting.

III. Third-Party Sources and "Electronic Parasites": Extremely High Risk!

Downloading apps from webpages or unknown links may bundle viruses, steal privacy, or commit payment fraud. Here we specifically mention a new concept: "electronic parasites."

What are "electronic parasites"?

This refers to trojan-implanted "cracked" software—you think you "saved money," but actually "got infected." Common forms:

  • "Cracked," "free," "ad-free" versions of paid software;
  • "Like-farming," "red packet grabbing," "game cheats" and other gray-area tools;
  • So-called "internal versions" of apps recommended by "small ads" in group chats.

These programs appear usable, but behind the scenes they may be stealing contacts, reading SMS, eavesdropping on the microphone, mining in the background. Pure Mode is the first line of defense against them—simply not letting you install them.

Second line of defense recommendations:
  • Regular full virus scans: Use the phone's built-in security center (like Huawei Phone Manager, Xiaomi Security Center) to scan once a month.
  • Cautiously grant sensitive permissions: Contacts, SMS, location, camera, microphone—these five "high-risk permissions" must be strictly reviewed.
  • Watch for battery and data anomalies: Phone suddenly heating up, battery draining fast, data running at night—most likely a "parasite" hiding in the background.

If you absolutely must install a third-party app:

  1. Ensure the source is absolutely trustworthy.
  2. Carefully review permission requests before installing.
  3. Scan with a security app afterward.

Ultimate advice: In system settings, turn off the "Install unknown apps" permission.

IV. Install, Update, Uninstall

  • Install: Tap "Get" or "Install" in the store. After installation, the first launch will ask for permissions.
  • Update: Must update! Mainly to fix security vulnerabilities. Recommend enabling auto-update.
  • Uninstall: Long-press the icon and drag to the uninstall area. For a more thorough clean, go to [Settings] > [App Management] to find and uninstall.

V. Permission Management: You Are the App's "Boss"

Apps requesting permissions (like contacts, location) is sometimes reasonable (maps need location), sometimes not (flashlight needs contacts).

  • Permission options: "Always allow," "Only while using the app," "Deny," "Ask every time."
  • Management principles:
    1. Minimization: Don't grant unless necessary.
    2. Post-audit: Regularly check permission settings to see who's "overstepping."
    3. Contextual: Like ride-hailing apps—only grant location permission while in use.

Key Permissions to Watch

  • Contacts/SMS: May be used to send spam messages.
  • Real-time location: Your movements may be tracked.
  • Camera/Microphone: May be used for secret photography/recording.
  • File access: May read your private files.

VI. Components of an App Installation Package

Have you ever wondered what's inside that installation package you download from the app store? Understanding the components of an installation package helps you better understand how apps work, and when problems arise, you'll know where to look.

1. Android APK File Structure

An APK (Android Package Kit) is essentially a ZIP archive. After extraction, you can see the following core directories and files:

  • AndroidManifest.xml: The app's "ID card" and "instruction manual." Records core information like app package name, version number, required permissions, supported system versions, entry Activity, etc. The system reads this file first when installing the app.
  • classes.dex: The app's core code. Java/Kotlin source code is compiled into Dalvik bytecode and stored in this file. An app may have multiple dex files (like classes2.dex, classes3.dex).
  • resources.arsc: The compiled resource index table. Records the IDs and corresponding relationships of all resources.
  • res/ directory: Resource folder. Contains images, layout files, strings, color definitions, etc. Images for different resolutions are placed in different drawable folders (like drawable-hdpi, drawable-xhdpi).
  • assets/ directory: Raw resource folder. Stores resources that don't need compilation, such as web files, fonts, audio, etc. Apps can read them via AssetManager at runtime.
  • lib/ directory: Native library folder. Stores compiled .so files (C/C++ compilation output), categorized by CPU architecture (like arm64-v8a, armeabi-v7a). Games and apps requiring high-performance computation usually have this directory.
  • META-INF/ directory: Signature information folder. Contains MANIFEST.MF, CERT.SF, CERT.RSA files, used to verify the APK's integrity and signature.
2. iOS IPA File Structure

IPA (iOS App Store Package) is also a compressed archive, with a slightly different structure:

  • Payload/ directory: Where the core content lives. Inside is a .app folder containing all the app's content.
  • Info.plist: The app's configuration file, similar to Android's AndroidManifest.xml. Records app ID, version, permission requirements, supported devices, etc.
  • Executable file: The main program with the same name as the .app folder, a compiled Mach-O format binary.
  • Frameworks/ directory: Dynamic libraries and frameworks the app depends on.
  • Resource files: Images, audio, video, and other resources, usually stored inside the .app folder.
  • _CodeSignature/ directory: Code signature information, used to verify the app's integrity and origin.
3. Signature Verification Mechanism

Why are apps downloaded from official stores safer? Signature verification is key.

  • Android signing: Developers sign APKs with private keys, and the system verifies with public keys during installation. If an APK is tampered with (e.g., implanted with a virus), the signature becomes invalid and the system refuses to install. Google Play also requires App Signing, where Google holds the signing key, further protecting app security.
  • iOS signing: Apple's review is stricter. Every IPA must be signed with a certificate issued by Apple, otherwise it cannot be installed. This is why iOS can only install apps from the App Store or enterprise certificates. Apps signed with enterprise certificates have limited validity and Apple can revoke them at any time.
  • Role of signatures:
    1. Verify origin: Confirm the app indeed comes from the claimed developer.
    2. Verify integrity: Ensure the app wasn't tampered with during transmission.
    3. Prevent impersonation: Malicious apps cannot disguise themselves as other apps.
4. Installation Package Size and Optimization

You may notice that the app size shown in the app store differs from the space it occupies after installation. This is because:

  • Download size: The compressed APK/IPA size.
  • Installation size: The extracted size, usually much larger than the download size.
  • Runtime footprint: Apps also generate cache and data files at runtime, occupying more space.

Modern app optimization methods:

  • App Bundle (Android): Google Play's dynamic delivery technology. Users only download resources suited to their device when downloading (e.g., only images for the corresponding screen resolution), greatly reducing download size.
  • App Thinning (iOS): Similar technology—Apple automatically trims unneeded resources based on the device.
  • On-demand download: Large apps like games can download the core package first, then download resource packs later.

Trivia: Why are some app installation packages so large?

Game apps are usually the largest because they contain lots of high-definition textures, 3D models, and audio files. Social apps are also quite large because they need to support various functional modules. Tool apps are usually the smallest. If you find a tool app that's unusually large, be alert to whether it bundles unnecessary features.

Hands-On: Audit 3 Apps' Permissions

Pick 3 commonly used apps (e.g., social, shopping, tool), go to the app permission management page in [Settings], review each item, and turn off or change unnecessary sensitive permissions to "Only while using the app."

V. Accessibility: The Power of Tech for Good

Don't be intimidated by the name "accessibility"—these features are for everyone. They were originally designed for people with disabilities, but they're also useful for ordinary people—temporarily injured hands, holding a baby, broken earbuds on the subway, or needing reading glasses for small text... they can all save the day. Ultimately, accessible design embodies the philosophy of tech for good: letting everyone equally enjoy digital life.

1. "Eyes" for Visually Impaired Users—Complete Screen Reader Guide

Screen readers are the core tool for visually impaired users to use phones. It reads all visible content on the screen aloud, and users operate the phone through gestures and voice. Below we introduce the usage of iOS "VoiceOver" and Android "TalkBack" respectively.

iOS VoiceOver

How to enable: [Settings] > [Accessibility] > [VoiceOver], turn on the switch. You can also triple-click the side button (or Home button) to quickly toggle.

Basic gestures:

  1. One-finger swipe: Browse screen content element by element;
  2. Two-finger swipe: Scroll up/down/left/right by full page;
  3. One-finger double-tap: Activate the current focus element (equivalent to "tap");
  4. Three-finger swipe: Switch pages;
  5. One-finger triple-tap: Zoom or select (different from regular triple-tap).

Personalization: [VoiceOver] > [Speech], you can adjust speaking rate (default about 50%, can be set to 70-80% for smoother listening), pitch, and switch between different voice packs. iOS has built-in "Enhanced Speech" and "Speech Controller"—the former reduces breathing sounds and pauses for a more natural listening experience.

Android TalkBack

How to enable: [Settings] > [Accessibility] > [TalkBack], turn on the switch. Or long-press both volume keys for 3 seconds to quickly enable (supported by some brands).

Basic gestures:

  1. One-finger left/right swipe: Switch focus element;
  2. One-finger double-tap: Activate element;
  3. Two-finger up/down/left swipe: Scroll;
  4. Three-finger single-tap: Enter reading mode (continuous reading);
  5. Four-finger single-tap: Pause/resume reading.

Personalization: [TalkBack] > [Text-to-Speech Settings], you can adjust speech rate, pitch, and switch speech engines (recommend installing Google Text-to-Speech engine for more natural reading).

2. Make Small Text Big, Make Blurry Clear—Visual Adjustments
Zoom Gestures
  • iOS: [Settings] > [Accessibility] > [Zoom], after enabling, three-finger tap the screen to zoom. Default is full-screen zoom; window zoom (partial zoom window) is optional. Pinch with two fingers to adjust magnification (1.5x to 15x), three-finger double-tap to lock the current zoom ratio.
  • Android: [Settings] > [Accessibility] > [Magnification], three-finger tap to zoom, three-finger tap again to exit; or directly three-finger triple-tap the screen to enable temporary magnification (up to 8x).
High Contrast Text / Bold Text
  • iOS: [Settings] > [Accessibility] > [Display & Text Size], turn on "Bold Text," "Increase Contrast," "Reduce Transparency," etc. "Smart Invert" can keep text black on light backgrounds, avoiding white-on-white.
  • Android: [Settings] > [Display] > [Font Size], adjustable in four levels: "Small, Normal, Large, Huge." Huawei/Xiaomi additionally provides "Bold" and "High Contrast Text" toggles.
Color Filters (Color Blind-Friendly)
  • iOS: [Settings] > [Accessibility] > [Display & Text Size] > [Color Filters], provides four color blind modes: red/green/blue/yellow, plus "Grayscale" and "Invert Colors" options. Recommend taking a color vision test first (built into the system) before selecting the corresponding filter.
  • Android: [Settings] > [Accessibility] > [Color Correction], includes three color blind modes + "Invert Colors" toggle.
3. AI Image Description: Let AI Be Your "Eyes"

When a screen reader encounters an image, the traditional approach is to skip it or just read "image." But now AI can describe image content in a sentence, which is very helpful for recognizing CAPTCHAs, reading images, and identifying menus.

AI Image Description Support by System

4. "Ears" for Hearing-Impaired Users—Complete Live Captions Guide

Live Captions is a system-level speech-to-text feature that's especially useful for hearing-impaired users in noisy environments, libraries, and foreign language scenarios.

Enabling and Optimizing Live Captions

iOS: [Settings] > [Accessibility] > [Live Captions], turn on the switch. The system detects all device audio (calls, videos, podcasts, FaceTime, voice memos) and automatically displays captions at the top of the screen.

Android 12+: Press the volume key → tap the "Live Caption" icon in the popup; or enable in [Settings] > [Accessibility] > [Live Caption].

Accuracy optimization tips:

  1. Use in a quiet environment as much as possible, away from air conditioners and machinery noise;
  2. Recognition is highest when the speaker articulates clearly and speaks at a moderate pace;
  3. Multi-person conversations reduce accuracy—use "show only one person" mode;
  4. Supports mainstream languages like Mandarin Chinese, Cantonese, and English; minority languages have lower recognition rates;
  5. Background music or voice interference during speech will cause intermittent recognition.
5. Dual-Channel Call Scenarios: Speech-to-Text + Text-to-Speech

When users can neither hear nor speak (such as sudden voice loss, laryngeal surgery recovery, severe stuttering), the phone can still act as a "spokesperson."

Real-Time Call Transcription (TTY / RTT)

Android: [Settings] > [Accessibility] > [Live Transcribe] + [Sound Notifications]. "Live Transcribe" displays the other party's speech as text in real time; "Sound Notifications" converts your input to speech when voice isn't convenient.

Compatibility:

  • RTT (Real-Time Text): Both parties need support; text appears as you type with no delay; mainstream carriers now support it;
  • TTY (Teletypewriter): Requires a dedicated TTY device, gradually being replaced by RTT;
  • WeChat/QQ voice-to-text feature is also an alternative: long-press a voice message → "Convert to Text."
6. "Small but Practical" Features for Everyone

IV. Recognizing and Preventing Telecom Fraud

In today's era of rapid AI development, the traditional "seeing is believing" cognitive model has completely failed. Fraudsters are using AI technologies like Deepfake to achieve a comprehensive upgrade from "scatter-shot" fraud to "precision hunting" crime.

1. The Most Common Fraud Patterns in the AI Era

① AI Face-Swap and Voice Cloning Fraud

Criminals illegally collect high-definition photos published by targets on social media or just a few seconds of voice samples, and use AI deepfake technology to clone "friend/family videos" or "voices" realistic enough to deceive. Common scripts include: sudden car accidents, emergency loans, kidnapping, etc. This type of fraud exploits emotional bonds like family and friendship, making it extremely deceptive.

② Impersonating Authority / Customer Service Fraud

AI technology can precisely simulate the voices and tones of official customer service and law enforcement personnel, and even synthesize highly realistic fake transfer screenshots, electronic documents, arrest warrants, and other files. By creating panic or authority pressure, they induce victims to transfer money or enable screen sharing, thereby stealing account information.

③ AI Investment and Wealth Management Fraud

Through AI algorithms, fraudsters forge "guaranteed profit" fake return screenshots, virtual quantitative stock-picking software interfaces, or "master-level" investment analysis reports. Using high returns as bait, they sell expensive investment services or induce victims to transfer funds into trading accounts they control, ultimately absconding with the money.

2. How to Break Through—"Magic Mirror" Defense Thinking

AI can clone voices and faces, but it's not invincible—there are several vulnerabilities to exploit:

  • Action verification method: During video calls, proactively ask the other party to complete preset continuous action commands, such as "wave your hand quickly in front of your face," "turn sideways to show the outline of your ear," or "blink rapidly 5 times in a row." Current AI face-swap technology easily shows flaws like screen stuttering, facial distortion, or edge bleeding when handling complex dynamic occlusion, rapid motion scenes, and lighting changes.
  • Secret signal verification method: Establish and use preset private verification questions, such as "What did you order when we last had dinner together?" or "What was the first gift I gave you?"—details only both parties know. If the other party hesitates noticeably or answers irrelevantly, you should immediately terminate the call and activate safety protocols.
  • Secondary verification method: Strictly follow the "Four Don'ts and One Verify" principle issued by the Ministry of Public Security's Cybersecurity Bureau—don't readily believe unfamiliar information, don't casually transfer money, don't disclose sensitive personal information, don't spread suspicious content. Whenever money is involved, be sure to call back using the known original number or confirm in person. When making transfers, it's recommended to choose the "24-hour arrival" mode to allow ample time for potential fraud identification and fund recovery.
3. Traditional Common Patterns (Still Need Vigilance):
  • Lottery/brushing rebates: Lure with small gains first, then scam big money.
  • Romance scams: "Pig-butchering" scams—build a relationship then steal money.
  • Impersonating acquaintances: Borrow money after account theft (even harder to distinguish in the AI era).
4. Built-in Phone Protections:
  • Harassment call blocking.
  • Verification code theft prevention.
  • SMS link security detection.
5. Anti-fraud mantra:

Don't readily believe, don't disclose, don't click, don't transfer, verify first when in doubt! When encountering an "emergency," stay calm for 5 minutes—almost all scams reveal their flaws in that moment.

V. Resist "Doxing" and Social Engineering Attacks: Cut the Privacy Leak Chain

The core principle of "doxing" (social engineering attacks) is to systematically collect, analyze, and piece together personal digital fragments to build a complete personal profile. The key to defense is drawing clear lines for personal privacy and establishing a multi-layered protection system.

1. Photo Desensitization

Before sharing photos on social media, you must use professional editing tools to completely delete the photo's metadata (EXIF information), including shooting time, device model, GPS location coordinates, and other sensitive data. Strictly redact or blur key information in photos such as house numbers, license plates, ticket barcodes, ID numbers to avoid inadvertently leaking your whereabouts.

  • iOS: [Settings] > [Privacy & Security] > [Camera] can turn off "Precise Location"; selecting "Current photo only" when sharing strips metadata.
  • Android: Some album apps (like Google Photos) prompt whether to keep location info when sharing.
  • General tools: ExifTool, Metapho, etc., can view/edit EXIF.
2. App Permission Convergence

Regularly review and turn off non-essential permissions for e-commerce and tool apps, such as microphone, contacts, location, to prevent apps from "eavesdropping" on daily conversations in the background or illegally collecting personal relationship networks. Uninstall apps that are idle for a long time but still hold high permissions, reducing privacy leak risk points at the source.

  • When you see a "Contacts permission" request, ask yourself: Does this app really need to know all my contacts?
  • Apps you haven't used in three months—uninstall decisively. Permissions should get tighter with use, not looser.
3. Smart Device Security Hardening

IoT devices like smart cameras, smart door locks, and smart speakers must have their default login passwords changed immediately upon first use. Set high-strength independent passwords containing uppercase and lowercase letters, numbers, and special symbols. Keep rarely used remote access features off, regularly check for device firmware updates, and promptly patch security vulnerabilities to prevent hackers from intruding and snooping on your home's private space.

Real Case Warning

In 2022, police in a certain area reported that hackers cracked the default passwords of a brand of home cameras (many users never changed them), stealing bedroom footage from thousands of households and selling it online. Not changing the default password = leaving your front door wide open.

VI. Privacy Protection: Your Data Is Your Property

Photos, chat history, and location trails on your phone are essentially your "digital assets." Once leaked, the consequences range from ad harassment to doxing, fraud, and even extortion.

1. The "Minimization Principle" for App Permissions:
  • Location: Use "Always allow" for ride-hailing/maps; use "Only while using the app" for others.
  • Contacts: Only grant when necessary (like adding friends on WeChat); otherwise deny.
  • Album / All Photos: Since iOS 14+, you can authorize "Selected Photos"; Android 13+ also supports granular authorization.
  • Microphone / Camera: Keep off normally, enable when needed. When suspecting secret recording, swipe out the Control Center to check for "green dot/orange dot."
  • Background tracking: Since iOS 14.5+, apps must ask before using IDFA; Android can turn off "Ad ID."
2. Privacy Management in Practice:
  • Privacy Report (iOS): [Settings] > [Privacy & Security] > [App Privacy Report], see which apps are secretly accessing your location, camera, microphone.
  • Permission Usage History (Android): [Settings] > [Privacy] > [Permission Usage History], see who accessed sensitive permissions and when.
  • Limit ad tracking: Turn off "Ad ID" or "Limit Ad Tracking" in system settings to reduce "targeted ad harassment."

VII. "First Aid" Checklist After Loss / Theft

Don't panic if your phone is lost—follow these steps in order:

  1. Immediately freeze the SIM card: Contact the carrier to report the loss, preventing it from being used to receive verification codes.
  2. Remote lock: Enable "Lost Mode" through cloud services (iCloud / brand cloud).
  3. Change important passwords: Immediately change passwords for all important accounts that were logged in on the phone (payments, banks, email).
  4. Report to police: File a report at the police station and record the IMEI number. This also helps prove identity if the phone is recovered.
  5. Notify family and friends: Prevent scammers from borrowing money in your name.
  6. (Last resort) Remote erase: Only do this when recovery is hopeless—after erasing, the location feature stops working.

VIII. SIM Card and eSIM Security

  • Set a SIM card PIN: Once enabled, you need to enter the PIN every time you restart the phone or swap cards, preventing "pull the card, swap the phone" bypass of the lock screen.
  • eSIM advantages: No physical card to be pulled out, higher security; also supports multi-number switching, very convenient for travel.
  • Secondary card swap risk: Beware of the "carrier secondary card swap" scam—scammers use your identity to reissue a SIM card, thereby taking over your phone number. Setting "card replacement requires the account holder to appear in person at a store with ID" is the most effective defense.

Things You Must Never Do

  • Use weak passwords like birthdays / 123456 / qwerty;
  • Send verification codes to anyone (even "customer service");
  • Click unfamiliar links in text messages, especially those from overseas numbers starting with 00;
  • Log into online banking or make payments on public Wi-Fi;
  • Enable "screen sharing" or "remote control" for strangers.

Try Enabling and Simulating "Remote Lock"

I. Satellite Communication: Call for Help Without Signal

Satellite communication is a major breakthrough in the phone field in recent years. When you're in remote wilderness or deep mountains and your phone has absolutely no signal, satellite communication could be your lifesaver.

Working principle:

The phone directly establishes a connection with orbiting communication satellites to send distress messages. Currently, it mainly supports sending text messages and location information—no phone calls.

Supported models:

Use cases:
  • Outdoor adventure, mountaineering emergencies.
  • Maritime emergencies.
  • Natural disasters causing ground communication outages.
How to use:
  1. Make sure you're in an open area with a view of the sky.
  2. Turn on your phone's satellite communication feature (usually in the emergency call interface).
  3. Follow the on-screen instructions to aim at the satellite.
  4. Send preset distress messages and your location.

Satellite communication requires special hardware antennas in the phone—not all phones support it. Additionally, satellite communication usually requires a paid subscription service, but the emergency SOS feature is generally free.

II. Off-Grid Communication: A New Way of Close-Range Communication

"Off-grid communication" refers to technology that allows two phones to communicate directly with each other without mobile networks or Wi-Fi.

Technical principle:

Uses Bluetooth or dedicated radio frequencies to establish point-to-point connections over short distances (usually a few hundred meters to a few kilometers).

Application scenarios:
  • Outdoor activities: Communication between teammates during hiking and camping.
  • Disaster relief: When communication is disrupted after earthquakes and floods.
  • Large events: When crowds at concerts and sporting events cause poor signal.
Supported platforms:
  • Apple: iOS 17's "NameDrop" and "Find Friends Offline" features.
  • Huawei: The Celia Chat app supports off-grid calls.
  • Xiaomi: Some features of Xiaomi Smart Hub.

III. Crash Detection: A Lifesaver at Critical Moments

Crash detection uses phone sensors to detect severe car crashes and automatically dial emergency numbers.

Working principle:

The phone uses multiple sensors including accelerometer, gyroscope, barometer, and microphone to comprehensively determine whether a severe collision has occurred. After detecting a crash, the phone will:

  1. Pop up a countdown interface, asking if you're okay.
  2. If you don't respond before the countdown ends, automatically dial emergency numbers.
  3. Send your location to emergency contacts.
Supported models:
  • Apple: iPhone 14 series and later.
  • Android: Some high-end models have successively added support.
Things to note:
  • The feature must be enabled to use.
  • May trigger false alarms (like roller coasters, intense exercise)—just cancel in time.
  • Relies on sensors and algorithms—not 100% reliable, but an extra layer of protection.

IV. SparkLink Technology: Next-Generation Wireless Connectivity

SparkLink is a new-generation wireless communication technology led by Huawei, aiming to replace Bluetooth and Wi-Fi in certain scenarios. It matches or even exceeds existing solutions on key metrics: air interface rate up to 900Mbps (far exceeding Bluetooth's 50Mbps), latency as low as 20μs (Bluetooth about 15ms), and a single network supports 4096 devices connected simultaneously (Bluetooth usually under 8).

Technical features:
  • Ultra-low latency: End-to-end latency of only 20 microseconds (0.02ms), two orders of magnitude lower than Bluetooth—gaming and music sync are virtually imperceptible.
  • High speed: Single-link peak rate of 900Mbps, dozens of times that of Bluetooth audio, capable of transmitting lossless audio and even HD video.
  • Low power consumption: Standby power consumption comparable to Bluetooth, but with higher transmission efficiency and lower energy consumption per unit of data.
  • Wide coverage + high concurrency: Connection distance over 50% farther than Bluetooth, with a single network supporting thousands of devices online simultaneously.
Application scenarios:
  • Wireless earbuds: Lower latency, perfect audio-video sync for gaming.
  • Smart watches: More stable connections.
  • Smart home: More devices connected simultaneously.
  • Automotive connectivity: Smoother phone-to-car connections.
Supported devices:

Currently mainly Huawei ecosystem devices, such as Huawei Mate 60 series, some Huawei earbuds and watches. More brands will support it in the future.

Technology Outlook

SparkLink technology represents the future direction of wireless communication. As more manufacturers join, we may see more devices supporting SparkLink, enjoying a better wireless experience.

  2. Search for "crash detection" or "emergency SOS" to check if it's enabled.
  3. If your phone is Huawei, see if it supports SparkLink device connections.

Chapter 10: Mobile Payment and Life Services

To be honest, my parents' generation was initially resistant to the idea of "paying with a phone". "If the money is in the phone, what if it gets stolen?" This concern is very normal, but the reality is: the cash in your pocket has a much higher chance of being lost than mobile payment being fraudulently charged. Mobile payment is not about storing money in your phone — it's just a key, a key that opens your bank account.

1. How Exactly Do You Bind a Bank Card?

Whether you use Alipay or WeChat, the first time you bind a card, you need to go through these steps:

  1. Open the app, find "Me" or "Wallet". Tap "Add Bank Card".
  2. Enter the card number. Most apps now support directly taking a photo to recognize the card number, so you don't have to type each digit.
  3. The bank will send a verification code SMS to your reserved mobile number. Enter it and set a 6-digit payment password.
  4. This payment password, your bank card password, and your lock screen password should ideally be three different passwords — don't set them the same for convenience.

A little tip: if you're concerned about security, you can open a Tier II or Tier III electronic account in your bank app specifically for binding to mobile payment. Only keep your everyday spending money in this card, so even if something happens, the loss is limited.

2. The Payment Code Is "Whoever Sees It, Takes the Money"

Many people are confused about the difference between a payment code and a collection code, and this distinction is very important.

  • Payment Code: The barcode + QR code you show to the cashier to scan. This code is equivalent to your "electronic wallet" — whoever scans it can deduct money from your account. So absolutely never screenshot it and send it to anyone. This code automatically refreshes once per minute precisely to prevent fraudulent use.
  • Collection Code: Others scan this code to transfer money to you. This code can be screenshotted, saved, and sent to others — it's safe.

Remember this distinction, and you can avoid many fraud traps. Anyone who asks you to "screenshot your payment code and send it over" is 100% a scammer.

3. These Security Settings — 5 Minutes to Set Up Can Save You a Lot of Worry

  • Fingerprint/Face Payment: In Alipay and WeChat's payment settings, turn on biometric payment. This way, every payment requires your fingerprint or face scan, so even if your phone is lost, the person who finds it can't spend your money.
  • Security Lock: Both Alipay and WeChat have a "Security Lock" feature. Once enabled, entering the app's "Wallet" page requires an additional identity verification. Path: Alipay → Me → Settings → Account & Security → Security Center → Security Lock.
  • Password-Free Payment Check: Many people have signed up for various password-free payments and forgotten about them. Go to "Payment Settings" in Alipay and WeChat, find "Password-Free Payment / Auto Deduction", and see which services you've signed up for — turn off the ones you don't need.
  • Login Device Management: Regularly check which devices have logged into your account. If you find an unfamiliar device, kick it out immediately. Path: Alipay → Settings → Account & Security → Login Device Management.

Chapter 11: Photography and Photo Management

To be honest, most people's phone photos fall far short of the phone's actual potential. It's not that your skills are bad — it's that you haven't noticed many small settings and habits. This chapter skips the fluff and only covers things you can use right away.

1. Have You Made These Mistakes Too?

Let's first talk about the most common problems — see how many you've hit:

  • The lens is dirty: This really is the most common problem. When you pull out your phone, fingerprints get on the lens, making photos look hazy — you might even think your phone is broken. Make it a habit to wipe the lens with the corner of your shirt before taking photos.
  • You never adjust exposure: In default mode, tapping the screen focuses, but if you swipe up or down after tapping, you can adjust brightness. Many people's photos come out either too dark or too bright simply because they don't know this operation.
  • The flash is always on: The effect of a phone's LED flash is really ugly — the light is harsh and cold, making people look like ghosts. Unless it's truly pitch black, turn it off. In low light, Night Mode works far better than the flash.
  • Zooming by pinching with two fingers: Pinch-to-zoom on the screen is digital zoom, which essentially crops and enlarges the image, noticeably reducing quality. If you want to shoot something far away, use the zoom buttons on the viewfinder (0.5x/1x/2x/3x) — these are optical zoom and don't lose quality.

2. A Few Small Tips to Immediately Boost Your Hit Rate

These don't require any theoretical knowledge — use them once and you'll see the effect:

  • Rule of Thirds + Grid Lines: Turn on "Grid Lines" (also called reference lines) in camera settings. When shooting, place what you want to highlight at the intersections of the four lines, and the composition will immediately "click". This is the simplest and most effective rule in photography — no mysticism, it just matches the visual habits of the human eye.
  • Look for "Leading Lines": When shooting landscapes or street scenes, notice whether there are natural lines in the frame — roads, railings, walls, a row of trees — and let these lines extend from the edge of the frame to the subject. Use this technique well, and passersby looking at your photos will think you've studied photography.
  • Don't always shoot standing up: Most people always shoot at their own eye level, so their photos all have the same perspective. Try crouching down for a low angle, or finding a high spot to shoot downward — the same place can produce a completely different feel. When shooting kids and pets especially, you need to crouch down — only eye-level shots produce a natural feel.
  • Light is the top priority: Shooting portraits under the bright midday sun produces hard, ugly shadows on the face. The best light is during the "golden hours" of early morning and late evening — the sunlight is angled and warm-toned, making everything look good. Overcast-day light isn't flashy, but it's even and soft, making it actually easier to shoot portraits than under bright sun.
  • Night Mode essentials: After turning on Night Mode, the phone automatically takes multiple shots and composites them. The most critical thing at this point is — your hand absolutely cannot shake. Lean against a wall if you can, rest on a table if you can, or hold your breath if you must. Night Mode requires you to hold steady for 2 to 4 seconds — any shake and the image blurs.

3. Portrait Mode and Pro Mode

  • Portrait Mode: It produces the effect of a blurred background with a sharp subject (shallow depth of field). Because phones have small lens sizes, their real bokeh capability is limited, so this relies on AI algorithms — simply put, the AI cuts you out and blurs the background. The best results come at a distance of 1-2 meters; too far or too close and the algorithm easily makes mistakes. Also, when shooting portraits, make sure the background doesn't have overly complex lines, or the AI cutout may "fail" around the hair edges.
  • Pro Mode: If you want full control over the image effect, try Pro Mode. A few key parameters to know: the lower the ISO, the cleaner the image quality, but the darker the image; the slower the shutter, the more light comes in, but hand shake causes blur; white balance controls color temperature — set poorly, photos will look blue or yellow. Most people don't need to use Pro Mode year-round, but understanding these parameters at least lets you know what the camera is doing.

4. Photo Editing: Don't Overdo It — Just Right Is Enough

Many people misunderstand photo editing, thinking that editing makes it "unreal". In fact, photos taken by a camera are already different from what the naked eye sees — appropriate adjustment is to restore the scene you saw at the time, not to fake it.

  • Snapseed: A free editing tool from Google, professional features with no ads. My daily editing flow is: first use "Tune Image" to adjust brightness and contrast → use "Details" to sharpen → use "White Balance" to correct color casts → finally "Crop" to the desired composition. Snapseed's "Selective" feature is very useful — you can adjust only a specific area of the image without affecting the whole thing.
  • Xingtu / Meitu: These two are suitable for editing portraits. But I think there's a principle: keep beauty filters under 30% — beyond that it looks fake, with skin like plastic. Natural is more beautiful than perfect.
  • Lightroom Mobile: If you're particular about color, this is the most professional. It has advanced features like curve adjustment and HSL per-color adjustment. But for most people, it's overkill.
  • Editing Workflow Suggestion: My own habit is to shoot the original photo first, then edit slowly on a computer screen or iPad at home — the phone screen is too small and the color isn't quite accurate. If you must edit on the phone, remember to set the screen brightness to medium-high, and don't edit in power-saving mode.

5. Too Many Photos? Organization Strategies

Thousands of photos piled up in the phone album, taking forever to find a specific one — everyone has had this experience. Here are a few practical organization methods:

  • Make good use of search: Now both iPhone and most Android phone albums support smart search. You can search for keywords like "cat", "seaside", "ID card", "flowers", and the system will use AI to identify image content and find them for you. You can also search by date and location, like "last May" or "Beijing". Not many people know about this feature, but it's really useful.
  • Regularly clean up junk: Screenshots, duplicate photos, blurry failed shots — these are the real culprits taking up space. The iOS album has an automatic "Duplicates" merge feature; on Android, you can search for the "screenshots" folder in the file manager and batch-delete unneeded screenshots. I spend five minutes a month cleaning up and free up several gigabytes.
  • Create albums — don't rely solely on default sorting: The system sorting by time is fine, but for cross-time content like "child's growth photos", "travel photos", and "important documents", you should manually create albums to categorize them. Apple's "Shared Albums" can even let family members add photos together — one person creates it, and the whole family can view it.
  • Backup is the bottom line: Photos are the most irreplaceable data — files can be recreated, chat histories can be lost and that's that, but a child's photos or an elderly person's videos, once lost, are truly gone. You must enable cloud backup. iCloud Photos is the most worry-free; on Android, each brand has its own cloud service, or you can use Baidu Netdisk / Aliyun Drive for auto backup. The principle is: have at least one cloud backup — don't put all your eggs in one basket.

6. Screenshots and Screen Recording

  • Screenshots: Press the power button + volume up button simultaneously — a universal method for all phones. Many Android phones also support three-finger swipe down screenshots and knuckle double-tap screenshots. Long screenshots (scrolling screenshots) are well-established on Android — after taking one shot, tap the "Scroll" button, and the phone automatically scrolls down and stitches together a long image, suitable for capturing chat histories and web pages.
  • Screen Recording: Add "Screen Recording" from the Control Center, tap once to start recording, tap again to stop. Before recording, long-press the record button to choose whether to record microphone sound — turn on the mic if you're making a tutorial with narration, turn it off if you're just recording operations. The recorded video is automatically saved to the album.

Practice: Complete a Full Shoot + Edit + Organize on Your Phone

  1. Turn on "Grid Lines" in camera settings, find a spot with natural light, and take three photos (remember to wipe the lens and adjust exposure).
  2. Select the best one, and use Snapseed for basic adjustments (brightness, contrast, cropping).
  3. Create a dedicated album for this photo, named "My Works".
  4. Check whether cloud backup is enabled, to ensure the photo has a copy in the cloud.

Chapter 11 Appendix: Professional Imaging Technology Knowledge

To truly understand the mysteries of phone photography and video, you need to know some professional imaging technology. This knowledge not only helps you better use your phone's camera, but also makes you more discerning when choosing devices.

1. Resolution, Frame Rate, and Bitrate: The Three Elements of Video Quality

Resolution:

Resolution determines image clarity, expressed in pixel count.

  • 720P (HD): 1280×720 pixels, entry-level clarity.
  • 1080P (Full HD): 1920×1080 pixels, currently the most mainstream resolution.
  • 2K (QHD): 2560×1440 pixels, clearer than 1080P.
  • 4K (UHD): 3840×2160 pixels, ultra HD with rich detail.
  • 8K: 7680×4320 pixels, the future standard, currently with limited device support.

The higher the resolution, the clearer the image, but the larger the file and the higher the demands on storage and processing power.

Frame Rate:

Frame rate determines image smoothness, expressed in frames per second (fps).

  • 24fps: The standard cinema frame rate, with a unique "cinematic feel".
  • 30fps: Commonly used for TV and web video, smooth and natural.
  • 60fps: High frame rate, suitable for sports scenes and game recording, very smooth.
  • 120fps/240fps: Ultra-high frame rate, can be used for slow-motion video.

The higher the frame rate, the smoother the image and the better the slow-motion effect, but the larger the file.

Bitrate:

Bitrate determines the data density of video, expressed in data per second (Mbps).

  • The higher the bitrate, the better the image quality and the more detail preserved, but the larger the file.
  • Too low a bitrate causes compression artifacts like mosaic blocks and color banding in the image.
  • Professional video shooting usually requires high bitrate to preserve more room for post-processing.

2. Video Coding Formats: H.264, H.265, and ProRes

H.264 (AVC):
  • Characteristics: The most widely used coding format, with excellent compatibility.
  • Pros: Almost all devices can play it, with moderate compression efficiency.
  • Cons: Compared to newer formats, compression efficiency is lower and files are larger.
  • Applicable scenarios: Web video sharing, everyday shooting by ordinary users.
H.265 (HEVC):
  • Characteristics: An upgrade to H.264, with about 50% better compression efficiency.
  • Pros: Smaller files at the same quality, supports higher resolutions and frame rates.
  • Cons: Encoding and decoding require more powerful processors; some older devices are incompatible.
  • Applicable scenarios: 4K video shooting, scenarios with limited storage space.
Apple ProRes:
  • Characteristics: A professional video codec developed by Apple, with nearly lossless compression.
  • Pros: Extremely high image quality, large room for post-processing, accurate color.
  • Cons: Extremely large files, requiring massive storage space, and professional equipment for processing.
  • Applicable scenarios: Professional video production, cinema-grade shooting.
ProRes RAW:
  • Characteristics: Combines the advantages of ProRes and RAW formats.
  • What is RAW: Unprocessed raw sensor data, preserving maximum information.
  • Pros: Extremely large room for post-adjustment — you can change exposure, white balance, and other parameters without losing image quality.
  • Cons: Extremely huge files, requiring professional post-production software to process.
  • Applicable scenarios: High-end professional production. The iPhone 15 Pro series supports shooting Apple ProRes video (up to 4K 60fps), but the ProRes RAW format currently requires an external recording device — phones do not yet support shooting it directly.

3. HDR: High Dynamic Range Technology

HDR (High Dynamic Range) technology can simultaneously preserve detail in both the brightest and darkest parts of an image.

  • HDR10: The most basic HDR standard, with good compatibility.
  • HDR10+: A standard promoted by Samsung, supporting dynamic metadata.
  • Dolby Vision: The top-tier HDR standard developed by Dolby, with the best image quality, but requires licensing.
  • HLG: Broadcast-grade HDR standard, compatible with ordinary displays.

When shooting HDR video, you need an HDR-capable display to see the full effect.

4. Color Space and Color Depth

Color Space:
  • sRGB: The standard color space, with the best compatibility.
  • Display P3: A wide-gamut standard promoted by Apple, with richer colors.
  • Rec.2020: The standard color space for HDR video, with the widest gamut.
Color Depth:
  • 8-bit: 16.7 million colors, the standard for ordinary video.
  • 10-bit: 1.07 billion colors, the standard for professional video, with smoother color transitions.
  • 12-bit: 68 billion colors, a high-end professional standard.

Practical Advice

  • Everyday shooting: 1080P/4K + 30fps + H.265 encoding, balancing quality and file size.
  • Sports shooting: 1080P/4K + 60fps, to ensure smoothness.
  • Professional creation: 4K + ProRes (if supported), to preserve maximum post-production room.
  • Storage advice: High-bitrate video takes up a lot of space — back up to your computer or cloud drive regularly.

Explore: Your Phone's Video Settings

  1. Open the Camera app and switch to video mode.
  2. View the resolution and frame rate options, and try different combinations.
  3. If supported, check whether there are advanced options like HDR and ProRes.
  4. Shoot a few video clips with different settings, and compare the image quality and file size.

5. Mobile Gaming: Play Well, Without Harming Your Phone

Chapter 14: Password Manager Special Topic

In the digital age, passwords are the keys to your front door. But with too many keys that are too complex, what do you do? A password manager is your "universal key keeper".

1. Why Do You Need a Password Manager?

Modern people have an average of dozens or even hundreds of online accounts. If you use the same password for every account, once one website is hacked, all accounts are at risk. If you use a different complex password for every account, you simply can't remember them all.

A password manager solves this dilemma: you only need to remember one master password, and it remembers all your other passwords for you.

2. How Password Managers Work

  1. Encrypted Storage: All passwords are stored with high-strength encryption, and only you can decrypt them.
  2. Master Password: You set a master password to unlock the password vault. The master password itself is not stored on the server.
  3. Auto-Fill: On website or app login pages, the password manager automatically fills in your account and password.
  4. Password Generation: Can generate random, complex passwords to improve security.

3. Recommended Mainstream Password Managers

System Built-in:
  • iCloud Keychain: The first choice for Apple ecosystem users. Seamlessly integrated with Safari and apps, free, secure. The downside is that cross-platform use is inconvenient.
  • Google Password Manager: Convenient for Android users. Integrated with Chrome and the Android system, free.
  • Huawei Password Vault: Built into HarmonyOS, suitable for Huawei users.
Third-Party Professional Tools:
  • Bitwarden: Open source and free, powerful, supports all platforms. Highly recommended for privacy-conscious users.
  • 1Password: Beautiful interface, rich features, suitable for families and teams. Paid.
  • LastPass: A long-established password manager, with limited features in the free version.
  • KeePass: Completely local storage, open source and free. Suitable for geek users.

4. Best Practices for Using Password Managers

  • Set a Strong Master Password: The master password is the last line of defense — make sure to set a long, complex password and memorize it firmly.
  • Enable Two-Factor Authentication: Turn on 2FA for your password manager account to add a layer of protection.
  • Regularly Check Weak Passwords: Most password managers have a "security audit" feature that helps you find weak and duplicate passwords.
  • Don't Forget the Master Password: The master password cannot be recovered — forgetting it means losing all passwords. It's recommended to write it down on paper and keep it in a safe place.
  • Emergency Access: Set up an emergency contact, so if you can't access your account, a trusted person can obtain your passwords.

Important Reminder

Password managers are convenient, but they also carry risks: if the master password is leaked, all passwords are exposed. So the master password must absolutely not be the same as other passwords, nor written in an easily found place. Also, it's recommended to choose a service provider with a good security track record.

Chapter 15: Battery Science and Battery Life Optimization

The battery is the "energy heart" of your phone. This chapter helps you understand the little quirks of batteries, learn scientific charging and battery life techniques, and completely bid farewell to battery anxiety.

1. What's the Deal with Batteries? Cycles and Health

A phone battery (lithium-ion or lithium-polymer) is like an energy sponge: charging is absorbing water (storing power), discharging is squeezing water (using power). A few characteristics are worth remembering:

  • Charge as you use: Unlike old batteries, it has no memory effect—you don't have to fully drain it before recharging.
  • Afraid of heat: High temperature is the number one killer of batteries.
  • Aging: Over time, the maximum capacity will decrease—this is normal.
Core Metrics: Cycle Count and Battery Health
  • One cycle is not one charge. For example, if you use 60% today and recharge to full, then use 40% tomorrow and recharge to full, the total 100% consumed counts as one cycle.
  • Battery health is the current maximum capacity as a percentage of brand-new. Below 80%, you'll noticeably feel the battery life shorten.
  • How to check? iPhone: Settings > Battery > Battery Health & Charging; Android: usually search "battery health" in Settings.

2. Charging Mysticism? Fast Charging, Wireless Charging, and the Golden Rule

  • Does fast charging damage the battery? Using original or certified fast-charging accessories causes minimal harm. The phone and charger have temperature control and will automatically reduce power if overheated.
  • Wireless charging: Convenient but less efficient, with more noticeable heat. Suitable for overnight charging.
  • Golden maintenance rule: Try to keep the battery level between 20%-80%. Don't keep it starving (0%) or stuffed (100%).

3. Complete Guide to Fast Charging Protocols: Why Won't Your Charger Charge Fast?

You may have encountered this situation: your phone clearly supports fast charging, but after changing the charger, it becomes very slow. This is because fast charging is not simply "more power is better"—it requires the charger, cable, and phone to "speak the same language". This is the fast charging protocol.

1. Basic Principles of Fast Charging

Charging power = voltage × current (P = U × I). To increase power, you either raise the voltage, raise the current, or both. Different protocols choose different technical approaches.

Fast charging workflow:

  1. Handshake phase: The phone and charger communicate through the data cable, exchanging their supported protocols and power information.
  2. Negotiation phase: Both parties determine a commonly supported maximum power level.
  3. Charging phase: The charger outputs voltage and current according to the negotiated values, and the phone manages the power.
  4. Adjustment phase: As the battery level increases, the phone gradually reduces the charging power to protect the battery.
2. Mainstream Fast Charging Protocols in Detail

USB PD (Power Delivery)—King of Universal Protocols

  • Features: Official standard developed by USB-IF organization, with the widest coverage. PD 3.0 supports up to 100W (20V/5A), PD 3.1 extends to 240W (48V/5A).
  • Voltage levels: 5V, 9V, 15V, 20V; PD 3.1 also supports 28V, 36V, 48V.
  • Compatibility: All iPhone models, Google Pixel, some Android flagships, laptops, and Switch game consoles all support the PD protocol.
  • Advantages: Strong universality—one charger can charge multiple devices; supports PPS (Programmable Power Supply) for fine-tuning voltage and current.
  • Disadvantages: Requires Type-C interface and dedicated chips, higher cost.

Qualcomm QC (Quick Charge)—Veteran of the Android Camp

  • QC 3.0: Supports adjustable voltage from 3.6V-20V in 0.2V steps, up to 18W.
  • QC 4.0/4+: Compatible with USB PD protocol, supports up to 27W, adds dual-path charging technology.
  • QC 5.0: Supports 100W+, 0-50% in just 5 minutes, significantly improved heat.
  • Compatibility: Most phones with Qualcomm Snapdragon processors support it, but brands usually prioritize their own proprietary protocols.

OPPO VOOC / OnePlus WARP / Realme Dart—Representative of Low Voltage, High Current

  • Technical approach: Low voltage, high current (starting from 5V/4A), less heat, higher efficiency.
  • VOOC 1.0: 20W (5V/4A), released in 2014, pioneered the fast charging era.
  • SuperVOOC: 50W-65W, uses dual-cell series design.
  • SuperVOOC 2.0: 65W, fully charges a 4000mAh battery in 30 minutes.
  • 150W/240W Super Flash Charge: Currently the fastest mass-produced fast charging technology.
  • Features: Requires dedicated charger and cable (with extra contacts), not compatible with other protocols.

Huawei SuperCharge—Evolution of High-Voltage Fast Charging

  • FCP (Fast Charge Protocol): 9V/2A, 18W, compatible with QC 2.0.
  • SCP (Super Charge Protocol): Supports higher power, from 22.5W to 66W to 100W.
  • Technical features: Uses charge pump technology to convert high voltage into low voltage, high current input to the battery, with efficiency up to 98%.
  • Compatibility: Exclusive to Huawei/Honor phones; some chargers support PD protocol.

Xiaomi Surge Fast Charging—Rise of Self-Developed Protocol

  • Surge P1 chip: Xiaomi's self-developed fast charging management chip, supports 120W wired fast charging.
  • Surge G1 chip: Battery management chip, works with P1 to achieve more efficient fast charging.
  • Technical features: Uses dual-cell series + charge pump solution, supports multi-protocol compatibility.

Apple Fast Charging—Conservative but Stable

  • Protocol: Only supports USB PD protocol.
  • Power: iPhone 8 and later support fast charging, up to about 27W (iPhone 15 Pro Max can reach 27W+).
  • Features: Relatively conservative power, but well-optimized charging curve, excellent heat control.
  • Accessory requirements: Requires USB-C to Lightning cable (or USB-C to USB-C cable) and PD charger.

4. Wireless Charging Protocols

Wireless charging also has its own protocol standards:

  • Qi standard: Developed by WPC (Wireless Power Consortium), the most universal standard. Basic Qi supports 5W-15W.
  • Qi2 standard: Released in 2023, introduces magnetic alignment (similar to Apple MagSafe), supports 15W+.
  • Proprietary protocols: Each brand has its own high-speed wireless charging, such as Xiaomi 50W wireless charging, Huawei 50W wireless charging, etc., requiring dedicated chargers.
5. Fast Charging Purchasing Guide
  • Check protocol matching: When purchasing a charger, confirm it supports your phone's fast charging protocol. Third-party chargers usually list supported protocols.
  • Check the cable: High-power fast charging requires a dedicated cable. Ordinary cables may only support below 60W.
  • Check certification: Apple users should choose MFi-certified cables; Android users should choose original brand or well-known third-party.
  • Check heat: It's normal for the phone to heat up during fast charging, but if it's burning hot, that's not normal—pause charging or change the charger.

Fast Charging Safety Tips

  • Avoid playing heavy games while charging, which will increase heat.
  • Do not use low-quality chargers and cables, which may cause safety hazards.
  • Remove thick phone cases while charging to help with heat dissipation.
  • If the charger or phone heats abnormally or has an unusual smell, stop using it immediately.

4. These Are the Real Battery-Saving Tips

  • Screen (the power hog): Lower brightness, shorten auto-lock time. OLED screen phones can save a lot of power with dark mode.
  • Poor signal: In elevators and basements, the phone will desperately search for signal, draining power quickly.
  • 5G: If the signal is poor, manually switch back to 4G, and battery life will be much better.
  • Location services: For non-navigation apps, set location permission to "Only while using".
  • Limit background activity: iPhone in Settings > General > Background App Refresh; Android in app battery settings.

  2. Analyze power consumption details: Look at the charts and app list in battery settings to find the power-hungry culprits.

Chapter 16: Account Systems and Cloud Services

I have a friend whose phone was stolen last year. His first thought wasn't "how much was the phone worth", but "can I get my photos back?" Unfortunately, he hadn't enabled cloud backup. A phone is just a physical object, but the data in your account—once truly lost, can never be recovered. In this chapter, we'll talk about how to put real insurance on your digital life.

1. How Much Does Your Phone Account Actually Manage?

Many people think an account is just for downloading apps. In reality, a phone account is tied to much more than you imagine:

  • Contacts: Phone numbers you've saved for years or even decades.
  • Photos and videos: From your child's birth to graduation ceremony, from first date to wedding trip.
  • Notes: Inspirations you jot down, diaries, account passwords (yes, many people keep passwords in Notes).
  • App purchase history: Paid apps and in-app purchases you've spent hundreds on.
  • Find My Phone: After your phone is lost, this is the only tool that can help you locate, lock, and remotely wipe data.

If you use an Apple phone, all of the above is tied to your Apple ID. If you use Android, it's tied to your Huawei/Xiaomi/OPPO/vivo account, plus your Google account. These accounts are the "household register" of your digital life—lose the register, and many things become unprovable.

2. Three Must-Do Things, Right Now

  1. Enable two-factor authentication: Whether you use Apple or Android, the first thing to do is go to account settings and turn on "Two-Factor Authentication" or "Two-Step Verification". This is like adding a deadbolt to your door—password alone isn't enough, your phone also needs to receive a verification code. Even if someone guesses your password online, without your phone, they can't get in.
  2. Bind a reliable recovery method: Two-factor authentication is secure, but there's a risk—what if you lose your own phone? So the system will prompt you to set up a "recovery key" or "backup email/phone number". This is your only way to get your account back in an emergency. Best to write it on paper, don't store it on your phone.
  3. Confirm "Find My Phone" is enabled: This feature can help you locate, lock, play sound, and even remotely wipe all data after your phone is lost. It's the last line of defense. Many people skip this step when buying a phone, and when they want to use it later, they find it wasn't enabled—and it's too late.

3. iCloud: The "Air" for Apple Users

If you use an iPhone, sooner or later you'll face a soul-searching question: what to do when iCloud storage is full?

Apple gives you 5GB for free, and honestly, this 5GB isn't even enough for one backup. So most people eventually receive that annoying popup: "iCloud Storage Full". At this point, you have three options:

  • Clean up: Go to Settings → Your Name → iCloud → Manage Storage to see who's taking up your space. Generally, photos and backups are the biggest. You can delete unnecessary old backups first.
  • Upgrade: 50GB is 6 yuan per month, 200GB is 21 yuan per month. Personally, I think 50GB is enough for most people, mainly for backups and photo thumbnails.
  • Workaround: If you don't want to pay, you can periodically export photos to your computer, then turn off iCloud photo sync. But then you lose the convenience of automatic photo backup.

An important reminder: iCloud Photo Library is not a "cloud drive"—it's "sync". If you delete a photo on your phone, it will also be deleted from iCloud. If what you want is "backup to cloud then clear phone", then iCloud Photos is not what you need—you need iCloud Backup, or a third-party cloud drive.

4. Don't Confuse Sync with Backup

These two words sound similar, but the consequences are completely different:

  • Sync is bidirectional: Delete on phone, delete on cloud; delete on cloud, delete on phone. iCloud Photos, Contacts, Notes are all sync. The benefit is consistency across multiple devices; the downside is "once deleted, it's really gone".
  • Backup is one-way: The phone takes a "snapshot" of its current state and stores it in the cloud. After that, if you delete something on the phone, the copy in the cloud backup is still there. iCloud Backup, local computer backup—these are backups. The benefit is safer data; the downside is they take up space and can't update in real time.

5. The Account Matrix for Android Users

The situation on the Android side is more complicated than Apple, because your data may be scattered across several accounts:

  • Brand account (Huawei/Xiaomi/OPPO/vivo): Manages your cloud space, Find My Phone, app store, wallet. Each brand has its own cloud service, usually a basic-free + paid-expansion model.
  • Google account: If you use Gmail, Google Photos, Google Contacts, these are all in your Google account. Google gives 15GB for free, more generous than Apple, but it's also shared with Gmail and Drive.
  • WeChat/QQ: They are independent of the phone system. Your chat history and WeChat favorites are neither in the brand account nor in the Google account—they only exist on WeChat's own servers.

So when Android users do backups, they need to check separately: brand cloud backup, Google backup, WeChat chat history backup—all three are essential.

6. Family Sharing: Let Your Family Benefit Too

Apple's "Family Sharing" lets you share app purchases, music, movies, iCloud space with your family, and even share location. You can add up to 6 people. Setup method: Settings → Your Name → Family Sharing. Huawei and Xiaomi also have similar features, findable in account settings as "Family Sharing" or "Family Cloud".

A very practical scenario: Set up Family Sharing on your parents' phones, so you can always see their location (provided they agree). If an elderly person wanders off or can't be reached by phone, you have a location.

The Consequences of Account Theft Are More Serious Than You Think

If a hacker gets your account password, they can: remotely lock your phone and extort ransom, view your photos and contacts, make purchases with your payment methods. More troublingly, if your account is tied to too many things, recovering the account itself is a nightmare. So, two-factor authentication—go enable it now.

  2. Check whether "trusted phone numbers" and "recovery key" are set up; write the key on paper.

  • Watch out for latency: When playing games with Bluetooth headphones, there may be slight latency (you hear the gunshot half a second after it fires). If you're sensitive to latency, either use wired headphones or buy Bluetooth headphones that support "low latency mode".

6. With Car Systems: A Safe Partner While Driving

Looking at your phone while driving is illegal and life-threatening. But sometimes you really need navigation, phone calls, and music. This is where CarPlay (Apple) and Android Auto (Android) come in handy.

  • How to connect: If your car supports it (most new cars in recent years do), use a data cable to plug your phone into the car's USB port, and the car screen will automatically switch to the CarPlay or Android Auto interface. Some new cars support wireless connection, even more convenient.
  • What it can do: Navigation (much better than built-in car navigation), phone calls (answer from steering wheel), music (music apps on your phone directly cast to the car), voice WeChat (Siri or Xiaoai helps read messages and reply).
  • Old cars can use it too: If your car is older and doesn't support it, buy an external CarPlay screen for a few hundred yuan, stick it on the dashboard—the experience is about the same.

7. Security and Privacy in Integration

The more devices connected, the larger the surface area for privacy leaks. A few points to note:

  • Before screen casting in public, first check whether your phone notification bar has any private messages—don't let the whole family see what they shouldn't.
  • Before giving or selling a device to someone else, unbind it first—go to phone settings, find that device, and select "Remove" or "Unpair".
  • For smart home cameras, buy branded, encrypted ones—don't buy no-name ones for a few dozen yuan. There's been too much news about cameras being hacked for live streaming.

Try It: Cast Your Phone Screen to TV and Set Up a Smart Scene

  1. Make sure your phone and TV are on the same Wi-Fi.
  2. Find "Screen Mirroring" or "AirPlay" on the TV and turn it on.
  3. On your phone: iPhone taps "Screen Mirroring" from Control Center; Android swipes down to find "Wireless Projection".

  5. If you have smart home devices, try creating a "Leave Home Mode" in the phone app: one-tap to turn off lights, turn off AC, and start the robot vacuum.

Chapter 18: Data Backup and Migration

A neighbor of mine dropped her phone into a pool, and by the time she fished it out, it wouldn't turn on. What hurt her most wasn't the phone itself, but the three years of unbacked-up photos—every record of her child from birth to learning to walk. After hearing her story, I felt terrible, because this was completely avoidable. In this chapter, we'll seriously put insurance on your data.

1. First, Figure Out: What in Your Phone Is Gone Forever If Lost

Different data has different "survival states"—you need to distinguish them:

  • Stored locally on the phone, truly gone if lost: Photos and videos (if cloud sync isn't enabled), notes, text messages, call logs, downloaded files.
  • Stored on app servers, available by re-logging in on a new phone: WeChat friend list, Taobao orders, transaction records in banking apps. These are stored on the corresponding company's servers, not on your phone.
  • In a gray area, requiring special handling: WeChat chat history (although WeChat servers store it, you can't download it from the server yourself), device credentials bound to mobile banking, keys in two-step verification apps (like Google Authenticator).

So what you need to focus on protecting is the first and third categories.

2. Full Phone Backup: Get Everything in One Go

A full phone backup is like taking a complete "snapshot" of your phone—current system settings, home screen layout, app data, photos, contacts, all packaged up.

Option 1: Local Computer Backup (Most Reliable, Recommended for People with Computers)
  • iPhone: Connect your phone to the computer with a data cable, open iTunes (older Windows version) or Finder (Mac), and select "Back Up Now" on the device page. Note: check "Encrypt backup", otherwise passwords and health data won't be included.
  • Android: Each brand has its own computer software, such as Huawei Phone Assistant, Xiaomi Assistant, Samsung Smart Switch. Connect to the computer and follow the instructions.
  • The benefit of computer backup is that you don't need to pay for cloud space, and the restore speed is much faster than cloud restore. The downside is that you need to remember to plug in and back up periodically—most people can't manage this.
Option 2: Cloud Backup (Most Worry-Free, Recommended for Everyone)
  • iPhone: Settings → Your Name → iCloud → iCloud Backup → Turn on. The phone will automatically back up when plugged in, connected to Wi-Fi, and locked. The free 5GB is most likely not enough—recommend spending 6 yuan to upgrade to 50GB.
  • Android: Each brand's cloud has backup functionality. Huawei phones search "cloud backup" in Settings, Xiaomi goes to "Xiaomi Cloud Service", OPPO/vivo similarly. There's also a Google-built backup (Settings → System → Backup)—turn this on too.
  • The biggest benefit of cloud backup is "you don't have to worry about it"—it's automatic. The biggest downside is that restoring takes a long time to download, and the free space is usually not enough.

3. Categorized Backup Strategy: Store Important Things Separately

Full phone backup is the safety net, but some things are too important and deserve a separate extra copy:

Photos and Videos (Most Precious Digital Assets)
  • Dual insurance plan: Enable iCloud Photos/Google Photos auto-sync, and every few months manually copy photos to your computer or external hard drive. This way, one copy in the cloud, one copy locally—mutual backup.
  • Free plan: Google Photos gives 15GB for free, can be set to "compressed quality" to save space. Or every month, use a data cable to copy all photos to your computer.
Contacts (Easily Overlooked but Extremely Important)
  • iPhone contacts sync with iCloud by default—as long as sync is on, no extra backup needed. But if you want to be safer, you can export a vCard file from iCloud.com and save it on your computer.
  • Android contacts usually sync with Google or brand cloud. Go to the Contacts app settings to export a .vcf file—that's a complete backup.
WeChat Chat History (Handle Separately, Top Priority)

This must be discussed separately, because WeChat chat history is often incomplete in full phone backups, and phone migration tools simply can't handle it.

  • Method 1: Migrate to new phone. WeChat → Me → Settings → General → Chat History Migration & Backup → Migrate → Migrate to Phone/Tablet. Connect both old and new phones to the same Wi-Fi, scan the QR code to transfer. Note: Don't log out of WeChat on the old phone until migration is complete.
  • Method 2: Back up to computer. Install WeChat PC version on your computer, three bars in the bottom left → Migration & Backup → Backup & Restore → Back up chat history to computer. Afterward, when you change phones, you can restore it to the new phone.
  • Method 3: Back up to cloud. WeChat also supports backing up to iCloud or phone cloud space, but note: this backup can only be restored on your current phone, not cross-device. To change phones, you still need to use the first two methods.

4. Phone Replacement Guide: From Old Phone to New Phone

Getting a new phone is a happy event, but if the data doesn't transfer over, it becomes a headache. Three scenarios, handled separately:

Same Brand Upgrade (Simplest)
  • iPhone → New iPhone: When turning on and activating, place the old phone nearby, and "Quick Start" will automatically pop up. Use the old phone to scan the pattern on the new phone. All data, settings, and app layout will be transferred intact, including Wi-Fi passwords. Takes about 20-30 minutes.
  • Android same brand: Use the brand's built-in phone replacement tool. Huawei uses "Phone Clone", Xiaomi uses "Xiaomi Phone Transfer", OPPO uses "Phone Move", vivo uses "EasyShare". The steps are similar: install the same app on both old and new phones, scan to connect, check what to transfer, start.
Cross-Brand Phone Replacement (Android to Android, Different Brands)

Use third-party phone replacement tools or the old brand's built-in replacement app (most brands' replacement apps also support transferring to other Android phones). What can be transferred: contacts, photos, text messages, call logs, some apps. What can't be transferred: in-app login status (you need to re-login), WeChat chat history (use WeChat's own migration feature).

Cross-Platform Phone Replacement (Android to iPhone, or Vice Versa)
  • Android → iPhone: Apple has a "Move to iOS" app. Download it on your Android phone, and when activating the new iPhone, select "Move Data from Android". Can transfer contacts, photos, text messages and other basic data, but app data can't be transferred. WeChat chat history still needs WeChat's own migration feature.
  • iPhone → Android: Each Android brand has corresponding migration tools, but honestly, cross-platform migration experience isn't as good as same-platform. Do the most important things first: export contacts, back up photos, migrate WeChat chat history. Take your time with the rest.

5. The Golden Rule of Backup: The 3-2-1 Principle

This is a classic principle in the data security field, simple and easy to remember:

  • 3: Keep at least three copies of important data (one original, two backups).
  • 2: Use two different storage media for backups (e.g., one in the cloud, one on an external hard drive).
  • 1: At least one backup should be in a different physical location (to prevent fire, theft, etc. from destroying all data at once).

For ordinary people, achieving "one in the cloud, one on the computer" is already quite good. Don't make it too complicated—the key is that you actually do it.

Special Attention for WeChat Chat History

WeChat chat history cannot be migrated by any phone replacement tool! It's not covered by full phone backup. You must use WeChat's built-in "Chat History Migration" feature, or back up to the computer version of WeChat. Many people discover after getting a new phone that all chat history is gone—and by then it's too late. The old phone may have already been factory reset.

Practice: Do a Complete Backup Drill

  1. Check whether iCloud Backup or brand cloud backup is enabled, confirm the last backup time.
  2. Manually back up photos: Use a data cable to connect to the computer, copy the DCIM and Pictures folders to the computer.
  3. Back up contacts: iPhone users export vCard from iCloud.com; Android users export .vcf file in Contacts app settings.
  4. Back up WeChat chat history: Open WeChat → Me → Settings → General → Chat History Migration & Backup → Back up to computer.
  5. Copy the exported contacts vcf and photo folders to an external hard drive or USB drive, completing the "one offline copy elsewhere" of the 3-2-1 rule.

Chapter 19: Troubleshooting and Self-Rescue

Phones, like people, occasionally "get sick". Lagging, crashing, black screen, won't charge... most of these issues aren't that scary, and you can try fixing them yourself before sending it for repair. This chapter is your "phone emergency manual"—try low-cost methods first, then seek a professional doctor if needed.

1. Phone Lagging and Overheating—The Most Frequently Asked Question

Honestly, it's totally normal for a phone to start lagging after about a year of use. The reason isn't necessarily that the phone is broken—more often it's "dust accumulation"—junk files, background processes, storage space slowly filling up.

Diagnose First, Don't Rush to Fix:
  1. Check storage space: Go to Settings → Storage to see remaining space. If it's under 10%, the system will become very laggy because it's struggling to free up space. My experience: keep at least 15%-20% free space for the phone to run smoothly.
  2. Check power consumption ranking: Go to battery settings to see the power consumption ranking over the past 24 hours. If an app takes a large share but you barely used it, it's running in the background. Uninstall it directly or restrict background activity.
  3. Think about what you did recently: Did it start lagging after a system upgrade? (It's normal to lag for the first few days after a major version update, as the system rebuilds indexes in the background.) Or did it start lagging after installing a certain new app? (Try uninstalling it.)
  4. Look at the usage environment: If the phone is left in the blazing sun, or in a thick case while charging and playing games, overheating is inevitable. High temperature also causes the CPU to automatically throttle, making the phone feel suddenly laggy. Let it cool down and try again.

Treatment methods (from simple to complex): Restart the phone (solves 60% of problems) → Clean up storage space (delete useless apps and videos) → If a specific app is causing trouble, clear its cache or uninstall and reinstall → Last resort: Back up data and factory reset.

2. App Crashes and Unresponsiveness—When Apps Misbehave

An app crashes when you open it, or suddenly freezes while in use. The handling process is like a doctor's consultation—step by step:

  1. Close and reopen first: Swipe away the app from the background, then reopen it. Most temporary bugs are fixed with this one move.
  2. Update the app: Check the app store for updates. Sometimes it's because the app version is too old and incompatible with the system.
  3. Check permissions: Some apps need a certain permission to work properly, but you didn't grant it. For example, a camera app crashes when opened because it doesn't have camera permission. Go to Settings, find that app, and grant the necessary permissions.
  4. Clear cache: Go to Settings → App Management → Find that app → Storage → Clear Cache. Note: "Clear Cache", not "Clear Data"! Clearing data will delete all your login status and settings in the app, equivalent to factory resetting the app.
  5. Uninstall and reinstall: The last resort. Before uninstalling, confirm whether the app's data will be lost—for WeChat, back up chat history first; for games, confirm whether your account is bound to a phone number.

3. Won't Charge or Charges Slowly—It Might Not Be the Battery's Fault

Not being able to charge is a particularly anxiety-inducing problem, but often the cause is with the accessories, not the phone being broken.

  • Check the charging port first: Use a toothpick or non-conductive small tool to gently clean out dust and lint from the charging port. I've encountered this myself—a thread from my pocket happened to block the charging port. When I plugged it in, I thought it was poor contact, but after cleaning it out, it was fine.
  • Try a different cable and charger: Cables have a much higher failure rate than chargers, especially cables that are often rolled up. Swap in a known-good cable and charger for cross-testing to quickly determine which accessory is broken.
  • Wireless charging issues: If you use wireless charging, check whether the phone case is too thick, or whether there's a metal plate on the back of the case (like a magnetic mount). Also, place the phone precisely in the center of the charging pad—if it's off, it may not charge.
  • Slow charging: Confirm the charger's power is sufficient (iPhone needs at least a 20W PD charger for fast charging), the cable supports fast charging (not all cables can handle 3A+ current), and the phone isn't too hot (too hot will automatically reduce power to protect the battery).

4. Can't Connect to Network, Wi-Fi Keeps Dropping

Mobile data works fine outside, but can't connect to Wi-Fi at home; or Wi-Fi is connected but there's no internet. Troubleshoot in this order:

  1. Toggle Airplane Mode: Swipe down to Control Center, turn on Airplane Mode, wait 5 seconds, then turn it off. This is equivalent to making the phone search for the network again, very effective.
  2. "Forget" Wi-Fi and reconnect: Go to Wi-Fi settings, tap the exclamation mark next to the network name (gear icon on Android), select "Ignore This Network" or "Forget", then re-enter the password to connect.
  3. Restart the router: Many people's home routers haven't been turned off for months, and over time they become unstable. Unplug the power, wait 30 seconds, then plug it back in—most Wi-Fi problems are solved.
  4. Check if it's the phone's problem: Do other devices connect to the same Wi-Fi normally? If they all work fine but your phone doesn't, the phone may need a restart or network settings reset (Settings → General → Transfer or Reset → Reset Network Settings—won't delete your data, but will clear all Wi-Fi passwords).

5. Screen Issues: Unresponsive Touch, Flickering, Image Retention

  • Unresponsive touch: First peel off the tempered glass film to see if it's the film's fault—cheap tempered films can indeed affect touch over time. Water on the screen or oil on your hands can also cause touch drift. If neither, restart and try again.
  • Marks/ghost image on screen (burn-in): Displaying the same image for a long time (like keeping the screen on while browsing TikTok) will leave "ghost image" on OLED screens. This isn't screen damage, just temporary. Turning off the screen and resting for half an hour usually recovers it.
  • Screen flickering: If it flickers at low brightness, it may be a PWM dimming issue, more common on OLED screens. Look for "DC dimming" or "anti-flicker" option in Settings—turning it on helps a lot. Not all phones have this feature.
  • A completely dark/bright area on the screen: This is more serious—likely physical screen damage, requiring screen replacement. Non-official repair screens usually have inferior color and brightness compared to originals, and also affect water resistance.

6. Won't Turn On, Stuck at Logo—Don't Panic First

This situation is most likely to make your heart race, but stay calm and try these first:

  • Charge for half an hour first: Often the battery is just completely drained. Plug in the original charger, wait at least 30 minutes, then try to turn on. Some phones need to charge to a certain level before responding after being fully drained.
  • Force restart: For iPhone full-screen models, press volume up once, volume down once, then press and hold the side power button until the Apple Logo appears (may need to hold for 10-15 seconds). General Android method: press and hold the power button for 10+ seconds until vibration or screen lights up.
  • Stuck at boot screen, rebooting repeatedly: This is called "white Apple" (iPhone) or "stuck at Logo" (Android). If force restart doesn't work, you may need to enter recovery mode and use a computer to repair. Connect iPhone to computer, open iTunes/Finder, enter recovery mode, then select "Update" (not "Restore")—this will reinstall the system but preserve data. For Android, enter Fastboot or Recovery mode; key combinations vary by brand, recommend searching your phone model + "enter recovery mode".
  • If none of the above work: Then you can only seek professional repair. If your data is backed up, no need to worry too much.

7. Sound Issues: Speaker Not Working, Low Call Volume

  • No speaker sound: First confirm it's not silent mode (iPhone side silent switch), Do Not Disturb mode, or volume turned all the way down. Sounds silly, but this accounts for 40% of cases. Then check whether Bluetooth is connected to a headphone or speaker.
  • Caller can't hear you: Check whether the microphone hole is blocked—the small hole at the bottom of the phone is easily blocked by dust. Clean gently with a soft brush. Don't poke hard with a needle or toothpick, you'll damage the dust mesh.
  • Distorted/cracking sound: If caused by water in the speaker—don't use a hair dryer on hot! Use a paper towel to absorb surface moisture, then let it air dry naturally. Online suggestions of water-ejection apps (playing low-frequency sound to vibrate water out) have some effect, you can try.

8. Finding Official Repair, Avoiding Pitfalls

If you've tried all the above methods and it still doesn't work, you need professional repair. But the repair market is full of pitfalls—let me share my own experiences and lessons learned:

  • Find real official, not "looks official": Go to the brand's official website (Apple.com.cn, Huawei.com.cn, etc.) to find the "authorized service provider" list. Non-authorized shops on the street with an Apple logo may not use original parts—after repair, your water resistance is gone in a flash.
  • Must-do before sending for repair: Back up all data (don't assume data won't be lost during repair), log out of all important accounts (Apple ID, WeChat, Alipay), turn off "Find My Phone". Some repair shops will ask for your lock screen password, which has privacy risks—you'd better log out of accounts yourself before sending it over.
  • Screen replacement pitfalls: Non-official screens come in "original-pressed" (original glass replaced), "aftermarket-pressed" (non-original glass), and "assembled" (all non-original). Prices differ by several times, but display quality and durability also differ a lot. If your phone is still under warranty, stick with official repair.
  • Battery replacement pitfalls: Non-original batteries may have falsely labeled capacity, and after half a year of use, degrade more than original batteries do in two years. Apple's official battery replacement price isn't much more expensive than non-official—recommend going official.
  • Data recovery pitfalls: If your phone gets water-damaged or broken, and the repair shop says "data recovery costs extra", be careful—some unscrupulous merchants will deliberately exaggerate the damage to charge more. Legitimate data recovery quotes a price first, then operates, and doesn't charge if recovery fails.

The Universal First Move: Restart Your Phone

No matter what problem you encounter—lagging, crashing, can't connect to network, sound issues—first restart your phone. This sounds like a brush-off, but in my own experience, at least 60% of minor issues are resolved after a restart. Phones, like computers, accumulate all kinds of junk in memory over time—restart clears it and starts fresh. So next time your phone has issues, don't rush to search "what to do"—restart first, then see.

Memorize Now: These Lifesaving Operations Should Be Second Nature

  1. Force restart: Full-screen iPhone: press volume up → volume down → long press power button until Logo appears. Android: long press power button for 10-15 seconds until vibration.
  2. Clean charging port: Find a small tool (toothpick or SIM ejector pin), gently clean dust from the charging port.
  3. Wi-Fi reconnect: Go to Wi-Fi settings, "Forget" your current Wi-Fi, re-enter password to connect.
  4. Toggle Airplane Mode: Swipe down to Control Center, turn on Airplane Mode, count to 5 in your head, turn it off, see if network recovers.
  5. Screenshot the steps for these operations, or write them in Notes. When problems arise, you don't need to frantically search—just flip through Notes.

Chapter 20: Developer Mode and Advanced Permissions

This chapter is for users who want to deeply explore the "hidden features" of their phone. Developer Mode, Root permissions, BL lock... these geeky-sounding concepts are actually not far from ordinary users.

1. Developer Mode: Unlock Hidden Settings

Developer Mode is a hidden menu built into the phone system, containing many features that ordinary users don't need but developers do.

How to enable:
  • Android: Go to Settings > About Phone > tap "Build Number" 7 times continuously, until it prompts "You are now in developer mode".
  • HarmonyOS: Settings > About Phone > tap "HarmonyOS Version" 7 times continuously.
  • iOS: Ordinary users cannot enable full Developer Mode—requires a paid Apple Developer account.
Common features:
  • USB Debugging: Connect to computer for advanced operations like flashing, data recovery.
  • Animation speed adjustment: Reduce or turn off animations to make the phone feel faster.
  • Show touches: Display touch points on screen, suitable for recording tutorials.
  • Background process limit: Limit the number of apps running in the background, saves power.
  • Force GPU rendering: In some cases, improves smoothness.

Notes

Settings in Developer Mode may affect system stability—don't change options you don't understand. After enabling USB Debugging, be careful when connecting to computers, and don't connect to untrusted computers.

2. BL Lock (Bootloader Lock): The First Door to Flashing

Bootloader is the program that loads the operating system when the phone starts. BL lock is a lock added by manufacturers for security, to prevent users from flashing non-official systems.

Purpose of BL lock:
  • Security protection: Prevents malicious software from being flashed into the system.
  • Anti-theft: Even if the phone is flashed, it can't be used without the original account password.
  • Warranty restriction: Unlocking BL usually means losing official warranty.
BL lock policies by brand:
  • Xiaomi: Can be unlocked, requires account binding waiting period (7-30 days).
  • Huawei: Stopped providing unlock codes after 2018, basically cannot be officially unlocked.
  • OnePlus: Can be unlocked, relatively lenient.
  • OPPO/vivo: Some models can be unlocked, requires application.
  • Samsung: Can be unlocked, but triggers Knox fuse, losing Samsung Pay and other features.
  • Apple: iOS devices don't have a traditional BL lock concept, but have similar security mechanisms.

3. Root Permission: Android's "Super Administrator"

Root permission is equivalent to Windows Administrator, the highest permission in the Android system. After obtaining Root permission, you can modify system files, uninstall pre-installed apps, and use advanced apps that require Root.

Benefits of Root:
  • Uninstall pre-installed apps that can't be uninstalled.
  • Use advanced feature apps (like Titanium Backup, Greenify).
  • Modify system interface and functionality.
  • Install third-party Recovery and custom ROMs.
Risks of Root:
  • Loss of warranty: Most manufacturers don't warranty Rooted phones.
  • Security risks: Malicious apps may obtain Root permission, causing serious consequences.
  • System instability: Misoperation may cause system crashes.
  • Banking apps won't work: Many banking and payment apps refuse to run when Root is detected.
  • OTA update failure: May not be able to receive official system updates normally.

Severe Warning

Root operations carry certain risks and may brick your phone (won't turn on). If you're a beginner, recommend fully understanding the relevant knowledge first and ensuring you have a complete backup. Read tutorials carefully before operating, and confirm your phone model and system version.

4. Advice for Ordinary Users

  • Developer Mode: Can be enabled to experience animation acceleration and other features, but don't randomly change other settings.
  • BL lock: If you don't plan to flash, keeping it locked is safer.
  • Root: Not recommended for ordinary users—risks outweigh benefits. If you really need certain features, first look into whether there are alternatives that don't require Root.

Trivia: Magisk

Magisk is a modern Root solution. Its feature is "Systemless"—it doesn't modify the system partition, but achieves Root through mounting. This way, it can hide the Root status, allowing apps that detect Root (like banking apps) to run normally. However, using Magisk still requires unlocking the BL lock.

Hands-on: Enable Developer Mode and Adjust Animation Speed

  1. Go to Settings > About Phone.
  2. Tap "Build Number" 7 times continuously, until prompted that Developer Mode is enabled.
  3. Return to Settings, find "Developer Options" (usually at the bottom of Settings or in "System").
  4. Find "Window animation scale", "Transition animation scale", "Animator duration scale", and set them all to 0.5x or off.
  5. Feel whether the phone has become faster.

Chapter 20 Appendix: Termux—A Portable Linux Terminal Environment on Android Devices

If you feel Developer Mode isn't "hardcore" enough and want to experience a complete Linux command line on your phone, then Termux is for you. It requires no root permission, yet can turn an ordinary Android phone into a portable Linux workstation—a powerful tool for advanced users to explore mobile development and operations.

1. What Is Termux

Termux is an open-source terminal emulator and Linux environment application designed specifically for Android devices. It provides a complete Linux shell experience on your phone without requiring root permission. As a lightweight application (installation package only about 30MB), Termux supports a rich software ecosystem through the APT package management system, allowing users to run tools like Python, Node.js, Git, SSH on mobile devices, and even set up servers or perform penetration testing.

Termux's core advantages lie in its lightweight nature and high extensibility, suitable for development, learning, and system administration scenarios. It combines standard software packages with precise terminal emulation in an open-source solution.

2. Core Features and Characteristics

🖥️ Complete Linux Environment

Termux comes with multiple shell environments including Bash, Zsh, and fish, supports editors like Vim, Nano, and Emacs, providing a near-native command-line experience. Users can install 2000+ packages via "pkg install", including development and security tools like GCC, OpenSSH, and SQLMap.

📦 Powerful Package Management

Uses the APT package manager (similar to Debian and Ubuntu), can install thousands of Linux software packages, covering development tools, network tools, and more. Supports multiple programming language environments including Python, Ruby, Node.js, Go, Rust, and Swift.

🔒 Security and Network Features
  • SSH remote connection: Supports SSH client and server functionality, can remotely connect to other devices or make the phone an SSH host.
  • Encryption tools: Provides encryption tools like OpenSSL, supports data encryption and secure communication.
  • Server setup: Can configure Nginx or Apache to set up a static web server.
🔧 System Integration and Extension
  • Access phone storage permissions for local file management.
  • Call Android hardware features like sensors and cameras through the Termux:API plugin.
  • Supports Bluetooth keyboard and external display connection, with full mouse support.

3. Plugin Ecosystem

Termux's core application can be extended through several optional plugin apps, providing additional functionality and integration:

Plugin Name Feature Description
Termux:API Exposes Android API functionality to command-line programs
Termux:Boot Allows running scripts when the device boots
Termux:Float Provides a floating terminal window
Termux:Styling Enables color schemes and font customization for the terminal
Termux:Tasker Integrates Termux scripts with the Tasker automation app
Termux:Widget Allows running scripts from home screen widgets

Important Note

Termux and its plugins must be installed from the same source, because they share the same sharedUserId. Mixing installations from different sources (such as F-Droid and GitHub) will result in installation errors.

4. Installation and Configuration Guide

📥 Installation Steps
  1. Download the app: Download and install Termux from official channels (F-Droid or GitHub), no root permission required.
  2. Initialize the system: Wait for "Installing bootstrap packages" to complete after first launch.
  3. Get storage permission: Execute termux-setup-storage to get phone file system permission.
  4. Update package sources: Execute pkg update && pkg upgrade to update the package list.
⚙️ Basic Optimization
  • Change mirror source: Use the termux-change-repo command to change the mirror source through a graphical interface (Tsinghua University mirror recommended).
  • Shortcut settings: Volume down key simulates Ctrl, volume up key simulates Esc, improving input efficiency.
  • Interface beautification: Install Zsh + Oh My Zsh, configure themes and plugins.

Chapter 21: Mobile Networks and Information Security In-Depth

We use our phones every day to surf the internet, pay, and chat, feeling quite convenient. But have you ever thought about how often your chat records, payment passwords, photos... these pieces of information are "running naked" online? Honestly, most people never think about this question, but once something goes wrong—such as your account being stolen or your bank card being swiped—it's too late to regret. In this chapter, let's talk about how to make your digital life less "transparent."

1. VPN: The "Invisibility Cloak" in the Network

VPN stands for Virtual Private Network. You can imagine it as building a dedicated, encrypted underground tunnel between your home and the website you want to visit. You walk back and forth in the tunnel, and people outside can't see what you're holding or where you're going.

  • Regular internet access: Your phone → public network → target website. Any of the routers and switches in between could have someone "peeking" at the data you send. Especially on public Wi-Fi in coffee shops and airports, to put it bluntly, it's basically "running naked."
  • Using a VPN: Your phone → VPN encrypted tunnel → VPN server → target website. The data is encrypted and encapsulated, so "peeping toms" can only see a bunch of garbled text. Additionally, the target website sees the IP address of the VPN server, not your real IP.

So what can a VPN actually do? Simply put: first, protect your privacy on public Wi-Fi; second, bypass geographical restrictions (such as watching content only available in certain countries); third, prevent your Internet Service Provider (ISP) from logging your browsing habits. But I must remind you: don't use free VPNs! Free often means your data is their "commodity." I have a friend who used a free VPN, and his phone started popping up ads for no reason. Later we found out that the VPN had packaged and sold his browsing data to advertisers. So when choosing a VPN, either choose a paid big brand (like NordVPN, ExpressVPN), or simply don't use one.

Also, VPN is not omnipotent. It can only protect data "in transit." If the target website itself is insecure, or your phone already has malware, a VPN can't help you.

2. The "Sweet Trap" of Public Wi-Fi—More Dangerous Than You Think

You might think, it's just connecting to free Wi-Fi, how much risk can there be? Let me tell you a true story: security researchers once did an experiment where they set up a Wi-Fi hotspot called "Starbucks_Free" at an airport, and within an hour, over a hundred people connected. In reality, this hotspot was set up by the researchers themselves, and all unencrypted traffic passing through this hotspot—such as the content of HTTP web pages you visit and App communications without TLS enabled—they could indeed intercept and view.

But note a key premise: today, the WeChat, online banking, and Alipay you use daily have mostly already adopted HTTPS encryption. In other words, even if attackers intercept the data packets, what they see is just a bunch of garbled text, and they cannot directly read your chat content or payment password. However, they can still know which websites you visited (domains). If certain old Apps or web pages are still using HTTP plaintext transmission, the content will be completely exposed. This type of attack is called a "Man-in-the-Middle" attack, and the principle is that the attacker "inserts themselves" between you and the real Wi-Fi router. What's more frightening is that many people's phones are set to "automatically connect to known Wi-Fi." Attackers only need to set their hotspot name to one you've connected to before (such as "ChinaNet" or "CMCC"), and your phone will automatically connect to it.

So how to defend against this? Here are a few practical suggestions:

  • Look for HTTPS: The address bar starts with https://, and the extra "s" stands for Secure. When accessing any website that requires a password, if you see http:// (without the s), stop immediately and don't enter anything! Now mainstream browsers will display an "insecure" warning before non-HTTPS websites, so be alert when you see this mark.
  • Turn off "auto-connect": Whether iPhone or Android, turn off "auto-join" or "auto-connect" in Wi-Fi settings. Rather than letting your phone judge for itself, manually tap each time.
  • Use mobile data for sensitive operations: Transfers, logging into online banking, entering ID numbers... honestly use your own 4G/5G data for these operations, don't save that little bit of data.
  • Forget infrequently used Wi-Fi: After returning from a business trip or travel, "forget" all the hotel Wi-Fi and airport Wi-Fi you temporarily connected to before. This way, next time your phone won't automatically connect to a fake hotspot with the same name.

3. How Does HTTPS Protect You?—SSL/TLS Handshake

I just mentioned HTTPS, so how does it actually work? Every time you visit an HTTPS website, an "SSL/TLS handshake" occurs between the browser and the server. The whole process goes something like this:

  1. Your browser says: "Hey, server, I want to communicate securely with you, here's my list of encryption capabilities (supported encryption algorithms, etc.)."
  2. The server replies: "Okay, I've selected this set of encryption schemes from your list, here's my digital certificate, which contains my public key, signed and certified by a certain CA organization."
  3. The browser verifies the certificate's authenticity: Was it issued by a trusted CA? Has it expired? Does the domain name match?
  4. After verification passes, both parties use public key encryption to negotiate a "session key," and all subsequent communication is encrypted with this key.

This is like going to a bank to do business: you first hand over your ID card (certificate), the teller verifies your identity, then the two of you agree on a secret code, and subsequent conversations can only be understood by the two of you. Even if someone eavesdrops nearby, they can only hear encrypted garbled text.

But note: HTTPS only guarantees that the "transmission process" is encrypted, not that the target website is a good actor. Phishing websites can also use HTTPS—that little lock icon only indicates that you're connected to "some website," not that the website is the one you want to visit. So you still need to verify the URL yourself.

4. DNS Security: Is Your "Network Phonebook" Safe?

DNS (Domain Name System) is like the internet's phonebook: you type "baidu.com," and DNS helps you look up which IP address it corresponds to (such as 110.242.68.66), and then the browser can connect. The problem is that traditional DNS queries are plaintext and not encrypted! This means your ISP, Wi-Fi provider, or any intermediate node can see which websites you've visited.

What's even more disgusting is "DNS hijacking": attackers tamper with the DNS process to point "baidu.com" to a fake phishing website. You clearly typed the correct URL, but you're taken to an identical but actually fake login page, and once you enter your account password, you're done for.

Fortunately, there are now encrypted DNS solutions:

  • DNS over HTTPS (DoH): Hides DNS queries in HTTPS traffic, mixed in with normal web browsing traffic, making it impossible to distinguish which ones are DNS queries.
  • DNS over TLS (DoT): Uses the TLS encryption protocol to specifically protect DNS queries, with an independent port. Network administrators can know you're using DoT but can't see the content.

I personally recommend using Cloudflare's 1.1.1.1 or Google's 8.8.8.8 as DNS servers; both support encrypted DNS. In Android's "Private DNS" setting, just enter "one.one.one.one" or "dns.google," super simple. iPhone is a bit more troublesome, requiring you to install a profile or set it up through an App, but it's worth doing.

5. Does "Incognito Browsing" Really Make You Invisible?

First, the conclusion: Incognito mode is your window curtain, not an invisibility cloak.

What it does: It doesn't leave traces locally—after closing the incognito window, the browser doesn't save your browsing history, cookies, form fill-in records, or search history. It's appropriate to use incognito mode when looking something up on someone else's computer or buying a gift without family members finding out from browsing records.

What it can't do:

  • The website you visit can still see that you came; your IP address, device information, and browsing behavior are clear on the website's end.
  • Your network operator can still see which websites you visited, just not the specific content (if HTTPS is used).
  • Company/school network administrators can also monitor your internet behavior.
  • Files you download and bookmarks you add will be retained and won't disappear when you close the window.

So remember: Incognito mode is a "local eraser," not a "network invisibility cloak." If you really need to hide your internet traces, you need a combination punch like VPN + Tor browser, but for daily use, knowing this much is enough.

6. Browser Privacy: Don't Let the Browser Sell You Out

You may not realize that the browser itself is a huge information collector. Cookies, browsing history, cache, autofill, location permissions... each one could leak your privacy. A few suggestions:

  • Clean browsing data regularly: Every once in a while, go to settings and clean out cookies, cache, and history. Not to prevent "being tracked"—that's no longer very effective by clearing cookies—but to prevent your phone from getting slower and slower. Browser cache can take up several GB of space.
  • Give up the "Do Not Track" fantasy: The "Send 'Do Not Track' request" option in browsers is essentially "asking" websites not to track you. But whether websites listen is another matter entirely; most websites simply ignore it. Don't count on it.
  • Make good use of privacy-protecting browsers: Firefox Focus, DuckDuckGo browser, Brave—these browsers block trackers and ads by default, much cleaner than the default settings of Chrome and Safari.
  • Search engines matter too: Use DuckDuckGo instead of Baidu/Google search; it doesn't record your search history or build user profiles. Honestly, the search results are indeed not as precise as Google, but it's enough for daily research.
  • Check App permissions: Go to phone settings and see what permissions your browser has been granted. Does it really need to access your location, microphone, contacts? Probably not.

7. Practical Security Checklist: Protect Yourself Starting Now

Having said all this, here's a simple and straightforward security checklist. Spend half an hour to get it done, and your phone's security will have a qualitative leap:

  1. Turn off Wi-Fi auto-connect: iPhone in Settings-Wireless LAN, Android in Wi-Fi settings.
  2. Switch to an encrypted DNS: Android directly set Private DNS to "dns.google"; iPhone use an App or profile to set up 1.1.1.1's DoH.
  3. Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA): WeChat, Alipay, Apple ID, Google account—enable 2FA wherever possible. SMS verification codes are already the lowest level of 2FA; using TOTP (such as Google Authenticator, Microsoft Authenticator) is even better.
  4. Review all App permissions: Go through each App's permissions, especially these five: location, contacts, SMS, microphone, camera. You'll find some flashlight Apps surprisingly want your contacts—uninstall them immediately.
  5. Clean browser data regularly: Set a calendar reminder, once a month, to clean cache and cookies.
  6. Don't use data cables from public charging stations: USB ports can both charge and transmit data; malicious charging stations (called "Juice Jacking") can steal data while you charge. Bring your own charger head and cable when going out.

Do It Now: Give Your Phone a "Security Checkup"

  1. DNS upgrade: Android: Settings > Connections & Sharing > Private DNS > enter "one.one.one.one" or "dns.google". iPhone: Use Safari to search "1.1.1.1 DNS profile" to install the profile, or download Cloudflare's 1.1.1.1 App.
  2. Wi-Fi audit: Open the Wi-Fi list and "forget" all unfamiliar, infrequently used, and strangely named hotspots.
  3. Permission cleanup: Go to Settings > Privacy/Permission management, review permissions for each App. Mark Apps with unreasonable permission requirements (such as a calculator wanting contacts), and consider uninstalling or using alternatives.
  4. Check account security: Open the security page of your Apple ID or Google account, see if there are any unknown device logins, confirm 2FA is enabled, and check whether recovery contacts are trustworthy.

6. Prompt Optimization: The "Communication Formula" That Makes AI Follow Your Commands

The quality of AI's answers largely depends on how you "ask." The same question, asked differently, will get vastly different answers from AI. Below we introduce a most practical, easiest-to-use "Role-Background-Task-Constraint" communication approach, with many examples.

Why do we need a framework?

Many people use AI like using Baidu—typing in a few keywords. But AI is not a search engine; it's a language model that "reads, understands, and then answers." Give it more context, clearly state what you want, and constrain it from going off track, and it can give you precise results. A framework translates "what a good answer looks like" in your head into instructions AI can understand.

1. Role: Tell AI Who It Is

Give AI a specific "identity," and it will draw on the professional knowledge and expression style of the corresponding field. The more specific the role setting, the more professional the output.

Practical examples:
  • "You are a Xiaomi phone repair engineer with 10 years of experience." (Technical question)
  • "You are a middle school physics teacher who excels at explaining complex concepts simply." (Science popularization question)
  • "You are a book editor with good aesthetics, well-read, and your own style." (Writing question)
  • "You are a cooking blogger who makes breakfast for office workers every day." (Life question)
  • "You are a patient, gentle Traditional Chinese Medicine practitioner who never laughs at beginners." (Health question)

Why is it effective? The role sets AI's knowledge scope, expression style, and professional depth. An AI without a role is a "jack of all trades but master of none"; with a role, it can "enter" that identity and think like that professional.

2. Background: Tell AI Your Situation

Clearly state "who you are, your current situation, your constraints" in advance, and AI can give answers that are truly suitable for you, rather than generic talk.

Practical examples:
  • "My phone is a Xiaomi 14, bought half a year ago, recently overheating severely when playing games." (Device situation)
  • "I am a 60-year-old retired auntie with poor eyesight, not very good at using smartphones." (User profile)
  • "I am preparing for the June 2026 CET-6 English exam, with average foundation." (Study background)
  • "I run a small coffee shop in a third-tier city, with monthly revenue of 30,000 yuan and two employees." (Business background)
  • "I just had a baby and am doing postpartum confinement at home, severely sleep-deprived." (Life stage)

Why is it effective? Background information lets AI know "who to serve" and "under what conditions". The same question is explained differently to students vs. parents; different suggestions are given to urban vs. rural people. Without background, AI can only give "lowest common denominator" answers, which are often bland.

3. Task: Clearly Tell AI What to Do

This step should start with a verb (write, list, compare, translate, explain, summarize...), and describe the task specifically and executably. Vague tasks only get vague answers.

Practical examples:
  • "Please list 5 permissions I should immediately turn off and explain why." (Clear action)
  • "Please translate the following English email into Chinese, maintaining a business tone." (Specific task)
  • "Please compare these four phones in the 3,000 yuan price range and provide a pros and cons comparison table." (Clear format)
  • "Please explain 'what is 5G' in words a primary school student can understand." (Clear style)
  • "Please write three short video scripts for my breakfast shop, targeting office workers, couples, and parents with children respectively." (Clear segmentation)

Why is it effective? The task part is the "command center" for AI's actions. The clearer the verb and the more specific the requirements, the better AI knows "what to deliver". Vague words like "do something," "make a," "take a look" are taboos for AI—it will guess your meaning, and the results often go off track.

4. Constraints: Tell AI What Not to Do

"Constraints" sound like "restrictions," but they're actually a powerful tool for making AI converge on the output that suits you best. Common dimensions include: word count, format, tone, things to avoid, things to include.

Practical examples:
  • "No more than 200 words, list in bullet points." (Length + format)
  • "The tone should be warm and natural, like chatting with a friend, don't use bureaucratic language like 'first, second, third'." (Tone)
  • "Don't use technical terms; when encountering technical terms, explain them in passing." (Word restrictions)
  • "Each paragraph no more than 3 lines, for easy mobile reading." (Layout)
  • "Must include: usage scenarios, pros and cons, my budget match." (Must-include items)
  • "Don't give specific investment advice, only explain principles." (Boundaries)

Why is it effective? Without constraints, AI tends to "freestyle"—either too academic, too long, or off-topic. Clear constraints = clear "delivery standards," aligning expectations on both sides, making results controllable.

5. Prompt Quality Assessment: 5 Characteristics of a Good Prompt

Writing good prompts is not a one-time thing. After writing, first self-check these five points, then send to AI:

  1. Specific rather than abstract: Not "write an article," but "write an 800-word, 3-section phone buying guide for new moms."
  2. Include background rather than isolated: Tell AI "who I am, why I'm asking this," so it can give you a "customized" answer.
  3. Have a clear output format: Is it a table, list, paragraph, or JSON? State it in advance.
  4. Constraints in place: Word count, style, things to avoid—any one of these three brings noticeable improvement.
  5. Verifiable by AI: After asking, have AI repeat your requirements; if it can't articulate them clearly, you haven't been clear.

Doesn't it feel great just thinking about it?

Automation is essentially the "if... then..." logic chain, the if-else in programming. Once you understand this way of thinking, even if you never write code in your life, your problem-solving organization will be much stronger. Core elements:

  1. Trigger condition: When to start? It can be "every morning at 7 AM," "when I connect to home Wi-Fi," "when I arrive at a certain location," "when phone battery is below 20%."
  2. Action to execute: What specifically to do? It can be sending a WeChat message, opening an App, adjusting volume, switching wallpaper, playing music, sending notifications... almost everything you can do manually on your phone, automation can do.
  3. Intermediate logic (optional but powerful): If situation A, do X; otherwise do Y. For example, "if it's a workday, play news podcast; on weekends, play music"—this is called conditional branching.

2. iOS "Shortcuts"—The Automation Magic Tool for Apple Users

Apple's "Shortcuts" App is a built-in application that doesn't need to be downloaded. Its core concept is to put various operations together like building blocks. First, let's cover a few key concepts:

  • "Shortcut": A complete automation flow that can be manually tapped to run, or triggered by Siri voice. For example, you say "Hey Siri, going home mode," and it automatically executes a series of operations you've set up.
  • "Automation": Instructions that run automatically based on trigger conditions, without needing you to manually trigger them. For example, "when the phone connects to car Bluetooth, automatically open the Maps App." Automations are set up under the "Automation" tab, separate from "Shortcuts."
  • "Action": The smallest execution unit. Opening an App is an action, sending a message is an action, getting weather is an action, adjusting brightness is an action. You can chain hundreds or thousands of actions together.

Let me show you a few genuinely useful examples; you'll understand after following along:

Example 1: One-Click "Good Morning Routine"

5. Pitfall Guide: Common Problems with Automation

Automation isn't all smooth sailing; I've stepped into a few pitfalls for you first:

  • Location triggers may not work reliably: iOS's "arrive/leave location" trigger uses geofencing technology, and accuracy depends on GPS signal and system optimization. Sometimes it triggers only 10 minutes after you've arrived home. It's recommended to use Wi-Fi connection or cell tower triggers instead of location triggers—more reliable and more power-efficient.
  • Shortcuts "Automation" requires authorization: In iOS, most triggers under the "Automation" tab require manual confirmation to run (iOS's security mechanism). However, Wi-Fi connection, Bluetooth connection, and alarm stopped triggers can run fully automatically without confirmation.
  • Tasker requires accessibility permission: Many advanced features (such as simulating taps) require accessibility service permission, which makes the phone display a persistent notification in settings. This is Android's security design and can't be removed.
  • Don't make automation too complicated: When starting automation, begin with small flows of "2-3 actions." Don't jump straight into a 20-step super flow—you'll go crazy debugging it.

2. The Difference Between Git and GitHub (Key Concepts!)

Many people confuse these two terms when first encountering them; let me clarify for you:

Comparison Item Git GitHub
Nature Version control tool (software) Code hosting platform (website)
Running location On local computer In the cloud (browser access)
Core responsibility Record every change to files (what changed, who changed it, when) Put Git repositories in the cloud for easy storage, display, and collaboration
Network required? Not required, works offline Requires internet access
Ownership Open source software, created by Linus Torvalds (father of Linux) Microsoft product
Similar competitors SVN, Mercurial GitLab, Gitee, Bitbucket

Simply put: Git manages "how to record changes," GitHub manages "where changes are stored and how to collaborate". Git is a tool that runs locally; GitHub is a platform that puts Git repositories in the cloud for easy collaboration and display. You can use Git without GitHub (such as doing version management for your own code), but using GitHub requires using Git (because GitHub is based on Git).

3. GitHub's Core Concepts and Features

📁 Basic Concepts

Before getting started, first get to know a few high-frequency words, otherwise you'll be confused when looking at GitHub's interface:

  • Repository (Repo for short): One project corresponds to one repository, which stores all the code files, historical versions, and documentation for that project. You can think of it as a "project folder."
  • Commit: A record of one modification to the code. Each Commit comes with a description, recording "what was changed this time, why it was changed." This is the foundation of Git/GitHub's ability to "time travel."
  • Branch: An independent development line split from the main line. For example, if you want to add a new feature, you can open a branch to develop separately, and merge it back to the main line after development, without affecting others.
  • Clone: Copy a complete version of the cloud repository to your local computer, after which you can modify the code locally.
  • Push/Pull: Push is uploading local code to GitHub, Pull is downloading the latest code from GitHub to local. These are the two core actions for syncing code during team collaboration.
  • Pull Request (PR for short): When you've finished modifying code on a branch and want to merge it into the main branch, you initiate a PR, letting team members review the code before merging. This is GitHub's core collaboration mechanism.
  • Issue: Used to record bugs, feature requests, and discussion topics. Each Issue is like a discussion thread, where you can assign responsible persons and add labels.
  • Fork: Copy someone else's repository to your own account, where you can modify your copy freely without affecting the original repository. After modifications, you can send a PR to the original author, requesting to merge your improvements.
🛠️ Core Features

GitHub provides more than just code storage, including:

  • Version control: Records the history of every code change; if you break the code, you can always return to the last working version.
  • Team collaboration: Supports parallel development of the same project by multiple users; multiple people developing simultaneously won't overwrite or conflict.
  • Issue tracking: Use Issues to record bugs and feature requests, making it easy for teams to follow up.
  • Project management: Integrates to-do items, project boards (Projects), and other management tools, equivalent to a built-in lightweight Trello.
  • Automation: Through GitHub Actions, achieve automatic compilation, testing, packaging, and deployment after code submission—the core tool of modern "CI/CD (Continuous Integration/Continuous Deployment)."
  • Documentation management: README introduces the project, Wiki stores detailed architecture and development standards, and Pages can even directly host static websites.
  • Code hosting and display: Each repository has a beautiful web display with syntax highlighting—it's the "front door" of your technical work.
  • Open source community: You can Star (favorite) projects you like, Watch (follow) project updates, Follow (follow) developers, and participate in global open source collaboration.

5. Basic Workflow: From Local to Cloud

To understand GitHub's workflow, the core is to remember this cycle: local modification → staging → commit → push → cloud update. Below, each step is explained in plain language:

  1. Modify files: Write code or modify documents using an editor on your local computer.
  2. git add: Add the modified files to the "staging area," equivalent to telling Git "I'm ready to record these changes."
  3. git commit: Formally record the changes in the staging area as a "commit," with a description, such as "fix login bug." This step is only done locally.
  4. git push: Upload local commits to GitHub cloud, so others can see them.
  5. git pull: Before starting work, first pull the latest code from the cloud to ensure you're in sync with the rest of the team.

For beginners, remember this mnemonic: "Pull first then modify, add after modifying, commit with description, finally push up". Develop this habit, and team collaboration won't be chaotic.

6. Team Collaboration Process: How Pull Request Works

GitHub's most powerful aspect is its collaboration mechanism. A typical team collaboration process goes like this:

  1. Open a branch: Open a new branch from the main branch (usually called main or master), such as feature/login, and develop new features on this branch.
  2. Develop and commit: Write code on the new branch, with multiple commits to record progress.
  3. Push to GitHub: Push the branch to the cloud repository.
  4. Initiate Pull Request: Initiate a PR on the GitHub webpage, requesting to merge your branch into the main branch. Clearly state what changes this PR makes and why.
  5. Code Review: Team members review your code, offering feedback or suggesting changes. You can continue to commit based on feedback, and the PR will automatically update.
  6. Merge: After review passes, click "Merge" to merge the code into the main branch. Your contribution is officially part of the project!

This process ensures that all code is reviewed before entering the main branch, greatly reducing the chance of errors. Open source projects also use this mechanism to accept contributions from strangers worldwide—you can completely submit a PR to a well-known open source project, and once adopted, you become a contributor to that project.

8. Advice for Beginners

  • Write clear commit descriptions: Don't write useless descriptions like "update." A good commit message says "fix the issue of jumping to the wrong page after user login"—half a year later, you'll still know what you did.
  • Commit frequently, take small steps: Commit once for each small feature completed, rather than writing all day before committing once. This makes it easier to locate problems.
  • Develop on branches, don't directly modify the main branch: Even for personal projects, develop the habit of opening branches, so you won't be flustered when collaborating on teams in the future.
  • Make good use of .gitignore: This file tells Git which files not to include in version management (such as password configs, build artifacts, dependency folders), avoiding uploading junk files.
  • Look at excellent open source projects more: Browse GitHub Trending, look at the code structure, README writing, and collaboration process of high-star projects—it's the fastest free way to improve.
  • Apply for GitHub Student Pack: After student verification, you can get free access to many paid tools and services (Copilot, domains, cloud services, etc.), worth thousands of dollars.

Security Reminder

  • Never commit sensitive information like passwords, API keys, database connection strings to GitHub, especially public repositories—once leaked, they'll be scraped by crawlers in seconds, with serious consequences.
  • If you accidentally commit sensitive information, just deleting the file isn't enough (it's still in the history); use git filter-branch or professional tools to clean history, and immediately change the leaked keys.
  • Private repositories aren't absolutely secure either; sensitive information is best managed with environment variables, not in code repositories.

Appendix F: Foldable Screen Buying & Usage Guide

Foldable screens have evolved from "future technology" into a mature product category that you "can buy now." However, they differ greatly from traditional slab phones in form factor, usage habits, and maintenance methods. Whether it's worth buying, how to choose, and how to use them—this guide will help you sort it all out.

I. How Far Have Foldable Screens Developed?

First, the conclusion: Foldable screens are no longer "novelty toys," but neither are they a mainstream form factor that "everyone should buy". They sit somewhere between niche enthusiast products and mass-market consumer goods, suitable for specific groups of people.

Currently, there are two mainstream categories:

  • Horizontal Folding (Book-style): When closed, it's the size of a regular phone; when opened, it's nearly square like a small tablet. Representatives: Huawei Mate X series, Samsung Galaxy Z Fold series, Xiaomi MIX Fold series, vivo X Fold series. Main selling point: "one phone = phone + tablet".
  • Vertical Folding (Clamshell-style): When closed, it looks like a compact powder case; when opened, it's a normal-sized phone. Representatives: Huawei Pocket series, Samsung Galaxy Z Flip series, OPPO Find N Flip, Xiaomi MIX Flip. Main selling point: "compact portability + cover screen quick view".

Key advancements in 2024-2025: Hinge lifespan is generally rated at 500,000 folds (opening/closing 100 times daily lasts 13 years), UTG ultra-thin glass has replaced early plastic films, creases are visible to the naked eye but barely perceptible by touch, prices have dropped from the 10,000+ yuan range down to the 5,000-6,000 yuan range.

One-Sentence Verdict

If your current slab phone fully meets your needs and you have no pain point of "screen not being big enough," don't buy a foldable. It's a "nice-to-have" device, not a "must-have" device.

II. Who Should Buy a Foldable Phone?

Based on real user feedback, these groups have a high probability of finding foldables "worth it":

  1. Heavy Readers / Document Processors: Frequently view PDFs, annotate documents, and read long articles on their phone. When unfolded, the ~7.5-inch inner screen lets you view A4 PDFs without zooming—a transformative experience.
  2. Frequent Travelers / Business Users: Don't want to carry a tablet but find phone screens too small. Horizontal foldables let you view PPTs, sign contracts, and compare emails side-by-side—significantly more efficient than a slab phone.
  3. Creatives Who Love "Flex Mode": Fold to 90° and place on a desk—upper screen for framing, lower screen for shutter (selfies/timelapse); upper screen video, lower screen notes; upper screen livestream, lower screen comments—a form factor slab phones simply cannot achieve.
  4. Female Users Who Want Portability + Big Screen: Vertical foldables fit into small bags when closed, unfold to normal phone size, and the cover screen can serve as a makeup mirror or notification viewer.

These People Should NOT Buy One

  • If you only use your phone for short videos, WeChat, and calling—foldables offer zero benefit and are heavier and thicker instead.
  • If you have small hands or wrist tendonitis—horizontal foldables weigh 230g+ when unfolded, making single-handed use tiring.
  • If you're on a tight budget and plan to keep a phone for 4+ years—foldables have lower resale value than slab phones, and repair costs are high.

III. Buying Guide: Check These 6 Metrics

Don't get dazzled by the word "folding"—focus on these key points when shopping:

  • Hinge Type: Waterdrop hinge (shallower crease, longer lifespan, currently mainstream) is better than U-type hinge (deeper crease, early solution). Ask sales staff or check reviews to confirm which type.
  • Inner Screen Material: Must be UTG ultra-thin glass, not early plastic film. UTG feels close to glass; plastic film feels indented under fingernails and easily retains marks.
  • Crease Performance: Go to a store and unfold it yourself. The 2025 qualified standard: almost invisible head-on with screen on, visible as a shallow groove under angled light, slight depression when finger runs across but not sharp.
  • Cover Screen Size: For horizontal foldables, the larger the outer screen the better (ideally 6.4"+) so it works as a normal phone when closed; for vertical foldables, larger is more practical (4"+ allows replying to messages and viewing navigation).
  • Weight & Thickness: Horizontal foldables at 230-260g unfolded is normal; under 230g is excellent. Closed thickness of 12-14mm; under 12mm is thin. Vertical foldables must fit comfortably in a shirt pocket when closed to qualify.
  • After-Sales & Warranty: Focus on inner screen crack repair cost. Some brands offer free inner screen replacement once in the first year; others charge 2,000-4,000 yuan out-of-pocket. This is the biggest hidden cost of owning a foldable.

In-Store Experience Checklist

  1. Open and close it 10 times yourself—feel if the hinge damping is smooth and if there are any unusual sounds.
  2. Unfold it and look at the crease under angled light, then look at it head-on with the screen on.
  3. Hold it single-handed in unfolded state for 30 seconds—feel if the weight is acceptable.
  4. Try Flex Mode: fold to 90°, place on desk, try taking photos and watching videos.
  5. Ask clearly about the standalone inner screen repair price and warranty policy.

IV. Unique Foldable Usage Tips

After buying one, here are experiences you can't get with a slab phone:

1. Split-Screen & Multitasking

When a horizontal foldable is unfolded, most systems support top/bottom or left/right split-screen. Typical scenarios: upper screen for online course video, lower screen for notes; left side WeChat, right side documents; upper screen map navigation, lower screen music. Much more practical than slab phone split-screens because the screen is large enough that neither half feels cramped.

2. Flex Mode (90° Fold)

This is the "killer feature" usage of foldables:

  • Selfies / Group Photos: Place on desk folded to 90°, upper screen for framing, lower screen for shutter—no need to ask someone else to take the photo.
  • Timelapse / Night Shots: Same stable placement on desk—saves you from needing a tripod.
  • Video Calls: Frees both hands; the other person sees you from a more natural angle too.
  • Watching Videos: Use as a mini TV on a desk—especially practical on airplane/high-speed rail tray tables.
  • Cover Screen Preview + Inner Screen Shooting: When using the rear camera to shoot yourself, the cover screen displays the live preview for more accurate composition.
3. Cover Screen Quick View

When a vertical foldable is closed, the cover screen can: show time/notifications/weather, reply to WeChat (voice-to-text or quick replies), tap transit cards, view navigation, act as a makeup mirror (using front camera). No need to unfold every time—saves power and effort.

4. Desktop Mode

Some horizontal foldables (Huawei, Samsung) can switch to "Desktop Mode" when unfolded. After connecting an external display, keyboard, and mouse, it works like a basic PC—adequate for temporary document processing and email replies. Not a primary productivity tool, but great for emergencies.

V. Maintenance Tips: Help Your Foldable Last Longer

The most delicate parts of a foldable are the inner screen and hinge. These habits can significantly extend its lifespan:

  • Don't Force Open/Close in Low Temperatures: Below 0°C, UTG glass becomes brittle. Warm it in your pocket for a few minutes before opening/closing. This is something written in every official manual but nobody reads.
  • Avoid Nails/Hard Objects Touching the Inner Screen: The inner screen has a protective layer but is softer than the outer screen. Operate with your fingertips, never poke with fingernails.
  • Check for Foreign Objects Before Closing: A single grain of sand caught inside when closing could directly crush the inner screen. Glance over it before closing.
  • Don't Apply Regular Tempered Glass Film: The inner screen can only use official specialized film or UTG protective film. Regular tempered glass film affects folding and touch functionality. Recommend going to official after-sales for screen protector application.
  • Keep Dust & Liquids Away from the Hinge: Although most claim IPX8 water resistance, dust resistance ratings are generally low. Be careful at beaches, construction sites, etc.
  • Apply Even Force When Opening/Closing: Don't violently flick it open with one hand. Opening smoothly with both hands from the center is most stable.

High-Risk Scenarios for Inner Screen Damage

  • Catching headphone/charging cable when closing—directly cracks the inner screen.
  • Mixing with keys in a bag—outer screen fine, but keys press creases into the inner screen.
  • Immediately opening/closing when moving from cold outdoors to warm indoors in winter—temperature difference causes glass stress changes.
  • After a year of use, feeling "the crease got deeper" and trying to peel off the factory film—peeling the film will damage the inner screen along with it.

VI. Foldable "Pitfalls" & Realistic Expectations

Before buying, keep these things in mind:

  • The Crease Will Always Be There: 2025 technology cannot achieve "completely invisible"—only "almost invisible head-on with screen on". If you're a perfectionist who would stare at the crease uncomfortably every day, don't buy.
  • Heavier & Thicker: Horizontal foldables weigh 230g+ when unfolded—more tiring for single-handed use than slab phones. Closed thickness of 12-14mm makes pockets bulge more.
  • Battery Life Is Generally Average: Because space must accommodate the hinge mechanism, battery capacity is compressed. At the same price point, battery life typically falls short of slab phones.
  • Lower Resale Value: After one year, the second-hand price is usually 50-60% of the new phone price—drops faster than slab phones.
  • Expensive Repairs: Inner screen assembly repair costs 2,000-4,000 yuan; hinge repair costs 500-1,500 yuan. Check warranty policies clearly before buying.
  • Inconsistent App Adaptation: Mainstream apps have adapted to large screens, but niche apps may just be "stretched/enlarged" with mediocre experience.

A foldable is not a "better phone"—it's "a phone in a different form factor." Buy it because you need its unique form, not because it's "more advanced."
