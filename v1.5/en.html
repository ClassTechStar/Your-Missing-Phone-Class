Your phone is far more powerful than you might imagine.This section is the foundation for all the "pro moves." Chapter 0: Hi, Before We Begin

Before we dive in, let's go over some basic conventions so everything makes sense. Don't worry—sharpening the axe won't delay the work.

1. What This Book Can Do For You (And What It Can't)

It will help you systematically understand how to use your phone, learn the principles behind features, and most ideas apply to both Android and Apple users.It will guide you through hands-on practice, taking you from "knowing how to use" to "understanding how to use."

It's not a manual for any specific phone, nor a tech news roundup, nor a guide to phone addiction.Honestly, if you're expecting "how to scroll videos more enjoyably," you may be disappointed—we care more about how to make your phone serve you, not the other way around.

2. Our "Code Words" Explained

To be clearer, the book uses some markers—think of them as our inside shorthand:

  • [Button Name]: Represents a specific button or app name on the screen, e.g., [Settings] or [OK].
  • >: Represents an operation sequence, e.g., "tap [Settings] > [Sound]", means first tap Settings, then tap Sound.
  • Bold：Very important! Could be a key concept or safety warning—please pay careful attention.
  • Italic：Supplementary thoughts or analogies to help you understand better.

3. Android and iOS—We Cover Both

You know what, some things have different names but do the same job.For example: "Application" (Android) and "App" (iOS), Settings (same name everywhere), File Manager (Android) and Files (iOS).Just know they're the same thing—don't get lost in terminology.

Additionally, in the Android camp, Huawei has HarmonyOS, which has its own features we'll cover later.In short, no matter what phone you use, this book can help you.

4. Phone Storage: Why Does 128G Show Less Than 110G?

You probably know the units: 1TB = 1024GB, 1GB = 1024MB, 1MB = 1024KB...but here's a catch: hard drive manufacturers use base 1000, while systems use base 1024, so the "128G" storage you buy is actually "smaller."

There are three main reasons your phone storage shrinks:

  1. System itself takes up space：The operating system is like a house's load-bearing walls—it must exist. Modern systems typically take 10-20GB, which is normal.
  2. Pre-installed apps from manufacturers：Pre-installed apps on new phones also take up space; some can be uninstalled, others cannot.
  3. Partitioning and formatting overhead：Technical reasons, like how a backpack's lining takes up volume.

Phone buying tip

Today 128GB phones are basically insufficient; 256GB is a stable starting point.If you like taking photos and gaming, go straight for 512GB or more.Remember, with storage, it's better to have too much than too little.

Hands-On: Check Your Phone's "ID Card"

Open [Settings], find About Phone (or similar), and note: phone model, system version, total storage, and remaining space.This is the first step in understanding it.Also check battery health, to get a sense of it.

Chapter 1: Hello, My Personal Computer

Your phone is actually the most personal supercomputer in your pocket.It's countless times more powerful than the computers that put humans on the moon, yet lies quietly in your palm.Today, let's get to know this "silent giant" properly.

1. Phone Hardware Components: An In-Depth Analysis

Small as it is, a phone has all the essentials. Like an anatomy class, let's examine these precision components one by one.

1. SoC (System on Chip): The Phone's "Brain + Heart"

SoC stands for System on Chip, literally "fit the whole system onto one chip."It's not just a CPU, but integrates CPU, GPU (Graphics Processing Unit), NPU (Neural Processing Unit), ISP (Image Signal Processor), baseband (for communication), and more all together.

It's like cramming a city's government buildings, power plant, communications center, and police station into one building—extremely efficient.

CPU: The Core of General Computing

The CPU is the most core component of the SoC, handling the vast majority of computation tasks.Phone CPUs use ARM architecture, different from computers' x86. ARM features a Reduced Instruction Set (RISC) with lower power consumption, more suitable for mobile devices.

CPU big.LITTLE Architecture：Modern phone CPUs widely adopt a "big.LITTLE" architecture (ARM's big.LITTLE technology), dividing cores into different performance tiers:

  • Prime Core / Performance Core：The strongest performer, handles heavy tasks. Such as game loading, app launching, complex calculations.Highest frequency (3GHz+), but also highest power consumption.
  • Middle Core：Balanced performance and power, handles daily medium-load tasks. Browsing Weibo, watching videos, multitasking switching rely mainly on these.
  • Efficiency Core：Lowest power consumption, handles light background tasks. Standby, music playback, receiving messages—barely drain battery.

The three tiers of cores work in concert: the system scheduler allocates automatically based on task load—light tasks to small cores to save power, heavy tasks to large cores to crunch, then back to small cores.This is why phones can both run games smoothly and last all day on standby.

Architecture Characteristics by Brand：

  • Qualcomm Snapdragon 8 Elite：Uses Oryon self-developed CPU architecture (from Nuvia acquisition), 2 super cores (4.32GHz) + 6 performance cores (3.53GHz), all based on TSMC's second-gen 3nm process.Completely bidding farewell to ARM public architecture, with leapfrog improvements in single-core and multi-core performance.GPU is Adreno 830, with sliced architecture, 40% graphics performance improvement, Hexagon NPU computing power ~45 TOPS, 45% AI inference performance boost.(Note: Samsung Galaxy custom version has higher frequency, with super cores reaching 4.47GHz.)
  • MediaTek Dimensity 9400：1 super core (Cortex-X925, 3.62GHz) + 3 performance cores (Cortex-X4, 3.3GHz) + 4 efficiency cores (Cortex-A720, 2.4GHz), continuing the all-big-core architecture, TSMC second-gen 3nm process.GPU is Immortalis-G925 (12 cores), peak performance up 41%, ray tracing performance up 40%.AI processor is 8th-gen NPU 890, supporting on-device large model execution, on-device LoRA training, and on-device video generation.
  • AppleA18 Pro：2 performance cores (~4.04GHz) + 4 efficiency cores (~2.4GHz), TSMC second-gen 3nm process.6-core GPU supports hardware-level ray tracing, 16-core NPU (~35 TOPS) provides powerful computing for on-device AI and Apple Intelligence.Apple's self-developed architecture still leads in single-core performance and power efficiency, with unified memory architecture providing high-bandwidth advantages for AI inference.
  • Huawei Kirin 9020：Featured in the Mate 70 series, using self-developed Taishan CPU architecture, with continuously enhanced NPU and ISP capabilities, supporting 5.5G and satellite communications—representing the latest breakthrough in domestic chips.
GPU: Graphics Rendering Engine

The GPU (Graphics Processing Unit) specializes in graphics and parallel computing tasks.It has many more cores than CPU (often hundreds to thousands), but each core is relatively simple, suited for processing many similar calculations simultaneously.

Graphics Rendering Pipeline：

  1. Vertex Processing: Calculate the position coordinates of each vertex in a 3D model.
  2. Rasterization: Convert 3D graphics into pixels on a 2D screen.
  3. Pixel Shading: Calculate color, texture, and lighting effects for each pixel.
  4. Output Composition: Composites all layers into the final image and sends it to the screen for display.

GPUImpact on Daily Use：Not just gaming! UI scrolling, animations, video playback, photo filters, AR apps all depend on GPU.Phones with weak GPUs may drop frames when scrolling, and games will lag directly.

GPU by Brand：Qualcomm uses self-developed Adreno GPU; MediaTek uses ARM Mali GPU; Apple uses self-developed GPU, with performance and efficiency as industry benchmarks.

NPU: AI Acceleration Engine

NPU (Neural Processing Unit) is a processor designed specifically for neural network operations.Compared to CPU and GPU, NPU is more efficient and lower power when handling AI tasks.

Neural Network Computation Method：NPU's core operations are matrix multiplication and convolution—the most basic and frequent calculations in neural networks.Through hardware-level optimization, NPU can process many matrix operations in parallel, dozens of times faster than CPU with much lower power.

AIProcessing Workflow：

  1. Data Input: Camera images, microphone audio, sensor data, etc.
  2. Preprocessing: Adjust data format and dimensions to match neural network input requirements.
  3. Inference Computing: The NPU loads the trained model, performs forward computation on the input data, and outputs the result.
  4. Post-Processing: Converts AI output into usable results, such as face coordinates, text content, and scene labels.

Practical Application Scenarios：

  • Computational Photography: Night mode compositing, portrait bokeh, HDR processing, AI beauty enhancement.
  • Voice Assistant: Speech recognition, natural language understanding, speech synthesis.
  • Real-Time Translation: AI subtitles, conversation translation.
  • Smart Recognition: Text recognition (OCR), object recognition, scene recognition.
  • On-Device Large Model：The latest flagship chips support running small AI large models (7B parameter level) on-device, enabling offline AI conversation, document summarization, smart writing, and more.Snapdragon 8 Elite's Hexagon NPU computing power is ~45 TOPS, Dimensity 9400's NPU 890 also supports on-device large models (LLM inference speed up 80%), Apple A18 Pro's 16-core NPU provides ~35 TOPS.
  • AI Image Processing: AI Erase (remove passersby from photos with one tap), AI Expand (expand image boundaries), AI Style Transfer, text-to-image generation, and more.
ISP: Image Signal Processor

The ISP processes raw data from the camera, turning light signals into beautiful photos.

How to Judge Performance?Look at the model! Apple A18 Pro, Qualcomm Snapdragon 8 Elite, MediaTek Dimensity 9400, Huawei Kirin 9020 are all flagship tier.For mid-range, look at Snapdragon 8s Gen3/7+ Gen3, Dimensity 8400.Don't just look at core count—architecture and process node (nanometer) matter more.3nm is currently the most advanced, with much better power efficiency than 5nm and 4nm.

2. RAM (Random Access Memory): The Temporary Workbench

RAM is like your office desk—the larger it is, the more files you can spread out at once.12GB is now mainstream, 16GB suits heavy users, 24GB+ is for gamers and geeks.

RAM type also matters: LPDDR5 is faster and more power-efficient than LPDDR4X.Although Apple's RAM is not large, system optimization is good, so it appears "sufficient."

What Happens When RAM Is Insufficient?Apps get killed in the background, requiring reload on switch.This is why sometimes when you switch to WeChat for a moment, your game is gone.

3. Storage (ROM/Flash): The Long-Term Warehouse

Storage is like your bookshelf, where all photos, videos, and apps live.Current mainstream is UFS 3.1 or UFS 4.0, several times faster than the older eMMC.

Why Is Storage Speed Important?It determines app launch speed, game loading speed, and photo saving speed.Over time, slow storage will make your phone increasingly laggy.

4. Screen: Your Eyes

The screen is the main window for you and your phone to interact—quality directly affects experience.

  • Material: OLED has vivid colors, high contrast, and better power efficiency (pixels don't emit light when displaying black), but may cause eye strain from flickering at low brightness; LCD is more eye-friendly and cheaper, but has lower contrast and color vibrancy compared to OLED.
  • Resolution: 1080P is mainstream, 2K is clearer but more power-hungry.Apple's "Retina display" concept means: at a certain distance, the human eye can't distinguish individual pixels.
  • Refresh Rate: 60Hz is standard, 90Hz/120Hz is smoother, like silk when scrolling. High refresh rate drains battery, so many phones support adaptive refresh rate (LTPO), adjusting dynamically based on content.
  • Touch Sampling Rate: Affects touch response speed—gamers should pay attention; the higher, the more responsive.
  • Dimming Method: DC dimming is more eye-friendly than PWM dimming; eyes tire less easily at low brightness. Many phones now support high-frequency PWM dimming, also for eye protection.
Screen HDR Technology

HDR (High Dynamic Range) lets the screen display brighter highlights and deeper shadows simultaneously, dramatically improving image depth.

  • Implementation Principle: Standard screens have a limited brightness range (SDR), so the gap between the brightest and darkest isn't wide enough. HDR screens have higher peak brightness (typically 1000 nits+), greater contrast, and wider color gamut, showing more detail.
  • Mainstream Standards: HDR10 (most basic, free and open-source), HDR10+ (Samsung-driven, dynamic metadata), Dolby Vision (best quality but requires licensing). Which standard a phone supports depends on the screen hardware and system licensing.
  • Actual Effect: When watching HDR video, sunlight is more glaring, shadows are deeper, and colors are richer. The prerequisite is that the video itself is HDR-produced and the screen supports HDR display.
Imaging HDR Technology

Photo HDR and screen HDR are two different things. Image HDR uses algorithms to solve the problem of "blown-out highlights and crushed shadows."

  • Algorithm Principle: When you press the shutter, the phone actually takes multiple photos at different exposures (underexposed to preserve highlight details, overexposed to preserve shadow details, normal exposure for midtones), then synthesizes them into a single high dynamic range photo.
  • Processing Workflow: Multi-frame capture → Alignment Compensation (Anti-Shake) → Exposure Fusion / Tone Mapping → Color Correction → Output the final photo.
  • Effect Optimization：Phones can now achieve "real-time HDR"—what you see in the viewfinder is already the HDR-processed result. WYSIWYG. Apple's Smart HDR and Android's auto HDR are both very mature.
5. Battery and Charging: The Source of Energy

The battery is the most "short-lived" component of a phone; after two or three years it noticeably degrades.Current mainstream capacity is 4500-5500mAh.

  • Charging Power: The Android camp has raced to 100W or even 200W, fully charging in just over ten minutes; Apple still lingers around 20-30W. Does fast charging damage the battery? Not much with original accessories—phones have temperature control protection.
  • Wireless Charging: Convenient but low efficiency and generates significant heat. Good for casual office charging, not ideal when you need to rush out.
  • Reverse Charging: Phone becomes a power bank, charging earbuds and watches for emergencies.
6. Camera: The Magic of Light and Shadow

Modern phones often have three or four cameras, each with its own role:

  • Main camera: The most commonly used, with the largest sensor and best image quality.
  • Ultra-wide angle: Shoot landscapes and architecture with a wider field of view.
  • Telephoto: Shooting distant subjects, there are Optical Zoom and Digital Zoom.
  • Macro: Shoot very close-up small objects.

Megapixels Aren't Everything!Sensor size (a bigger sensor is decisively better), aperture size (f/1.8 is larger than f/2.4), and stabilization (OIS optical stabilization is important) all affect image quality.Computational photography (AI algorithms) is increasingly important—it can combine multiple photos into one great shot.

Periscope Telephoto Lens

Traditional telephoto lenses require a long optical path, which would make the phone thick.Periscope telephoto uses a 90-degree prism to refract light, allowing the optical path to spread horizontally inside the phone, achieving 5x or even 10x optical zoom while keeping the phone slim.

  • Structural Principle: Light enters from the lens → Prism refracts 90° → Passes through lens group along the phone's width → reaches the sensor. Equivalent to placing a vertical telescope horizontally.
  • Optical Characteristics：High optical zoom multiples (5x-10x), image quality far exceeds digital zoom. But the sensor is usually smaller than the main camera, so low-light performance is average.
  • Imaging Advantages：Shooting the moon, distant buildings, and stage close-ups—beyond what ordinary phones can do.
OIS Optical Image Stabilization vs EIS Electronic Image Stabilization

Stabilization is a key technology affecting photo and video quality, with two completely different principles:

  • OIS (Optical Image Stabilization): Physically moves the lens or sensor to counteract hand shake. Inside the lens, a micro gyroscope detects the direction and magnitude of shake, then drives motors to move the lens group in the opposite direction. The advantage is no loss of image quality; the disadvantage is high cost and space requirements.
  • EIS (Electronic Image Stabilization): Uses software algorithms to crop and shift the frame to counteract shake. The phone records a slightly larger frame range, then crops out a stable area based on gyroscope data. The advantage is that no extra hardware is needed; the disadvantage is that it crops the image (narrowing the field of view) and may cause blur in low light.
  • Actual Choice: For photos, prioritize OIS; for video, combining both works best. Many flagship phones now support "Sensor-Shift" sensor-shift stabilization, which is more effective than traditional lens stabilization.
Optical Zoom vs Digital Zoom
  • Optical Zoom: Changes focal length by physically moving the lens group—no loss in image quality. Phone optical zoom is usually at fixed multiples (e.g., 2x, 3x, 5x, 10x), not continuously variable like a dedicated camera.
  • Digital Zoom: Enlarges the center area of the frame—essentially cropping plus interpolation enlargement. The more you zoom, the worse the quality. 100x digital zoom looks cool, but the actual image quality is already very blurry.
  • Hybrid Zoom: Combines optical and digital zoom, using multi-frame synthesis algorithms to compensate for image quality loss between optical zoom levels. Better than pure digital, but still not as good as optical.
Audio: Speakers and Dedicated Display Chip

Single Speaker vs Dual Speakers：

  • Single Speaker: Only one sound unit at the bottom; sound leans to one side, no stereo feel. Common in mid-to-low-end phones.
  • Dual Speakers (Stereo): One speaker at the top and one at the bottom, forming a stereo soundstage. Clear left-right channel separation when watching videos or listening to music, much more immersive. Using the earpiece as the top speaker is a common solution.
  • Sound Quality Differences: Dual speakers aren't just louder—more importantly, they provide spatial sense and layering. Watching a blockbuster with a single speaker always feels like something is missing.

Gaming phone dedicated display chip：

Some gaming phones and performance phones are equipped with dedicated display processing chips (such as Redmi's "Wild Engine" display chip and iQOO's self-developed display chip). Their functions include:

  • Frame interpolation: Uses algorithms to interpolate 30fps game footage to 60fps or 120fps, making visuals smoother. This process runs on the dedicated display chip, not consuming SoC GPU resources.
  • Super resolution: Uses AI algorithms to upscale low-resolution images to high resolution for clearer picture quality.
  • Reduce Power Consumption: Because frame interpolation and super resolution are handled by the dedicated display chip, the SoC load decreases—heat and power consumption drop, and gaming becomes more stable.
GPS Positioning: Single-Band and Dual-Band

Phone positioning relies primarily on GPS and other satellite navigation systems.

  • Single-frequency GPS: Only receives L1 band signals, with positioning accuracy of about 3-5 meters. Sufficient in open areas, but in dense urban canyons, signal reflections cause significant positioning drift.
  • Dual-frequency GPS: Receives L1 and L5 band signals simultaneously. The L5 band has a longer wavelength and stronger anti-interference capability. Dual-frequency joint solving can eliminate most ionospheric errors, achieving positioning accuracy of 1-3 meters. The advantage is clear for city navigation and ride-hailing positioning.
  • Multi-Constellation Support: Modern phones support not only GPS but also BeiDou (China), GLONASS (Russia), Galileo (Europe), and other satellite systems. The more satellites connected simultaneously, the faster and more accurate the positioning.
7. Sensors: The Phone's Little Antennas

Phones are packed with over a dozen sensors working silently:

  • Accelerometer + Gyroscope: Senses phone orientation; used for game steering and auto screen rotation.
  • Ambient Light Sensor: Automatically adjusts screen brightness.
  • Proximity Sensor: Screen turns off automatically when the phone is near your ear during a call.
  • Fingerprint Sensor: The mainstream now is under-display optical or ultrasonic fingerprint.
  • Facial Recognition: Apple's Face ID uses 3D structured light with high security; many Android phones use 2D photo recognition with average security.
  • NFC: Near-field communication, tap for transit cards, access cards, bank cards.
  • Barometer: Measures altitude, even detects when you climb stairs.
  • Hall Sensor: Detects the opening/closing of the magnetic case.

2. Operating Systems: Android VS iOS VS HarmonyOS

Hardware is the body; the operating system is the soul. The three major systems each have their own character:

  • Android: Like an open-source community—phone makers can deeply customize, offering many choices. You can change themes, install third-party app stores, and tinker with various tricks. But because of its openness, fragmentation is severe, and the experience varies significantly between brands.
  • iOS: Like a carefully tended garden—Apple controls both software and hardware, providing a unified and smooth experience. High app quality and good privacy protection, but low freedom—many things must follow Apple's rules.
  • HarmonyOS: Huawei's new species focused on distributed capabilities, enabling different devices to work together seamlessly. We'll cover this in detail later.

All three have incredibly rich ecosystems; just pick the lifestyle you like. There's no absolute right or wrong—only what fits you.

3. Complete Analysis of Phone Interfaces

Phone interfaces are bridges to the external world; different interfaces vary significantly:

Micro USB
  • Physical Characteristics: Flat trapezoidal connector with distinct front/back (won't insert the wrong way). Basically obsolete on phones, but some low-end and old devices still use it.
  • Transfer Limits: Only supports USB 2.0 standard, with a maximum transfer rate of 480Mbps and charging power typically not exceeding 10W.
  • Disadvantages: Non-reversible, short insertion lifespan, slow transfer speeds, and low charging power.
Lightning (Apple's connector, discontinued)
  • Physical Characteristics: Apple's proprietary 8-pin connector, reversible. Used in iPhone 14 and earlier, now fully discontinued.
  • Transfer Limits: The Lightning connector physically only supports the USB 2.0 standard with a maximum transfer rate of 480Mbps. Even with a USB-C to Lightning cable, transfer speed is limited by the Lightning connector itself.
  • Reasons for Discontinuation: The EU passed regulations requiring all phones to use USB-C starting 2024. Apple fully switched to Type-C starting with the iPhone 15 series. The Lightning connector officially exits the stage of history.
  • Note: Apple MFi-certified cables contain authentication chips; non-certified cables may not charge or transfer data properly. If you're still using an older iPhone, it's recommended to keep Lightning cables handy.
USB Type-C
  • Physical Characteristics: A 24-pin oval connector that can be inserted either way. It has become the standard interface for phones and most electronic devices. The EU has legislated that all phones, tablets, earphones, and other portable devices must use USB-C starting 2024, with other regions worldwide following suit.
  • Transfer Protocol: Type-C is just the connector shape; actual transfer capability depends on the internal protocol. Supports USB 2.0 (480Mbps), USB 3.0/3.1/3.2 (5-20Gbps), and even USB4 (40Gbps).
  • Important Distinction: Not all Type-C interfaces support USB 3.0! Many phones use Type-C connectors but only have USB 2.0 pins internally, with the same slow transfer speed as the old connector. iPhone 15/16 standard models only support USB 2.0 speed; only the Pro version supports USB 3.0 (requires a USB-C to USB-C cable).
  • Charging Capability: Supports fast charging protocols like USB PD, up to 240W charging.
  • Video Output: Supports DisplayPort Alt Mode, can output video to a monitor via adapter cable.
3.5mm Audio Jack
  • Physical Characteristics: Round 3-segment or 4-segment jack; 3-segment transmits left/right audio channels, 4-segment adds a microphone channel.
  • Features: Analog signal transmission—plug and play, no drivers or conversion needed. Stable sound quality, zero latency.
  • Current status: Most flagship phones have removed the 3.5mm jack, requiring Type-C adapters or Bluetooth earphones. Some mid-range phones and gaming phones still have it.

5. Baseband and Antenna: The Unsung Heroes of Mobile Communication

A phone's ability to make calls and go online depends on the baseband chip and antenna duo.

Baseband Chip:

The baseband is the processor responsible for wireless communication in phones. It modulates digital signals into radio waves for transmission and demodulates received radio waves back into digital signals.Baseband performance directly affects call quality, internet speed, and signal stability.Qualcomm is the largest phone baseband supplier; MediaTek and Samsung also have self-developed basebands.Apple began using its self-developed C1 baseband chip starting with iPhone 16e, marking Apple's official entry into the self-developed baseband era. The C1 baseband uses a 4nm process with excellent power efficiency; iPhone 16/16 Plus/16 Pro/16 Pro Max still use Qualcomm basebands (X71, etc.), with subsequent models expected to gradually transition to self-developed basebands.Earlier iPhone XS/11 series used Intel basebands with poor signal performance—a pain point for many users.

Antenna Design:

Phone antennas are hidden inside the body, usually arranged around the frame. Antenna design directly affects signal reception performance:

  • MIMO technology: Multiple Input Multiple Output—uses multiple antennas to simultaneously send and receive data, improving speed and stability. 4×4 MIMO is faster than 2×2 MIMO.
  • Number of Antennas: Flagship phones typically have over a dozen antennas (cellular, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, NFC, and GPS each require separate antennas).
  • Impact of Metal Body: Metal shields signals, so metal-body phones need antenna bands (plastic strips on the frame). Glass and plastic bodies have less impact on signals.
Network Technology Evolution:
  • 4G LTE: Theoretical peak speeds of 1Gbps down/500Mbps up (Cat.18); real-world experience is typically 50-150Mbps. Widest coverage, good enough.
  • 5G NR: Theoretical peak rate of 10Gbps+ downlink. Sub-6GHz band offers good coverage; mmWave (millimeter wave) is extremely fast but has very limited coverage.
  • 5G-A（5G Advanced）: The enhanced version of 5G, also called 5.5G. Downlink speeds up to 10Gbps, uplink 1Gbps, with significantly improved connection density and positioning accuracy. Since 2024, China Mobile, China Telecom, and China Unicom have started deploying 5G-A networks in key cities. Flagship phones like Huawei Mate 70 series and Xiaomi 15 series already support 5G-A. 5G-A also brings "integrated sensing and communication" capability, where base stations can simultaneously communicate and sense the environment, providing infrastructure for low-altitude economy (drone delivery) and vehicle-to-everything (V2X).
Network Band Classification:

The network bands a phone supports determine which countries and carriers' networks it can use:

  • FDD-LTE Band (B series): Such as B1, B3, B5, etc., 4G frequency bands. Different countries use different frequency bands. China mainly uses B1/B3/B5/B8/B34/B38/B39/B40/B41.
  • TDD-LTE Band: Another 4G standard, widely used in China on bands B38/B39/B40/B41.
  • 5G NR Band (N series): Such as n1, n3, n28, n41, n78, n79, etc. n78 (3.5GHz) is China's main 5G frequency band, n41 (2.5GHz) is used more by China Mobile.
  • Purchase Advice: If you need to use it abroad, verify before buying that the phone supports the target country's bands. China-version phones usually have the most complete support for Chinese bands but may lack some overseas bands.

3. Bluetooth: The Versatile Player in Short-Range Wireless Communication

Bluetooth is probably one of the most frequently used wireless technologies on your phone—wireless earbuds, smartwatches, car systems, keyboards, and mice all rely on it for connection.

Technical Principle:

Bluetooth operates in the 2.4GHz ISM band, using Frequency Hopping Spread Spectrum (FHSS) technology to distribute data across 79 channels with rapid switching transmission, effectively avoiding interference. Bluetooth communication range is divided into three classes: Class 1 approximately 100 meters, Class 2 approximately 10 meters (commonly used by phones), and Class 3 approximately 1 meter.

Version Evolution:
  • Bluetooth 4.0: Introduced Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE), enabling small-battery devices like fitness bands and heart rate monitors to connect wirelessly, extending battery life from days to months.
  • Bluetooth 4.2: Improves transfer speed and Privacy Protection, supports IPv6 direct connection.
  • Bluetooth 5.0: Double the transfer speed (2Mbps), quadruple the range (theoretical 300m), and 8x the broadcasting capacity. Supports dual audio (connecting two pairs of earbuds simultaneously).
  • Bluetooth 5.1: Introduces direction finding (AoA/AoD), positioning accuracy up to centimeter-level.
  • Bluetooth 5.2: The new LE Audio standard supports LC3 encoding (better sound quality, lower power consumption), multi-stream audio, and broadcast audio.
  • Bluetooth 5.3/5.4: Further optimizes power consumption and connection stability, with enhanced periodic broadcasting.

LE Audio Adoption Status: In 2024-2025, LE Audio has entered a phase of rapid adoption. Mainstream brands' new true wireless earbuds (such as Huawei FreeBuds Pro 4, Xiaomi Buds 5 Pro, Samsung Galaxy Buds3, etc.) all support LE Audio and LC3 codec, providing lower latency and better sound quality. When buying new earbuds, it's recommended to prioritize products that support LE Audio.

Application Scenarios:
  • Audio Transmission: Wireless earbuds, car audio systems. Bluetooth 5.2’s LE Audio delivers a quantum leap in both sound quality and latency.
  • Data Transfer: Transfer files and photos at close range. Though slower than Wi-Fi Direct, it wins in simplicity.
  • Device Pairing: Smart Watch, fitness band, keyboard & mouse, game controller.
  • Location Services: Bluetooth beacons used for indoor navigation, item tracking (like Apple AirTag).
  • Smart Home: Bluetooth Mesh network controls lights, door locks, etc.

4. IR Remote Control: Phone Becomes a Universal Remote

Infrared remote control is one of the oldest wireless control technologies, but it remains practical on phones. Some Android phones (such as certain Xiaomi and Huawei models) have built-in IR emitters that can control TVs, air conditioners, set-top boxes, and other home appliances.

Working Principle:

Infrared remote control works by emitting infrared light pulse signals at a specific frequency (usually 38kHz) through an IR LED. The appliance's IR receiver decodes these pulses to execute corresponding operations. Different brands use different encoding protocols, including common ones like NEC, RC5, RC6, and SONY. The IR app in the phone includes encoding libraries for various brands—simply select the brand and model to match.

Limitations:
  • Directionality: Infrared travels in straight lines; you need to point it at the device, and it can't pass through walls.
  • Short Distance: Effective distance is generally 5-8 meters.
  • Apple Doesn't Support: iPhone has no IR blaster, so this feature is unavailable.

5. NFC: The Magic of a Tap

NFC (Near Field Communication) is an extremely short-range (within 10cm) high-frequency wireless communication technology operating at 13.56MHz with a maximum transfer rate of 424Kbps.

Communication Principles and Modes:

NFC evolved from RFID technology and supports three operating modes:

  • Read/Write Mode: Phone reads information from NFC tags, such as triggering actions by reading NFC stickers.
  • Peer-to-Peer Mode: Two NFC devices transfer data to each other, like Android Beam (discontinued).
  • Card Emulation Mode: The phone emulates a smart card—this is the most commonly used mode. Transit cards, access cards, and bank cards all use this mode.
Security Mechanism:
  • Ultra-Short Distance: The communication distance within 10cm is itself a physical security guarantee—remote eavesdropping is virtually impossible.
  • Security Chip: The phone has a built-in dedicated Secure Element (SE) chip or uses HCE (Host Card Emulation), with payment keys stored in a secure area that cannot be extracted even if the phone is rooted.
  • Fingerprint/Face Verification: Requires biometric confirmation when paying, so even if your phone is lost, fraudulent payments cannot be made.
  • Tokenization: Apple Pay, Huawei Pay, etc. do not transmit actual card numbers but use one-time tokens that cannot be reused even if intercepted.
Typical Applications:
  • Transit Card/Access Card: Write physical card info to your phone, just tap your phone to use.
  • Mobile Payment: Apple Pay, HuaweiPay, etc. Bind your bank card to your phone and tap to pay.
  • Device Pairing: Tap via NFC to quickly pair Bluetooth earphones and speakers.
  • Authentication: NFC ID card reading, digital driver's license verification.

V. Components of a Software Installation Package

Have you ever wondered what's actually inside the installation package you download from the app store? Understanding the composition of an installation package helps you better understand how apps work and know where to investigate when problems arise.

1. Android APK File Structure

An APK (Android Package Kit) is essentially a ZIP archive. After decompression, you can see the following core table of contents and files:

  • AndroidManifest.xml: The app's "ID card" and "instruction manual." It records core information such as the app package name, version number, required permissions, supported system versions, and entry Activity. The system first reads this file when installing an app.
  • classes.dex: The app's core code. Java/Kotlin source code is compiled into Dalvik bytecode and stored in this file. An app may have multiple dex files (e.g., classes2.dex, classes3.dex).
  • resources.arsc: The compiled resource index table. Records the IDs and corresponding relationships of all resources.
  • res/Table of Contents: Resources folder. Contains images, layout files, strings, color definitions, etc. Images for different resolutions are placed in different drawable folders (e.g., drawable-hdpi, drawable-xhdpi).
  • assets/Table of Contents: Raw resources folder. Stores resources that are not compiled, such as web files, fonts, audio, etc. Apps can read these at runtime through AssetManager.
  • lib/Table of Contents: Native library folder. Stores compiled .so files (C/C++ code compilation output), categorized by CPU architecture (e.g., arm64-v8a, armeabi-v7a). Games and apps requiring high-performance computing usually have this directory.
  • META-INF/Table of Contents: Signature information folder. Contains three files—MANIFEST.MF, CERT.SF, and CERT.RSA—used to verify the integrity and signature of the APK.
2. iOS IPA File Structure

IPA (iOS App Store Package) is also a compressed package with a slightly different structure:

  • Payload/Table of Contents: Where the core content resides. Inside is a .app folder containing all the application's contents.
  • Info.plist: The app's configuration file, similar to Android's AndroidManifest.xml. Records the app ID, version, permission requirements, supported devices, etc.
  • Executable File: The main executable with the same name as the .app folder, a compiled binary in Mach-O format.
  • Frameworks/Table of Contents: Dynamic libraries and frameworks that apps depend on.
  • Resource File: Resources like Image, Audio, Video are usually stored in the .app folder.
  • _CodeSignature/Table of Contents: Code signing information, used to verify app integrity and origin.
3. Signature Verification Mechanism

Why are apps downloaded from official stores safer? Signature verification is the key.

  • Android Signature: Developers sign the APK with a private key, and the system verifies it with the public key during installation. If the APK has been tampered with (e.g., infected with a virus), the signature becomes invalid and the system refuses to install it. Google Play also requires App Signing, with Google safeguarding the signing key to further protect app security.
  • iOS signing: Apple's review process is stricter. Every IPA must be signed with a certificate issued by Apple, or it cannot be installed. This is why iOS can only install apps from the App Store or via enterprise certificates. Apps signed with enterprise certificates have limited validity periods, and Apple can revoke them at any time.
  • Purpose of Signatures：
    1. Verify source: Confirm the app actually comes from the claimed developer.
    2. Verify integrity: ensure the application has not been tampered with during transmission.
    3. Prevent impersonation: Malicious apps cannot disguise themselves as other apps.
4. Package Size and Optimization

You may notice that the app size shown in the app store differs from the space it occupies after installation. This is because:

  • Download Size: Compressed APK/IPA size.
  • Installation Size: Size after extraction, usually much larger than the Download Size.
  • Runtime Footprint: Apps also generate cache and data files while running, taking up more space.

Modern App Optimization Techniques:

  • App Bundle (Android): Google Play's dynamic delivery technology. When users download, they only get resources suitable for their device (e.g., only images matching their screen resolution), greatly reducing download size.
  • App Thinning（iOS）: Similar technology—Apple automatically strips unnecessary resources based on the device.
  • On-Demand Download: Large apps like games can download the core package first and then download resource packs later.

Tips: Why are some App Install packages particularly large?

Gaming apps are usually the largest because they contain large amounts of high-definition textures, 3D models, and audio files. Chapter 11 Supplement: Professional Imaging Tech Knowledge

To truly understand the mysteries of phone photography and videography, one must know professional imaging techniques. This knowledge not only helps you better use your phone camera but also gives you better judgment when choosing devices.

1. Resolution, Frame Rate, and Bitrate: The Three Pillars of Video Quality

Resolution（Resolution）：

Resolution determines image clarity, expressed in pixel count.

  • 720P (HD)(HD): 1280x720 pixels, entry-level clarity.
  • 1080P (Full HD)(Full HD): 1920x1080 pixels, currently the most mainstream resolution.
  • 2K (QHD)(Quad HD): 2560×1440 pixels, clearer than 1080P.
  • 4K (UHD)(Ultra HD): 3840x2160 pixels, ultra-high definition with rich detail.
  • 8K：7680×4320pixels, a future standard with limited device support today.

The higher the resolution, the clearer the image—but the larger the file, and the greater the demands on storage and processing.

Frame Rate:

Frame rate determines visual smoothness, measured in frames per second (fps).

  • 24fps: Cinema standard frame rate with a distinctive "cinematic feel."
  • 30fps: Commonly used for TV and online videos, smooth and natural.
  • 60fps: High frame rate, suitable for sports and game recording, very smooth.
  • 120fps/240fps: Ultra-high frame rate, usable for slow-motion video.

The higher the frame rate, the smoother the footage and the better the slow-motion effect—but the larger the file.

Bitrate:

Bitrate determines the density of video data, expressed as the amount of data per second (Mbps, megabits per second).

  • The higher the bitrate, the better the image quality and the more detail is preserved—but the larger the file.
  • Bitrate too low causes mosaic, color blocks, and other compression artifacts in the image.
  • Professional video shooting often requires high bitrates to leave more room for post-processing.

2. Video Encoding Format: H.264, H.265, and ProRes

H.264 (AVC) (Advanced Video Coding):
  • Features: The most widely used encoding format with excellent compatibility.
  • Pros: Can be played on almost all devices, moderate compression efficiency.
  • Disadvantages: Newer Format, lower Compression efficiency, larger file size.
  • Applicable Scenarios: Online video sharing, everyday casual shooting.
H.265 (HEVC) (High Efficiency Video Coding):
  • Features: An upgrade to H.264, compression efficiency improved by about 50%.
  • Pros: Smaller file size at the same quality, supports higher resolution and frame rate.
  • Disadvantages: Encoding and decoding require a more powerful processor; some older devices are incompatible.
  • Applicable Scenarios: 4K Video recording, scenarios with limited Storage Space.
Apple ProRes (Apple Professional Encoding):
  • Features: Professional Video codec developed by Apple, near-lossless Compression.
  • Pros: Extremely high image quality, large Post-Processing room, accurate colors.
  • Disadvantages: Files are huge, requiring more storage space and professional equipment to process.
  • Applicable Scenarios: Professional video production, cinema-grade shooting.
ProRes RAW (Raw Professional Encoding):
  • Features: Combines the strengths of ProRes and RAW formats.
  • What is RAW (Raw Format): Unprocessed raw sensor data, preserving maximum information.
  • Pros: Offers enormous post-processing flexibility—you can adjust exposure, white balance, and other parameters without degrading image quality.
  • Disadvantages: Files are extremely large, requiring professional post-processing software.
  • Applicable Scenarios: High-end professional production. The iPhone 15 Pro series supports recording Apple ProRes video (up to 4K 60fps), however the ProRes RAW format currently requires external recording equipment and direct capture on the phone is not yet supported.

3. HDR: High Dynamic Range Technology

HDR (High Dynamic Range) technology preserves details in both the brightest and darkest parts of an image simultaneously.

  • HDR10: The baseline HDR standard with good Compatibility.
  • HDR10+: Samsung's standard, supports dynamic metadata.
  • Dolby Vision(Dolby Vision): Top-tier HDR standard developed by Dolby, best image quality, but requires licensing.
  • HLG(Hybrid Log-Gamma): Broadcast-grade HDR standard, compatible with regular displays.

When shooting HDR video, an HDR-capable display is needed to see the full effect.

4. Color Space and Color Depth

Color Space:
  • sRGB(Standard RGB): Standard color space, best Compatibility.
  • Display P3: Wide color gamut standard promoted by Apple, richer colors.
  • Rec.2020: Standard color space for HDR video with an extremely wide color gamut.
Color depth:
  • 8-bit(8-bit): 16.7 million colors, the standard for common Video.
  • 10-bit(10-bit): 1.07 billion colors, professional video standard, smoother color transitions.
  • 12-bit(12-bit): 68 billion colors, high-end professional standard.

Practical Discussion

  • Daily shooting: 1080P/4K + 30fps + H.265 encoding, balancing quality and file size.
  • Sports shooting: 1080P/4K + 60fps, ensuring smoothness.
  • Professional creation: 4K + ProRes (if supported), preserving maximum post-production flexibility.
  • Storage advice: high-bitrate videos take up a lot of space; it's recommended to back them up regularly to a computer or cloud drive.

Chapter 15: Battery Science and Optimization

The battery is your phone's "energy heart." This chapter helps you understand the battery's little temperaments, learn scientific charging and battery life skills, and completely bid farewell to battery anxiety.

1. What's Up with Batteries? Cycles and Health

A phone battery (lithium-ion or lithium-polymer) is like an energy sponge: charging is absorbing water (storing energy), discharging is squeezing water out (using energy). Remember its three key characteristics:

  • Charge Anytime: Unlike old batteries, it has no memory effect—you don’t have to drain it completely before charging.
  • Afraid of heat: High temperature is the battery's number one killer.
  • Will Degrade Over Time: Over time, maximum capacity decreases; this is normal.
Key Metrics: Cycle Count and Health
  • One cycle is not equal to one charging session. For example, using 60% today and recharging, then 40% tomorrow and recharging—the total of 100% consumption counts as one cycle.
  • Battery Health is the current maximum capacity as a percentage of its brand-new capacity. When it drops below 80%, you'll noticeably feel shorter battery life.
  • How to view? iPhone: Settings > Battery > Battery Health & Charging; Android: Generally search for Battery Health in Settings.

2. The Mystery of Charging? Fast Charging, Wireless Charging, and the Golden Rules

  • Does Fast Charging Harm the Battery? Using original or certified fast charging accessories causes minimal damage. Phones and chargers have temperature control and will automatically reduce power if they overheat.
  • Wireless Charging: Convenient but less efficient, with more noticeable heat. Suitable for overnight charging.
  • Golden Health Rules: Try to keep battery level between 20%-80%; don't let it starve (0%) or overstuff (100%).

III. Fast Charging Protocols Full Analysis: Why Doesn't Your Charger Charge Fast?

You may have encountered this situation: your phone clearly supports fast charging, but it becomes very slow when you switch chargers. This is because fast charging isn't simply about "higher power"—the charger, cable, and phone all need to "speak the same language"—this is what fast charging protocols are about.

1. Basic Principles of Fast Charging

Charging Power = Voltage × Current (P = U × I). To increase power, you either raise the voltage, raise the current, or both. Different protocols have chosen different technical approaches.

Fast Charging Workflow:

  1. Handshake Stage: The phone and charger communicate via the data cable to exchange information about their supported protocols and power capabilities.
  2. Negotiation Phase: Both sides determine a commonly supported maximum power level.
  3. Charging Stages: The charger outputs at the negotiated voltage and current, while the phone handles power management.
  4. Adjustment Stage: As the battery level increases, the phone gradually reduces charging power to protect the battery.
2. Mainstream Fast Charging Protocols Explained

USB PD（Power Delivery）— The King of Universal Protocols

  • Features: The official standard established by the USB-IF organization, with the broadest coverage. PD 3.0 supports up to 100W (20V/5A), and PD 3.1 extends to 240W (48V/5A).
  • Voltage Levels: 5V, 9V, 15V, 20V. PD 3.1 also supports 28V, 36V, and 48V.
  • Compatibility: All iPhone models, Google Pixel, some flagship Android phones, laptops, and Nintendo Switch all support the PD protocol.
  • Advantages: Highly versatile—one charger can charge multiple types of devices; supports PPS (Programmable Power Supply) for fine-grained voltage and current adjustment.
  • Disadvantages: Requires a Type-C port and a dedicated chip, at higher cost.

Qualcomm QC (Quick Charge)— A Veteran of the Android Camp

  • QC 3.0: Supports adjustable voltage from 3.6V-20V in 0.2V steps, up to 18W.
  • QC 4.0/4+: Compatible with USB PD protocol, supports up to 27W, with dual-path charging technology.
  • QC 5.0: Supports up to 100W+, 0-50% in just 5 minutes, significantly reducing heat.
  • Compatibility: Phones with Qualcomm Snapdragon processors mostly support it, but brands typically prioritize their own proprietary protocols.

OPPO VOOC/OnePlus WARP/Realme Dart— The Representative of Low Voltage, High Current

  • Technical Approach: Low Voltage, High Current (starting from 5V/4A), less heat, higher efficiency.
  • VOOC 1.0: 20W (5V/4A), released in 2014, pioneered the fast charging era.
  • SuperVOOC: 50W-65W, using dual-cell series design.
  • SuperVOOC 2.0: 65W, fully charges a 4000mAh battery in 30 minutes.
  • 150W/240W Super Flash Charge: Currently the fastest mass-produced fast charging technology.
  • Features: Requires a dedicated charger and cable (with extra contact pins) and is not compatible with other protocols.

HuaweiSuperCharge— The Evolution of High-Voltage Fast Charging

  • FCP（Fast Charge Protocol）: 9V/2A, 18W, compatible with QC 2.0.
  • SCP（Super Charge Protocol）: Supports higher Power, from 22.5W to 66W and then to 100W.
  • Technical Features: Uses charge pump technology to convert high voltage into low-voltage, high-current input for the battery, with efficiency up to 98%.
  • Compatibility: Exclusive to Huawei/Honor phones, some chargers support PD protocol.

Xiaomi Surge Fast Charging— The Rise of Proprietary Protocols

  • Surge P1 Chip: Xiaomi's self-developed fast charging management chip, supporting 120W wired fast charging.
  • Surge G1 Chip: Battery management chip, works with P1 for more efficient fast charging.
  • Technical Features: Uses a dual-cell series + charge pump design, with broad protocol compatibility.

Apple Fast Charge— Conservative but Stable

  • Protocol: Only supports the USB PD protocol.
  • Power: iPhone 8 and later support fast charging, up to about 27W (iPhone 15 Pro Max can reach 27W+).
  • Features: Relatively conservative in power, but with well-optimized charging curves and excellent heat control.
  • Accessory Requirements: Requires a USB-C to Lightning cable (or USB-C to USB-C cable) and a PD charger.

4. Wireless ChargingProtocol

Wireless charging also has its own protocol standards:

  • Qi standard: Established by WPC (Wireless Charging Consortium), the most common Standard. Basic Qi supports 5W-15W.
  • Qi2 standard: Released in 2023, introduced magnetic positioning (similar to Apple MagSafe), supports 15W+.
  • Private Protocol: Each brand has its own high-speed wireless charging, such as Xiaomi 50W wireless charging, Huawei 50W wireless charging, etc., requiring dedicated chargers.
5. Fast Charging Buying Guide
  • Check Protocol Matching: When buying a charger, confirm that it supports your phone's fast charging protocol. Third-party chargers usually indicate the protocols they support.
  • Check the Data Cable: High-power fast charging requires dedicated data cables. Regular data cables may only support up to 60W.
  • Check certification: Apple users are recommended to choose MFi-certified cables; Android users should choose brand original or well-known third-party cables.
  • Check Heat: It's normal for a phone to heat up during fast charging, but if it's too hot to touch, that's abnormal—it's recommended to stop charging or change the charger.

Fast Charging Security Tips

  • Avoid playing heavy games while charging, as it increases heat.
  • Do not use low-quality chargers and cables, as they may pose safety hazards.
  • Remove heavy phone cases while charging to help dissipate heat.
  • If the charger or phone becomes abnormally hot or emits an unusual smell, stop using it immediately.

IV. Real Battery-Saving Tips That Actually Work

  • Screen (Power Drain King): Lower brightness and shorten auto-lock time. OLED screen phones can save significant power with dark mode enabled.
  • Poor Signal: In elevators and basements, the phone works hard to search for signal, draining battery very fast.
  • 5G: If signal is poor, manually switch back to 4G; battery life will improve significantly.
  • Location Services: For non-navigation apps, set location permission to "Allow only while using".
  • Restrict Background Activity: iPhone: Settings > General > Background App Refresh; Android: App battery settings.

Hands-On: Give Your Battery a Checkup

2. BL Lock (Bootloader Lock): The First Gate to Flashing

Bootloader is the program that loads the operating system when the phone starts up. The BL lock is a security lock added by manufacturers to prevent users from flashing unofficial systems.

Function of BL Lock:
  • Security Protection: Prevent malware from being flashed into the system.
  • Anti-theft: Even if the phone is flashed, it can't be used without the original account password.
  • Warranty Limitations: Unlocking the bootloader usually voids the official warranty.
BL Lock Policies of Each Brand:
  • Xiaomi: Can be unlocked, requires Bound Account Waiting Period (7-30 days).
  • Huawei: Unlock codes stopped being provided after 2018; official unlocking is basically impossible.
  • OnePlus: Can be unlocked, relatively lenient.
  • OPPO/vivo: Some models can be unlocked but require an application.
  • Samsung: Can be unlocked, but will trigger Knox fuse, losing Samsung Pay and other features.
  • Apple: iOS devices don’t have a traditional BL lock concept, but they have similar security mechanisms.

III. Root Access: Android's "Super Administrator"

Root access is equivalent to Windows Administrator privileges—it is the highest level of access on the Android system. After gaining root access, you can modify system files, uninstall pre-installed apps, and use advanced apps that require root.

Benefits of Root:
  • Uninstall bloatware that can't normally be uninstalled.
  • Use advanced feature apps (e.g., Titanium Backup, Greenify).
  • Modify system interface and features.
  • Install third-party Recovery and custom ROMs.
Risks of Root:
  • Loss of Warranty: Most manufacturers do not warranty rooted phones.
  • Security Risks: Malicious apps may gain Root access, causing serious consequences.
  • Unstable System: Misoperation may cause system crashes.
  • Banking Apps May Not Work: Many banking and payment apps will refuse to run if Root is detected.
  • OTA Update Failure: May not properly receive official system Updates.

Serious Warning

Rooting carries risks and may brick your phone (rendering it unable to boot). If you're a beginner, it's recommended to first fully understand the relevant knowledge and ensure you have a complete backup. Read the tutorial carefully before proceeding and verify your phone model and system version.

4. Advice for Regular Users

  • Developer Mode: You can enable it and try features like animation acceleration, but don't randomly change other settings.
  • BL Lock: If you don't plan to flash the device, keeping it locked is more secure.
  • Root: Not recommended for regular users—the risks outweigh the benefits. If you truly need certain features, first explore whether there are alternatives that don't require root.

Tips：Magisk

Magisk is a modern root solution characterized by being "Systemless"—it doesn't modify the system partition but achieves root through mounting. This can hide the root status, allowing apps that detect root (such as banking apps) to function normally. However, using Magisk still requires unlocking the bootloader.

Chapter 24: Root Access and BL Lock Deep Dive

This chapter is an in-depth extension of Chapter 20, suitable for users who want to truly understand the underlying mechanisms of their phones or even try flashing custom ROMs. Please note that these operations carry certain risks—proceed with caution.

I. Deep Understanding of Bootloader

Bootloader is the first program that runs when a phone powers on; its job is to initialize hardware and load the operating system kernel. You can think of the bootloader as the phone's "gatekeeper."

BL Lock Working Principle:
  • Verify Signature: The bootloader lock verifies the digital signature of the boot image—only systems signed by the manufacturer can boot.
  • Prevent Downgrade: Prevents users from flashing older system versions to exploit known vulnerabilities.
  • Protect Data: Even if a new system is flashed, user data cannot be accessed without the original account password.

2. Detailed Process to Unlock BL Lock (Xiaomi as Example)

  1. Apply for Unlock Permission: Apply for unlocking on the Xiaomi unlock tool website.
  2. Bound Account: Bind your Xiaomi account in phone settings.
  3. Waiting Period: Depending on account level, you may need to wait 7-30 days.
  4. Unlock Operation: Use Xiaomi unlock tool connected to a computer to unlock.

3. Multiple Rooting Solutions

  • Magisk: A modern Root solution, systemless design that can hide Root status.
  • Kernelsu: Kernel-based Root solution, more low-level, better at hiding.
  • SuperSU: A classic Root solution, now discontinued.

IV. Introduction to Flashing

Flashing means installing a new operating system on your phone. Common types of flashing:

  • Flash Official ROM: Installs the official system for unbricking or downgrading.
  • Flash Third-Party Recovery: Such as TWRP, used for backup recovery and flashing third-party ROMs.
  • Flash Third-Party ROM: Such as LineageOS, Pixel Experience, etc., to experience different systems.

Important Warning

Flashing carries risks; proceed with caution. Possible consequences:

  • Phone bricked (cannot boot up)
  • Loss of Warranty
  • Data Loss
  • Payment Function Abnormal

Before flashing, be sure to do a complete backup, carefully read the tutorial, and verify that your phone model and version match.
