Part I: Fundamentals (Chapters 0–7)

Your phone is far more powerful than you imagine. This section lays the groundwork for all the "pro moves" to come — we will start by getting everyone on the same page, then gradually get to know and take control of your device. Whether you have just unboxed your first smartphone or you have been using one for years without ever digging beneath the surface, this part will help you rediscover the remarkable little box sitting in the palm of your hand.

Chapter 0: Hey, Before We Begin

Before we dive in, let us cover some ground rules so everything that follows makes sense. Do not rush — a dull axe will not cut firewood, as the saying goes.

I. What This Book Can Do for You (and What It Cannot)

It can help you systematically understand how to use your phone, grasp the reasoning behind each feature, and whether you are an Android devotee or an Apple fan, most of the thinking applies universally. It will guide you through hands-on practice, upgrading you from "knowing how to use" to "understanding how to use."

It is not a user manual for any specific phone model, nor is it a tech-news feed chasing the latest gadgets, and it is certainly not a guide to getting addicted to your phone. Frankly, if you are expecting "how to binge-watch videos more comfortably," you may be disappointed — we care far more about making your phone truly serve you, not the other way round.

II. Our "Secret Code" Guide

To keep things clear, the book uses a few visual markers — think of them as our shared shorthand:

  • [Button Name]: Represents a specific on-screen button or app name, such as [Settings], [OK].
  • >: Represents a sequence of actions. For example, "tap [Settings] > [Sound]" means tap Settings first, then tap Sound.
  • Bold: Very important! Could be a key concept or a safety warning — please pay close attention.
  • Italics: Supplementary thoughts or analogies to help you understand better.

III. Android and iOS — We Cover Both

Here is the thing: some features go by different names but do the same job. For instance: "application" (Android) vs. "App" (iOS), "Settings" (both call it that), "File Manager" (Android) vs. "Files" (iOS). As long as you know they are the same thing, do not get tripped up by the terminology.

Additionally, the Android camp now includes Huawei's HarmonyOS, which has its own distinctive features — we will cover that in a dedicated section later. In short, no matter what phone you use, this book can help.

IV. Phone Storage: Why Does a 128 GB Phone Show Less Than 110 GB Available?

Most people know the capacity units: 1 TB = 1024 GB, 1 GB = 1024 MB, 1 MB = 1024 KB… But here is the catch: hard drive manufacturers typically calculate in base-1000, while the operating system calculates in base-1024, so the "128 GB" drive you bought will appear to "shrink."

However, the main reasons your phone's storage shrinks are threefold:

  1. The operating system takes up space: The OS is like the load-bearing walls of a house — it has to be there. Modern systems easily consume 10–20 GB, which is perfectly normal.
  2. Pre-installed manufacturer apps: Those apps that come on a new phone also take up space. Some can be uninstalled; some cannot.
  3. Partitioning and formatting overhead: For technical reasons — think of it like the lining of a rucksack that also takes up room.

Tips for Buying a Phone

These days, 64 GB is basically not enough. 128 GB is a safe starting point. If you love taking photos and playing games, consider going straight for 256 GB or more. Remember: when it comes to storage, it is better to have too much than too little.

Hands-On: Check Your Phone's "ID Card"

Open [Settings], find "About Phone" (or a similar option), and note down: phone model, system version, total storage, and remaining storage. This is the first step to understanding your device. Whilst you are at it, check the battery health — just to know where you stand.

Chapter 1: Hello, My Personal Computer

Your phone is, in fact, the most personal supercomputer in your pocket. It is infinitely more powerful than the machines that put humans on the Moon, yet it rests quietly in the palm of your hand. Today, let us get to know this "silent giant" properly.

I. Phone Hardware "Innards": An In-Depth Look

Small as it is, a phone has everything inside. Let us take it apart like an anatomy lesson and get to know each precision component.

1. SoC (System on Chip): The Phone's "Brain + Heart"

SoC stands for System on Chip — literally "cramming the entire system onto a single chip." It is not just the CPU (Central Processing Unit); it integrates the CPU, GPU (Graphics Processing Unit), NPU (Neural Processing Unit), ISP (Image Signal Processor), baseband (responsible for communications), and more all onto one chip.

It is like building a city's government building, power plant, communications centre, and police station all in one tower — extremely efficient.

CPU: The Core of General-Purpose Computing

The CPU is the most central component of the SoC, responsible for handling the vast majority of computing tasks. Phone CPUs use the ARM architecture, which differs from the x86 architecture used in computers. ARM architecture is characterised by a Reduced Instruction Set Computing (RISC) design, offering lower power consumption and making it better suited for mobile devices.

CPU Big-Little Core Design: Modern phone CPUs universally adopt a "big-LITTLE" architecture (ARM's big.LITTLE technology), dividing cores into different performance tiers:

  • Prime/Core: The most powerful, handling heavy-duty tasks such as game loading, app launches, and complex computations. Highest clock speed (3 GHz+), but also the highest power consumption.
  • Performance cores: Balancing performance and power efficiency, handling everyday medium-load tasks. Scrolling through social media, watching videos, and multitasking primarily rely on these.
  • Efficiency cores: Lowest power consumption, handling lightweight background tasks. Standby, listening to music, receiving messages — barely sipping power.

The three tiers of cores work in concert: the system scheduler automatically allocates tasks based on load — lightweight tasks go to efficiency cores to save power, heavy work goes to big cores to power through, then switches back to efficiency cores when done. This is why your phone can both run games smoothly and last all day on standby.

Brand-Specific Architecture Characteristics:

  • Qualcomm Snapdragon 8 Gen 3: 1 prime core (Cortex-X4, 3.3 GHz) + 5 performance cores (Cortex-A720, up to 3.15 GHz) + 2 efficiency cores (Cortex-A520, 2.27 GHz). Characterised by an extremely strong single-core performance from the prime core.
  • MediaTek Dimensity 9300: 1 prime core (Cortex-X4) + 3 performance cores (Cortex-A720) + 4 efficiency cores (Cortex-A520). MediaTek markets this as an "all-big-core" architecture, meaning traditional small cores have been replaced with 3 A720 performance cores, delivering outstanding multi-core performance.
  • Apple A17 Pro: 2 performance cores (up to 3.78 GHz) + 4 efficiency cores. Fewer cores, but single-core performance far ahead, thanks to Apple's custom architecture and advanced 3 nm process.
GPU: The Graphics Rendering Engine

The GPU (Graphics Processing Unit) is dedicated to graphics and parallel computing tasks. It has far more cores than the CPU (often hundreds or thousands), but each core is relatively simple, making it ideal for processing large volumes of similar computations simultaneously.

Graphics Rendering Pipeline:

  1. Vertex processing: Computing the position coordinates of each vertex in a 3D model.
  2. Rasterisation: Converting 3D graphics into 2D pixel points on screen.
  3. Pixel shading: Computing colour, texture, and lighting effects for each pixel.
  4. Output compositing: Merging all layers into the final image and sending it to the display.

GPU's Impact on Daily Use: It is not just for gaming! Interface scrolling, animation effects, video playback, photo filters, and AR apps all depend on the GPU. A phone with a weak GPU may drop frames when scrolling the interface, and gaming will stutter outright.

Brand-Specific GPUs: Qualcomm uses its in-house Adreno GPU; MediaTek uses ARM Mali GPUs; Apple uses its custom GPU, which is the industry benchmark for both performance and efficiency.

NPU: The AI Acceleration Engine

The NPU (Neural Processing Unit) is a processor specifically designed for neural network computations. Compared to the CPU and GPU, the NPU is far more efficient and consumes less power when handling AI tasks.

Neural Network Computation Method: The NPU's core operations are matrix multiplication and convolution — precisely the most fundamental and frequent computations in neural networks. Through hardware-level optimisation, the NPU can process large volumes of matrix operations in parallel, dozens of times faster than the CPU, yet with far lower power consumption.

AI Processing Pipeline:

  1. Data input: Camera images, microphone audio, sensor data, etc.
  2. Pre-processing: Adjusting data format and dimensions to meet neural network input requirements.
  3. Inference computation: The NPU loads a trained model and performs forward computation on the input data, producing output.
  4. Post-processing: Converting AI output into usable results, such as face coordinates, text content, or scene labels.

Practical Application Scenarios:

  • Computational photography: Night mode compositing, portrait bokeh, HDR processing, AI beauty filters.
  • Voice assistants: Speech recognition, natural language understanding, speech synthesis.
  • Real-time translation: AI subtitles, conversation translation.
  • Smart recognition: Text recognition (OCR), object recognition, scene recognition.
  • On-device large models: The latest flagship chips already support running small AI large models on the phone itself, enabling offline AI conversations, document summarisation, and more.
ISP: Image Signal Processor

The ISP processes the raw data from the camera, turning light signals into beautiful photos.

How to judge performance? Look at the model! Apple A-series, Qualcomm Snapdragon 8-series, MediaTek Dimensity 9000-series, and Huawei Kirin 9000-series are all flagship-tier. Mid-range options include Snapdragon 7-series and Dimensity 8000-series. Do not just look at core count — architecture and process node (nanometre number) matter more.

2. RAM (Memory): The Temporary Workbench

RAM is like your desk — the bigger it is, the more files you can have open at the same time. 8 GB is the current mainstream, 12 GB suits heavy users, and 16 GB+ is for gamers and enthusiasts.

Memory type matters too: LPDDR5 is faster and more power-efficient than LPDDR4X. Apple may not have a lot of RAM, but its system optimisation is excellent, so it always seems "enough."

What happens when RAM runs low? Apps get killed in the background, and you will need to reload them when switching. That is why sometimes you switch away from a game to reply to a message, and the game is gone.

3. Storage (ROM/Flash Memory): The Long-Term Warehouse

Storage is like your bookshelf — it holds all your photos, videos, and apps. The current mainstream is UFS 3.1 or UFS 4.0, which is several times faster than the older eMMC standard.

Why does storage speed matter? It determines how quickly apps open, games load, and photos save. Over time, slow storage will make your phone increasingly sluggish.

4. Screen: Your Window to the World

The screen is your primary interface with the phone, and its quality directly affects the experience.

  • Panel type: OLED delivers vibrant colours, high contrast, and better power efficiency (pixels turn off when displaying black), but may cause eye strain from flicker at low brightness; LCD is gentler on the eyes and cheaper, but cannot match OLED's contrast and colour quality.
  • Resolution: 1080p is mainstream; 2K is sharper but more power-hungry. Apple's "Retina display" concept means: at a normal viewing distance, the human eye cannot distinguish individual pixels.
  • Refresh rate: 60 Hz is standard; 90 Hz/120 Hz is smoother — scrolling feels silky. High refresh rates consume more power, so many phones support adaptive refresh rates (LTPO), dynamically adjusting based on content.
  • Touch sampling rate: Affects touch response speed — gamers should pay attention; the higher, the more responsive.
  • Dimming method: DC dimming is gentler on the eyes than PWM dimming; it is less tiring at low brightness. Many modern phones support high-frequency PWM dimming, also for eye comfort.
Screen HDR Technology

HDR (High Dynamic Range) allows the screen to simultaneously display brighter highlights and deeper shadows, dramatically improving the sense of layering in the image.

  • How it works: A standard screen has a limited brightness range (SDR), with insufficient contrast between the brightest and darkest areas. HDR screens have higher peak brightness (typically 1000 nits and above), greater contrast, and a wider colour gamut, able to display more detail.
  • Mainstream standards: HDR10 (the most basic, free and open-source), HDR10+ (driven by Samsung, with dynamic metadata), Dolby Vision (the best image quality but requires licensing). Which standard a phone supports depends on the screen hardware and system licensing.
  • Practical effect: When watching HDR video, sunlight looks more dazzling, shadows are deeper, and colours are richer. The prerequisite is that the video itself was produced in HDR and the screen supports HDR display.
Image HDR Technology

HDR in photography and screen HDR are two different things. Image HDR uses algorithms to solve the problem of "overexposed highlights and pitch-black shadows."

  • Algorithm principle: When you press the shutter, the phone actually captures multiple photos at different exposures (underexposed to preserve highlight detail, overexposed to preserve shadow detail, and normally exposed to preserve mid-tones), then composites them into a single high dynamic range photo.
  • Processing pipeline: Multi-frame capture → alignment compensation (anti-shake) → exposure fusion / tone mapping → colour correction → output final photo.
  • Optimisation: Modern phones can now achieve "real-time HDR" — what you see in the viewfinder is already the HDR-processed result, truly WYSIWYG. Apple's Smart HDR and Android's auto-HDR are both very mature.
5. Battery & Charging: The Source of Power

The battery is the phone's most "short-lived" component — it degrades noticeably after two or three years. Current mainstream capacity is 4500–5500 mAh.

  • Charging wattage: The Android camp has pushed to 100 W or even 200 W, fully charging in just over ten minutes; Apple is still hovering around 20–30 W. Does fast charging damage the battery? Using original or certified accessories, the damage is minimal — the phone and charger have thermal protection.
  • Wireless charging: Convenient but less efficient and generates more heat. Suitable for casual top-ups at the office, not for when you are in a rush.
  • Reverse charging: Your phone becomes a power bank, charging earbuds or a watch — for emergencies.
6. Camera: The Magic of Light and Shadow

Modern phones often have three or four cameras, each with its own role:

  • Main camera: The most frequently used, with the largest sensor and best image quality.
  • Ultra-wide: For landscapes and architecture, with a wider field of view.
  • Telephoto: For distant subjects; available as optical zoom or digital zoom.
  • Macro: For extremely close-up shots of small objects.

Megapixels are not everything! Sensor size ("a bigger sensor beats everything"), aperture size (f/1.8 is larger than f/2.4), and optical image stabilisation (OIS is crucial) all affect image quality. Computational photography (AI algorithms) is increasingly important, compositing multiple shots into one great photo.

Periscope Telephoto Lens

Traditional telephoto lenses require a long optical path, which would make the phone very thick. A periscope telephoto uses a 90-degree prism to refract light, allowing the optical path to extend horizontally within the phone, thereby achieving 5x or even 10x optical zoom whilst keeping the phone slim.

  • Structural Principle: Light enters through the lens → Prism refracts 90° → Passes through the lens group along the phone’s width → Reaches the sensor. It’s essentially laying a vertical telescope on its side.
  • Optical Characteristics: High optical zoom multiples (5x–10x), image quality far exceeds digital zoom. However, the sensor size is typically smaller than the main camera, so low-light performance is average.
  • Imaging Advantages: Photographing the moon, distant buildings, and stage close-ups — beyond the reach of ordinary phones.
OIS Optical Image Stabilisation vs. EIS Electronic Image Stabilisation

Image stabilisation is a key technology affecting photo and video quality. The two methods work on completely different principles:

  • OIS (Optical Image Stabilisation): Physically moves the lens or sensor to counteract hand shake. The lens contains a micro-gyroscope that detects the direction and amplitude of shaking, then drives a motor to move the lens group in the opposite direction. The advantage is no loss of image quality; the disadvantage is higher cost and larger space requirements.
  • EIS (Electronic Image Stabilisation): Uses software algorithms to crop and shift the image to counteract shaking. The phone records a slightly larger frame area, then crops a stable region based on gyroscope data. The advantage is no additional hardware needed; the disadvantage is that it crops the frame (reducing the field of view) and may produce blur in low light.
  • Practical Choice:For photography, prioritise phones with OIS; for video, both OIS and EIS work best together. Many flagship phones now feature "Sensor-Shift" stabilisation, which is more effective than traditional lens-based image stabilisation.
Optical Zoom vs. Digital Zoom
  • Optical zoom: Physically moves the lens group to change the focal length, with no loss of image quality. Optical zoom on phones is typically only available at fixed multiples (e.g., 2x, 3x, 5x, 10x) — you cannot zoom continuously like with a dedicated camera.
  • Digital zoom: Enlarges the centre of the frame — essentially cropping + interpolation. The more you zoom, the worse the quality. 100x digital zoom looks cool, but the actual image quality is very blurry.
  • Hybrid zoom: Combines optical and digital, using multi-frame compositing algorithms to compensate for quality loss between optical zoom steps. Better than pure digital, but still not as good as optical.
Audio: Speakers and Dedicated Display Chips

Single Speaker vs. Dual Speakers:

  • Single speaker: Only one sound unit at the bottom, audio biased to one side, no stereo effect. Common in mid-range and low-end phones.
  • Dual speakers (stereo): One speaker each at the top and bottom, creating a stereo soundstage. Left and right channels are distinct when watching videos or listening to music — much more immersive. The earpiece doubling as the top speaker is a common design.
  • Sound quality difference: Dual speakers are not just "louder" — they provide spatial awareness and layering. Watching a film with a single speaker always feels like something is missing.

Gaming Phone Dedicated Display Chips:

Some gaming and performance phones feature a dedicated display processing chip (such as Redmi's "Wild Engine" dedicated display chip, iQOO's custom display chip). Its functions include:

  • Frame interpolation: Using algorithms to interpolate 30 fps game footage to 60 fps or 120 fps for smoother visuals. This process runs on the dedicated chip, not consuming the SoC's GPU resources.
  • Super resolution: Using AI algorithms to upscale low-resolution footage to high resolution for sharper image quality.
  • Power reduction: Because frame interpolation and super resolution are handled by the dedicated chip, the SoC's load decreases, reducing heat and power consumption for more stable gaming.
GPS Positioning: Single-Frequency vs. Dual-Frequency

Phone positioning primarily relies on GPS and other satellite navigation systems.

  • Single-frequency GPS: Only receives L1 band signals, with positioning accuracy of approximately 3–5 metres. Adequate in open areas, but in dense urban canyons with tall buildings, signal reflection can cause significant positioning drift.
  • Dual-frequency GPS: Receives both L1 and L5 band signals simultaneously. The L5 band has a longer wavelength and stronger interference resistance; dual-frequency joint calculation can eliminate most ionospheric errors, achieving positioning accuracy of 1–3 metres. The advantage is clear when navigating in cities or using ride-hailing apps.
  • Multi-constellation support: Modern phones support not only GPS but also BeiDou (China), GLONASS (Russia), Galileo (Europe), and other satellite systems. The more satellites connected simultaneously, the faster and more accurate the positioning.
7. Sensors: The Phone's Little Antennae

Your phone hides over a dozen sensors, working silently:

  • Accelerometer + Gyroscope: Sense the phone's orientation — gaming steering, auto-rotate screen.
  • Ambient light sensor: Automatically adjusts screen brightness.
  • Proximity sensor: Turns off the screen when the phone is held to your ear during a call.
  • Fingerprint sensor: Current mainstream is under-display optical or ultrasonic fingerprint.
  • Facial recognition: Apple's Face ID uses 3D structured light with a high security level; many Android phones use 2D photo recognition with average security.
  • NFC: Near-field communication — tap for transit cards, access cards, bank cards.
  • Barometer: Measures altitude — can even detect when you climb stairs.
  • Hall sensor: Detects the opening and closing of magnetic flip cases.

II. Operating Systems: Android vs. iOS vs. HarmonyOS

Hardware is the body; the operating system is the soul. Each of the three major systems has its own character:

  • Android: Like an open-source community — phone manufacturers can deeply customise it, giving you plenty of choices. You can change themes, install third-party app stores, and tinker with all sorts of tricks. But because it is open, fragmentation is severe, and the experience varies greatly between brands.
  • iOS: Like a meticulously tended garden — Apple controls both hardware and software, delivering a unified and smooth experience. App quality is high, privacy protection is strong, but freedom is limited — many things must be done Apple's way.
  • HarmonyOS: Huawei's new species, featuring distributed capabilities that enable seamless collaboration between different devices. We will cover it in a dedicated section later.

All three ecosystems are incredibly rich — just choose the lifestyle you prefer. There is no absolute good or bad, only what suits you.

III. Phone Ports Explained

Phone ports are the bridge connecting to the outside world, and different ports vary significantly:

Micro USB
  • Physical characteristics: Flat trapezoidal connector, with a specific orientation (inserting it the wrong way round will not fit). Largely phased out on phones, but still found on some low-end and older devices.
  • Transfer limitations: Only supports USB 2.0 standard, with a maximum transfer rate of 480 Mbps and charging power typically not exceeding 10 W.
  • Drawbacks: Non-reversible, short insertion cycle life, slow transfer speeds, low charging power.
Lightning (Apple's Connector)
  • Physical characteristics: Apple's proprietary 8-pin connector, reversible. Used on iPhone 14 and earlier models.
  • Transfer limitations: The Lightning port physically only supports USB 2.0 standard, with a maximum transfer rate of 480 Mbps. Even using a USB-C to Lightning cable, transfer speed is limited by the Lightning port itself. The iPhone 15 series has switched to Type-C.
  • Note: Apple MFi-certified cables contain an authentication chip; non-certified cables may not charge or transfer data properly.
USB Type-C
  • Physical characteristics: 24-pin oval connector, reversible. Has become the standard port for phones and most electronic devices.
  • Transfer protocols: Type-C is merely the connector shape; actual transfer capability depends on the internal protocol. Supports USB 2.0 (480 Mbps), USB 3.0/3.1/3.2 (5–20 Gbps), and even USB4 (40 Gbps).
  • Important distinction: Not all Type-C ports support USB 3.0! Many phones use a Type-C connector but only have USB 2.0 pins internally connected, making transfer speeds just as slow as the old port. To enjoy USB 3.0 speeds, you need both phone support and a dedicated USB 3.0 cable.
  • Charging capability: Supports USB PD and other fast charging protocols, up to 240 W charging.
  • Video output: Supports DisplayPort Alt Mode, allowing video output to a monitor via an adapter cable.
3.5 mm Audio Jack
  • Physical characteristics: Round 3-pole or 4-pole socket; 3-pole carries left and right audio channels, 4-pole adds a microphone channel.
  • Features: Analogue signal transmission, plug-and-play, no drivers or conversion needed. Stable audio quality, zero latency.
  • Current status: Most flagship phones have removed the 3.5 mm jack, requiring a Type-C adapter or Bluetooth headphones. Some mid-range and gaming phones still retain it.

5. Baseband & Antenna: The Unsung Heroes of Phone Communication

Your phone can make calls and access the internet thanks to the partnership of the baseband chip and antenna.

Baseband Chip:

The baseband is the processor responsible for wireless communication in the phone. It modulates digital signals into radio waves for transmission and demodulates received radio waves back into digital signals. Baseband performance directly affects call quality, internet speed, and signal stability. Qualcomm is the largest mobile baseband supplier; MediaTek and Samsung also have their own basebands. Apple began using its own C1 baseband chip starting from the iPhone 16e, marking Apple’s official entry into the self-developed baseband era; previously iPhone 12 through iPhone 16 series all use Qualcomm basebands (X55/X60/X65/X66/X71, etc.). Earlier iPhone XS/11 series used Intel basebands with poor signal performance — a pain point for many users.

Antenna Design:

Phone antennas are hidden inside the body, typically arranged around the frame. Antenna design directly affects signal reception performance:

  • MIMO Technology: Multiple-input multiple-output, using multiple antennas to send and receive data simultaneously, improving speed and stability. 4×4 MIMO is faster than 2×2 MIMO.
  • Number of Antennas: Flagship phones typically have over a dozen antennas (cellular, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, NFC, and GPS each require independent antennas).
  • Impact of Metal Bodies: Metal shields signals, so phones with metal bodies need antenna bands (the plastic strips on the frame). Glass and plastic bodies have less impact on signal.
Network Technology Evolution:
  • 4G LTE: Theoretical peak rates of 1Gbps downlink / 500Mbps uplink (Cat.18), actual experience typically 50–150Mbps. Widest coverage, sufficient for most needs.
  • 5G NR: Theoretical peak downlink rate of 10Gbps+. Sub-6GHz bands have better coverage; mmWave (millimetre wave) is extremely fast but with very limited coverage.
  • 5G-A（5G Advanced）: An enhanced version of 5G, also called 5.5G. Downlink speeds can reach 10Gbps, uplink 1Gbps, with significant improvements in connection density and positioning accuracy. Some flagship phones began supporting it from 2024.
Network Band Classification:

The network bands a phone supports determine which countries and carriers’ networks it can be used on:

  • FDD-LTE Bands (B Series): E.g., B1, B3, B5, etc. — 4G bands. Different countries use different bands; China primarily uses B1/B3/B5/B8/B34/B38/B39/B40/B41.
  • TDD-LTE Band: Another 4G standard, heavily used in China on B38/B39/B40/B41.
  • 5G NR Bands (N Series): E.g., n1, n3, n28, n41, n78, n79, etc. n78 (3.5GHz) is China’s primary 5G band; n41 (2.5GHz) is more commonly used by mobile operators.
  • Buying Advice: If you need to use the phone abroad, confirm before purchase that it supports the target country’s bands. Domestic (Chinese) models typically have the most complete support for Chinese bands but may lack some international bands.

Chapter 3: Connect Your Phone to Its "Lifeline"— Networking

Without the internet, a smartphone is only half as smart. Networking is the lifeline of the modern phone — understanding it, using it well, and protecting it is aRequired course for every phone user.

I. Mobile Networks (4G/5G) & Data Allowance

Mobile networks are the foundation for connecting anytime, anywhere. From 2G to 5G, speeds have increased over a thousandfold, but the underlying principle is largely the same.

Network Standards:
  • 4G LTE: Currently the widest coverage, sufficient speed (theoretically up to 100 Mbps+), relatively low power consumption.
  • 5G: Very fast (theoretically up to 10 Gbps), but coverage is still being rolled out and power consumption is high. Ideal for downloading large files and watching high-definition video.
  • 5G SA vs. NSA: SA is Standalone Access; NSA relies on 4G base stations. SA provides a better experience, but requires both carrier and phone support.
Data Management:
  • What is data?: The amount of data generated when your phone accesses the internet. Watching videos and browsing images consumes the most data; text and voice use relatively little.
  • Data-saving tips: Enable "Data Saver" mode in settings; update apps or watch HD video only on Wi-Fi.
  • Signal bars ≠ speed: Strong signal only means a good connection; actual speed also depends on how busy the base station is and how far you are from it.

Important Warning: Turn Off [Data Roaming] Before Going Abroad!

Using mobile data abroad can result in astronomical bills! Before travelling, make sure to turn off data roaming in settings, or purchase a local SIM card / international roaming package in advance.

II. Wi-Fi: Connecting, Sharing & Security

Wi-Fi is the primary way to connect at home and in the office — fast and does not use your mobile data. But Wi-Fi is also constantly evolving, and the differences between standards are significant.

Connecting to Wi-Fi:

Go to [Settings] > [WLAN] and select a network to enter the password. Many phones now support QR code scanning for connection, which is even more convenient.

Sharing Passwords:

Both Android and iOS let connected phones quickly share passwords with friends. Android generates a QR code; iOS can share automatically when devices are brought close together.

Wi-Fi Standard Evolution:

Wi-Fi standards are set by the IEEE, with each generation offering significant improvements:

Wi-Fi 4 802.11n 2.4/5GHz 600Mbps Old standard, coverage still acceptable
Wi-Fi 5 802.11ac 5GHz 3.5Gbps Most widely deployed, sufficient speed

  • Wi-Fi 6 key technologies: OFDMA (Orthogonal Frequency-Division Multiple Access) allows multiple devices to transmit simultaneously; MU-MIMO (Multi-User MIMO) improves multi-device parallel efficiency; TWT (Target Wake Time) makes devices more power-efficient.
  • Wi-Fi 6E: Adds the 6GHz band on top of Wi-Fi 6, providing more channels and reducing interference. Requires both phone and router support.
  • Wi-Fi 7: Introduces MLO (Multi-Link Operation), which can use multiple bands simultaneously for data transmission, dramatically reducing latency. 320MHz ultra-wide channels enable exponential speed improvements. Flagship phones have progressively supported this since 2024.
  • 2.4GHz vs 5GHz: 2.4GHz penetrates walls well but is slow with more interference; 5GHz is fast but penetrates walls poorly. Wi-Fi 6E/7's 6GHz band is fastest but penetrates walls the least, best suited for use in the same room.
Public Wi-Fi Risks:

Public Wi-Fi may expose you to hackers eavesdropping on your data or setting up phishing hotspots. Rules to follow:

  • Avoid logging into bank accounts or entering passwords.
  • Use a VPN for encrypted connections whenever possible.
  • Do not auto-connect to unfamiliar Wi-Fi networks.
  • Use HTTPS websites (look for the padlock icon in the address bar).

III. Bluetooth: The Versatile Short-Range Wireless Communicator

Bluetooth is probably one of the most frequently used wireless technologies on your phone — wireless earbuds, smart watches, car systems, keyboards, and mice all rely on it.

Technical Principle:

Bluetooth operates in the 2.4GHz ISM band, using Frequency Hopping Spread Spectrum (FHSS) technology to rapidly switch data transmission across 79 channels, effectively avoiding interference. Bluetooth communication distance is divided into three classes: Class 1 approximately 100 metres, Class 2 approximately 10 metres (commonly used in phones), Class 3 approximately 1 metre.

Version Evolution:
  • Bluetooth 4.0: Introduced Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE), enabling small-battery devices like fitness bands and heart rate monitors to go wireless, extending battery life from days to months.
  • Bluetooth 4.2: Improved transfer speed and privacy protection, supports IPv6 direct connection.
  • Bluetooth 5.0: Double the transfer speed (2 Mbps), 4× the range (theoretically 300 metres), 8× the broadcast capacity. Supports dual audio (connecting two pairs of headphones simultaneously).
  • Bluetooth 5.1: Introduced direction-finding capability (AoA/AoD), with positioning accuracy down to the centimetre level.
  • Bluetooth5.2: The new LE Audio standard, supporting LC3 encoding (better sound quality, lower power consumption), multi-stream audio, and broadcast audio.
  • Bluetooth 5.3/5.4: Further optimised power consumption and connection stability, enhanced periodic broadcasting.
Application Scenarios:
  • Audio transmission: Wireless earbuds, car audio. Bluetooth 5.2's LE Audio brings a qualitative leap in both sound quality and latency.
  • Data transfer: Close-range file and photo sharing. Slower than Wi-Fi Direct, but simpler.
  • Device pairing: Smart watches, fitness bands, keyboards, mice, game controllers.
  • Location services: Bluetooth beacons for indoor navigation, item tracking (e.g., Apple AirTag).
  • Smart home: Bluetooth Mesh network for controlling light bulbs, door locks, etc.

IV. Infrared Remote: Your Phone Becomes a Universal Remote

Infrared remote control is one of the oldest wireless control technologies, but it remains practical on phones. Some Android phones (such as certain Xiaomi and Huawei models) have a built-in infrared emitter that can control TVs, air conditioners, set-top boxes, and other appliances.

How It Works:

Infrared remote control emits infrared light pulse signals at specific frequencies (typically 38kHz) through infrared light-emitting diodes, and home appliance infrared receivers decode these pulses to execute corresponding operations. Different brands use different encoding protocols, commonly NEC, RC5, RC6, SONY, and others. Infrared apps on phones have built-in encoding libraries for various brands — just select the brand and model to match.

Limitations:
  • Directionality: Infrared travels in a straight line — you need to point it at the device; it cannot go through walls.
  • Short range: Effective distance is generally 5–8 metres.
  • Apple does not support it: The iPhone has no infrared emitter and cannot use this feature.

V. NFC: The Magic of a Tap

NFC (Near Field Communication) is an ultra-short-range (within 10 centimetres) high-frequency wireless communication technology, operating at 13.56MHz with a maximum transmission rate of 424Kbps.

Communication Principles & Modes:

NFC evolved from RFID technology and supports three working modes:

  • Read/Write mode: The phone reads information from NFC tags, such as triggering actions by reading an NFC sticker.
  • Peer-to-Peer mode: Two NFC devices transfer data to each other, such as Android Beam (discontinued).
  • Card Emulation mode: The phone simulates a smart card — this is the most commonly used mode. Transit cards, access cards, and bank cards all use this mode.
Security Mechanisms:
  • Ultra-short range: The 10 cm communication distance is itself a physical security guarantee — remote eavesdropping is virtually impossible.
  • Secure chip: The phone has a built-in independent Secure Element (SE) or uses HCE (Host Card Emulation); payment keys are stored in a secure area and cannot be extracted even if the phone is rooted.
  • Fingerprint/Face verification: Payments require biometric confirmation — even if the phone is lost, it cannot be used for fraudulent transactions.
  • Tokenisation: Apple Pay, Huawei Pay, and similar services do not transmit the actual card number but use one-time tokens that cannot be reused even if intercepted.
Typical Applications:
  • Transit cards / Access cards: Write physical card information to your phone and just tap to use.
  • Mobile payments: Apple Pay, Huawei Pay, etc. — bind your bank card to your phone and tap to pay.
  • Device pairing: NFC tap for quick Bluetooth headphone or speaker pairing.
  • Identity verification: NFC ID card reading, digital driving licence verification.

Five、Software Package Components

Have you ever wondered what's actually inside that installation package you downloaded from the app store? Understanding the structure of installation packages can help you better understand how apps work, and know where to look when troubleshooting issues.

1. Android APK File Structure

APK (Android Package Kit) is essentially a ZIP compressed package. After extracting, you can see the following core directories and files:

  • AndroidManifest.xml: The app's "ID card" and "instruction manual". Records the app's package name, version number, required permissions, supported system versions, entry Activity, and other core information. The system reads this file first when installing an app.
  • classes.dex: The app's core code. Java/Kotlin source code compiles into Dalvik bytecode, stored in this file. An app may have multiple dex files (such as classes2.dex, classes3.dex).
  • resources.arsc: Compiled resource index table. Records all resource IDs and their corresponding relationships.
  • res/Directory: Resources folder. Contains images, layout files, strings, colour definitions, etc. Images of different resolutions are placed in different drawable folders (such as drawable-hdpi, drawable-xhdpi).
  • assets/Directory: Raw resources folder. Stores uncompiled resources such as web page files, fonts, audio, etc. Apps can read these through AssetManager at runtime.
  • lib/Directory: Native library folder. Stores compiled .so files (C/C++ code compilation products), classified by CPU architecture (such as arm64-v8a, armeabi-v7a). Games and apps requiring high-performance computing typically have this directory.
  • META-INF/Directory: Signature information folder. Contains MANIFEST.MF, CERT.SF, CERT.RSA three files used to verify APK integrity and signatures.
2. iOS IPA File Structure

IPA (iOS App Store Package) is also a compressed package, with a slightly different structure:

  • Payload/Directory: Where the core content resides. Contains an .app folder with all the app's content.
  • Info.plist: The app's configuration file, similar to Android's AndroidManifest.xml. Records the app ID, version, permission requirements, supported devices, etc.
  • Executable file: The main programme with the same name as the .app folder, a compiled Mach-O format binary file.
  • Frameworks/ directory: Dynamic libraries and frameworks the app depends on.
  • Resource file: Resources such as images, audio, and video, typically stored within the .app folder.
  • _CodeSignature/Directory: Code signature information used to verify the app's integrity and origin.
3. Signature Verification Mechanism

Why are apps downloaded from official stores safer? Signature verification is the key.

  • AndroidSignature: Developers sign APKs with a private key; when the system installs, it verifies with the public key. If an APK is tampered with (such asPlant virus), the signature becomes invalid and the system refuses to install. Google Play also requires App Signing, with Google holding the signing keys, further protecting app security.

1. Resolution, Frame Rate & Bitrate: The Three Pillars of Video Quality

Resolution:

Resolution determines image clarity, expressed in pixel count.

  • 720P (HD):1280×720Pixel，Entry-level clarity。
  • 1080P (Full HD):1920×1080Pixel，Currently most mainstream resolution。
  • 2K (QHD):2560×1440Pixel，Than1080PClearer。
  • 4K (UHD):3840×2160Pixel，Ultra HD，Rich details。
  • 8K:7680×4320Pixel，FutureStandard，Currently limited device support。

The higher the resolution, the clearer the image — but the file size also increases, demanding more storage and processing power.

Frame Rate:

Frame rate determines how smooth the motion appears, measured in frames per second (fps).

  • 24fps:MovieStandardFrame rate，Has unique"Cinematic"。
  • 30fps:TV and internetVideoCommon，Smooth and natural。
  • 60fps:High frame rate，Suitable for sports scenes and game recording，Very smooth。
  • 120fps/240fps:Ultra high frame rate，Can be used for slow motionVideo。

The higher the frame rate, the smoother the motion and the better the slow-motion effect — but file sizes also increase.

Bitrate:

Bitrate determines the data density of a video, measured in megabits per second (Mbps).

  • Higher bitrate means better image quality with more detail preserved, but larger file sizes.
  • A bitrate that is too low will cause visible compression artefacts such as pixelation and colour banding.
  • Professional video shooting typically requires high bitrates to preserve more room for post-processing.
